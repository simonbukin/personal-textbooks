# Chapter 1: Mental Models & Honest Foundations

## Why This Matters

You're going to be tempted to skip this chapter. You want to call an API, build something, watch tokens stream across your terminal. That impulse is fine — but acting on it right now will cost you weeks later. Most early frustration with AI engineering (the hallucinations you can't fix, the agent loops that never converge, the demo that works perfectly until a real user touches it) traces back to conceptual errors formed in the first week. Bad mental models produce bad architecture, and bad architecture is expensive to unlearn.

This chapter is intentionally slow. There's no code to run, no API keys to provision. The goal is calibration: building an accurate internal model of what these systems actually do, what they don't do, and where the boundaries between those two regions are blurry. If you get this right, every subsequent chapter will click faster. If you skip it, you'll keep bumping into the same walls and blaming the technology.

Think of this as the chapter where you earn the right to have opinions. By the end, when someone says "GPT-5 will be AGI" or "LLMs are just autocomplete," you'll be able to articulate exactly why both statements are wrong, and what a more honest framing looks like. You'll also have a vocabulary for discussing the technology's limits that doesn't collapse into either breathless optimism or reflexive dismissal, both of which are professionally dangerous in an industry where calibrated judgment is the scarcest resource.

## What LLMs Actually Are

An LLM — a **large language model** — is, at its mechanical core, a function that takes a sequence of tokens and produces a probability distribution over what the next token should be. That's it. The conversations, the code generation, the apparent reasoning: all of it emerges from this single operation applied at enormous scale. The word "token" here means a subword unit, typically 3-4 characters of English text, drawn from a fixed vocabulary. The model doesn't see words or sentences; it sees token IDs, and it predicts the next one.

**Next-token prediction** sounds trivially simple, and that's part of why LLMs are so often underestimated. But consider what accurate next-token prediction actually requires. To predict the next token in a passage about quantum electrodynamics, you need to have internalized something about physics. To predict the next token in a Python function, you need something resembling an understanding of programming semantics. To predict the next token in a dialogue, you need a model of how humans converse. The training objective is simple; the capability surface it produces is not.

Building an LLM involves three distinct stages, and conflating them leads to confusion. **Pretraining** is the first and most expensive stage. The model ingests a massive corpus — trillions of tokens of text from the internet, books, code repositories, and other sources — and learns to predict the next token. No human is labeling examples; this is unsupervised in the classical sense. What emerges is a model with broad knowledge and fluent language generation, but no particular inclination to be helpful, honest, or safe. A pretrained model will happily complete a passage about how to synthesize dangerous chemicals if that's what the token probabilities suggest comes next.

**Instruction tuning** (sometimes called supervised fine-tuning or SFT) is the second stage. Here, the model is trained on curated examples of instruction-response pairs — "given this question, here's a good answer." This transforms the model from a text-completion engine into something that follows directions. The shift is stark: the same underlying weights now produce qualitatively different behavior. Instead of continuing a passage, the model answers questions, follows formatting instructions, and generally behaves like an assistant. But instruction tuning alone doesn't solve alignment — the model might follow instructions to produce harmful content, or it might be helpful but not particularly calibrated about what it knows versus what it's making up.

**Reinforcement learning from human feedback** (RLHF) — or its variants like DPO (Direct Preference Optimization) and RLAIF (RL from AI Feedback) — is the third stage. Human raters compare pairs of model outputs and indicate which is better. This preference data trains a reward model, which is then used to fine-tune the LLM via reinforcement learning. RLHF is what makes models refuse certain requests, hedge when uncertain (sometimes too much), and generally conform to the behavior their developers want. It's also what makes models annoyingly sycophantic when miscalibrated, agreeing with users even when the user is wrong because raters tended to prefer agreeable responses.

Each stage changes the model's behavior in ways that matter for engineering decisions. A pretrained base model is powerful but unsteered. An instruction-tuned model follows directions but may lack safety guardrails. An RLHF-trained model is safer and more useful for most applications but has its own failure modes: it can be overly cautious, it can exhibit reward hacking, and its refusals can be inconsistent. When you're choosing a model for a production system, you need to know which stages it's been through and how each stage was calibrated.

Now, the part that keeps researchers honest: **emergent capabilities**. As models scale (more parameters, more training data, more compute), they don't just get incrementally better at the same tasks. They appear to acquire entirely new capabilities at certain scale thresholds. A model with 10 billion parameters can't do multi-step arithmetic; a model with 100 billion parameters can. This phenomenon was documented by Wei et al. (2022), who cataloged dozens of tasks that showed sharp performance transitions at specific scales.

But "emergence" is contested, and you should understand why. Schaeffer et al. (2023) argued that many apparent emergent capabilities are artifacts of the metrics used to measure them. When you use a discontinuous metric — like "exact match," where you score 0 for any wrong answer and 1 for a correct one — smooth underlying improvements in capability can look like sudden jumps. Switch to a continuous metric and the "emergence" often becomes a gradual curve. This doesn't mean scaling doesn't produce qualitatively new behaviors; it means we should be careful about claiming sharp phase transitions without checking whether the measurement itself is creating the appearance of one. The honest position is that scaling produces large capability improvements, some of which surprise researchers, but the mechanistic story of why and when is still being worked out.

**Scaling laws**, formalized by Kaplan et al. (2020) at OpenAI and refined by Hoffmann et al. (2022) with Chinchilla, describe predictable relationships between model size, dataset size, compute budget, and loss. They're remarkably consistent: double the compute, and you get a predictable reduction in loss. These laws have guided billions of dollars in training decisions. But they predict loss (how well the model predicts the next token on a test set), not task performance. The relationship between loss and actual usefulness (will the model write better code? will it hallucinate less?) is empirically strong but not theoretically guaranteed, and it's where scaling laws start to wobble. There's also active debate about whether scaling laws will continue to hold as models approach the limits of available training data and as returns on scale potentially diminish. The Chinchilla result, that most models were undertrained relative to their size, reshaped the field, but the next frontier of scaling is less clear.

## What LLMs Are NOT

Getting your positive mental model right is only half the job. You also need to kill the wrong models, because they're the ones that produce bad engineering decisions. Each misconception below will, if left uncorrected, lead you to build systems that fail in production.

**LLMs are not databases.** When a model tells you that the population of France is 67 million, it isn't looking that number up. It's generating tokens that are statistically likely given the input and its training data. The number might be right — it probably is, for well-known facts — but the mechanism is generation, not retrieval. This distinction matters because it means the model can generate plausible-sounding facts that are completely fabricated. It will do this with the same confidence and fluency it uses for accurate statements. There is no internal flag that distinguishes "I know this" from "this sounds right." The term for this is **hallucination**, though even that word is contested, since "hallucination" implies a deviation from a normal state of veridical perception, and LLMs don't have a normal state of perceiving truth. They generate. Sometimes what they generate is true. Sometimes it isn't. The model doesn't know the difference. When you need factual accuracy, you need retrieval systems (RAG, tool use, database lookups) feeding ground truth into the model's context. You cannot rely on the model's parametric knowledge alone.

**LLMs are not classical reasoners.** They can simulate reasoning, sometimes extraordinarily well, but the underlying mechanism is still pattern completion, not formal logic. A classical reasoner applies rules to symbols and produces guaranteed-correct conclusions given valid premises. An LLM produces text that looks like reasoning, and the conclusions are often correct, especially for problems similar to ones in the training data. But the failures are different from a reasoner's failures. A classical system fails when given invalid premises or insufficient information; an LLM can fail on valid, well-specified problems by taking reasoning paths that look locally coherent but are globally wrong. Modern reasoning models (like o1, o3, and Claude's extended thinking) mitigate this by generating explicit chains of thought that the model can condition on. This improves the quality of simulated reasoning; it doesn't transform the model into a formal reasoning engine. If you need guaranteed logical correctness, you still need formal verification tools.

**LLMs are not consistent.** Ask the same question twice with the same prompt, and you may get different answers. This is by design. The model samples from a probability distribution, and unless you set the temperature to zero (and even then, implementation details like floating-point non-determinism and batching strategies can introduce variation), the output is stochastic. Even with temperature zero, logically equivalent but syntactically different prompts can produce different answers. Rephrase "What is the capital of France?" as "Name the capital city of France" and you might get different surrounding text, different confidence levels, different amounts of elaboration. You cannot treat an LLM call the way you treat a function call in traditional software. `f(x)` always returns the same value; `llm(prompt)` does not. Any system that assumes consistency (caching based on exact prompt matches, expecting identical outputs for identical inputs across API versions, building downstream logic that depends on the model's exact phrasing) will eventually break. The engineering response: extract structured outputs, validate them, and build your system's logic around the validated structure, not the raw text.

**LLMs are not reliable in the way software is reliable.** Traditional software gives you a contract: given these inputs, you will get these outputs, within these error bounds, with these failure modes. LLMs give you a much weaker contract: given these inputs, you will get outputs that are usually pretty good, except when they're not, and you can't always predict when that will be. This is a characteristic of the technology, not a flaw to be fixed. The engineering challenge is building systems around the model that are reliable despite the model's probabilistic nature. That's what the rest of this book is about.

One more misconception worth naming explicitly: **LLMs are not static.** The model you're building against today may behave differently tomorrow. API providers update their models, sometimes with notice and sometimes without. A prompt that works perfectly with one model version might degrade with the next. OpenAI, Anthropic, and others have been bitten by this, and it's why version-pinning, regression testing, and eval infrastructure are essential components of any production system built on LLMs. If you treat the model as a stable dependency the way you treat a well-versioned library, you'll be caught off guard when it changes under you.

## The Capability/Reliability Gap

The single most important concept in AI engineering is also the one most often ignored by people coming from traditional software development. Every LLM has a gap between what it can do in a demo and what it can do reliably in production. This **capability/reliability gap** defines the field.

Demos are seductive. You can sit down with Claude or GPT-4 and, in thirty minutes, build something that looks like it took a team months to develop. A chatbot that answers customer questions. A code generator that writes working functions from natural language specs. An agent that browses the web, finds information, and synthesizes a report. These demos are real — the model really is doing those things. The problem is what happens next.

The **90/10 problem** captures it precisely: getting an LLM-based system to work 90% of the time is often surprisingly easy. Getting it to work 99% of the time typically costs more engineering effort than the first 90% combined. For many production applications, 99% isn't good enough; you need 99.9% or higher. Each additional nine requires fundamentally different strategies. The first 90% is prompt engineering. Getting to 95% might require few-shot examples and output validation. Getting to 99% might require fine-tuning, sophisticated guardrails, and human-in-the-loop fallbacks. Getting to 99.9% might require rearchitecting the system entirely — breaking the problem into smaller pieces where each piece can be validated independently.

Every architectural decision you'll make is shaped by this gap. Should you build an autonomous agent or a human-in-the-loop system? It depends on where your required reliability level falls relative to the model's capability/reliability gap for that specific task. Should you use a single powerful model or chain several smaller, specialized models together? The answer depends on whether decomposition improves reliability enough to justify the added complexity. Should you invest in better prompts or in better eval infrastructure? Almost certainly eval infrastructure, because you can't improve what you can't measure, and the capability/reliability gap means your intuition about what works is unreliable.

This gap also explains why so many AI startups fail. They demo brilliantly. Investors are impressed. Then the team spends eighteen months trying to close the last 10% of reliability and runs out of money or patience. The companies that succeed choose problems where the required reliability level falls within the model's current capabilities, or they build the engineering infrastructure to close the gap systematically. This book is designed to make you effective at the latter.

A subtler version of the gap catches experienced engineers: the gap between what works in your test suite and what works in the wild. You can build an eval set of 500 examples, get 98% accuracy, ship confidently — and then discover that your eval set didn't cover the distribution of real user inputs. Users are creative, adversarial, confused, and impatient in ways that eval sets rarely capture. The gap isn't just about the model; it's about the interaction between the model and the messy, unpredictable world of actual use. Building robust evals, which we'll cover in depth later, is how you shrink the gap between your test-time confidence and your production-time reality.

## How to Read Benchmarks Honestly

You will encounter benchmark numbers constantly: in model announcements, in research papers, in marketing materials, in Twitter arguments. Your ability to interpret these numbers correctly is a core professional skill. Most people in the AI space read benchmarks badly, and their confidence scales inversely with their understanding.

**MMLU** (Massive Multitask Language Understanding) tests knowledge across 57 academic subjects, from abstract algebra to virology. It was designed to measure how well models have absorbed factual knowledge from training data. When a model scores 90% on MMLU, it means the model can answer 90% of multiple-choice academic questions correctly. What it doesn't tell you: whether the model can apply that knowledge in context, whether it can synthesize across domains, or whether it'll hallucinate when the answer isn't in a multiple-choice format. MMLU has largely saturated (top models routinely score above 90%), and its discriminative power between frontier models is now minimal. MMLU-Pro, a harder version with 10-choice questions, is replacing it but will likely saturate within a year or two as well.

**HumanEval** measures code generation: given a function signature and docstring, can the model produce correct code? The original benchmark had 164 problems, roughly at the level of easy-to-medium coding interview questions. Top models now score above 90%, and the benchmark is widely considered saturated. HumanEval+ and other variants address some issues (like weak test cases in the original), but the limitation remains the same: interview-style problems don't reflect real-world software engineering, which involves navigating large codebases, understanding ambiguous requirements, and making design tradeoffs.

**SWE-bench** is more realistic. It presents models with actual GitHub issues from real open-source projects and asks them to produce patches that pass the project's test suite. SWE-bench Verified — a curated subset with human-validated tasks — has top scores around 74.4% as of early 2026. This is impressive, but read the number carefully. It means the best systems, with scaffolding and tool use, can resolve about three-quarters of curated issues. The remaining quarter includes tasks that require deep codebase understanding, multi-file coordinated changes, or reasoning that current models struggle with. "Curated" matters too: the full SWE-bench distribution includes harder tasks where performance drops considerably.

**GAIA** tests general AI assistants on real-world tasks that require multi-step reasoning, web browsing, and tool use. Top scores are near 90% as of early 2026, approaching saturation. GAIA was designed to be hard for AI and easy for humans, but the best agent systems have closed that gap faster than expected. This is a pattern: benchmarks that seem like they'll last years often saturate in months as teams specifically optimize for them.

A structural problem with benchmarks: **Goodhart's Law**. When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. Model developers optimize for benchmarks, sometimes explicitly (training on benchmark-similar data) and sometimes implicitly (making architectural choices that favor benchmark performance over general capability). Benchmarks still track real capability improvements, but you should never read a single benchmark number as a comprehensive quality assessment.

**Vibes vs. measurements.** Experienced practitioners use both. Benchmark numbers give you a coarse ranking and track progress over time. Your subjective experience using a model — what practitioners call "vibes" — captures dimensions that benchmarks miss: latency feel, consistency of tone, ability to follow complex multi-turn instructions, tendency toward certain failure modes. Neither is sufficient alone. Vibes without measurements are anecdata. Measurements without vibes miss the texture of actual use. The best practice: maintain your own eval suite, a set of tasks specific to your domain that you run against each new model, and combine those results with hands-on testing.

Pay attention to the **scaffolding** too. When you see "Model X achieves 74% on SWE-bench," the model itself is only part of the system. The full system typically includes retrieval pipelines, tool use, retry logic, and sometimes multi-agent architectures. The headline number doesn't distinguish between "the model is better" and "the team built better infrastructure around the model." Both matter, but they have different implications for what you can expect when you deploy the base model via an API. Always ask: what's the model doing, and what's the scaffolding doing? The answer changes your architecture.

## Brief Field History

The modern AI era has a specific origin point, and it's not GPT. It's a 2017 paper called "Attention Is All You Need" by Vaswani et al. The **transformer architecture** introduced in that paper replaced the recurrent neural networks that had dominated sequence modeling with a mechanism called **self-attention**, a way for the model to relate every token in a sequence to every other token simultaneously, rather than processing them one at a time. The architectural change was decisive. It made training massively parallelizable (RNNs had to process sequences sequentially, which limited how much hardware you could throw at them), and it handled long-range dependencies far better than anything before it.

The transformer enabled scaling, and scaling produced GPT. OpenAI's GPT series (GPT-1 in 2018, GPT-2 in 2019, GPT-3 in 2020) demonstrated that transformer language models, trained on progressively more data with progressively more parameters, produced progressively more capable systems. GPT-2 was notable for being the first model that OpenAI initially declined to release, citing safety concerns. That decision now looks quaint but was controversial at the time. GPT-3, with 175 billion parameters, demonstrated **in-context learning**: the ability to perform tasks it was never explicitly trained for, simply by providing examples in the prompt. This surprised researchers and opened the door to prompt engineering as a discipline.

But GPT-3 was powerful and dangerous in equal measure. It could generate toxic content, misinformation, and harmful instructions with the same fluency it used for helpful responses. The breakthrough that made LLMs commercially viable wasn't a bigger model; it was RLHF. InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022) showed that reinforcement learning from human feedback could substantially improve a model's helpfulness and reduce its harmfulness without sacrificing much capability. ChatGPT, launched in November 2022, was essentially GPT-3.5 with RLHF, and it became the fastest-growing consumer application in history. The period from late 2022 through 2026 was a phase transition. Not incremental progress, but a qualitative change in what was possible and what the public expected from AI.

The competitive landscape shifted fast. Google's Gemini (evolved from PaLM), Anthropic's Claude, Meta's Llama (open-weight), Mistral, and others formed a multi-player frontier. Open-weight models like Llama 3 brought near-frontier performance to anyone with a GPU, changing the economics of deployment. **Reasoning models** — OpenAI's o1 and o3 series, which generate extended chains of thought before answering — opened a new capability tier, trading latency and cost for substantially better performance on hard problems. Anthropic's Claude models introduced extended thinking along similar lines. These models bet that test-time compute — spending more computation during inference, not just during training — can unlock capabilities that pure scaling of training hadn't reached.

As of early 2026, an honest assessment of the frontier. What's solved, or close to it: summarization, translation, simple code generation, question-answering over provided context, and structured data extraction. These tasks work reliably enough for production use with appropriate guardrails. What's unsolved but progressing: complex multi-step reasoning, reliable long-horizon agent behavior, mathematical proof generation, and tasks requiring genuine novelty rather than recombination. What's actively contested: whether current architectures can reach human-level reasoning through scaling alone, whether LLMs "understand" anything in a meaningful sense, whether the scaling laws that have held for six years will continue to hold, and what the practical limits of the technology are. Honest people disagree on these questions, and anyone who tells you they know the answers is selling something.

Two developments from 2024-2025 deserve special mention because they changed the practical landscape for AI engineers. First, **long context windows** expanded from the 4K-8K tokens that were standard in 2023 to 128K-1M+ tokens by 2025. This qualitatively changed what you could do in a single model call. Entire codebases, full legal contracts, hours of meeting transcripts — all could now fit in context, reducing the need for complex chunking and retrieval pipelines in many applications. Second, **tool use and function calling** matured from a fragile novelty to a reliable production pattern. Models learned to generate structured tool calls — searching the web, querying databases, executing code — and to incorporate the results into their reasoning. This capability is what makes modern AI agents possible, and it's the foundation for much of what we'll build in later chapters.

The trajectory from "Attention Is All You Need" to the present — nine years — is remarkable for the speed of progress and for how much remains uncertain. The field has simultaneously produced the most capable AI systems ever built and the least understood ones. That combination of power and opacity is what makes AI engineering both exciting and treacherous.

## Reality Check

> Most AI curricula skip mental models or compress them into a single lecture before rushing to API calls. Resist that pressure. The framing that LLMs "understand" or "reason" is contested territory — not settled science — and building on contested foundations without acknowledging the cracks leads to brittle systems and brittle thinking. A better mental model: these are extremely capable pattern-completion engines that can simulate a wide range of human cognitive tasks, from writing poetry to debugging code to solving differential equations. That framing is both more accurate and, if you sit with it, more impressive than the anthropomorphized version. A system that doesn't "understand" language in the human sense but can nonetheless generate expert-level medical advice or write a working compiler is a novel kind of artifact. Treating it as either a glorified autocomplete or a nascent mind will lead you astray. Treat it as what it is — something new — and you'll make better engineering decisions.

## Case Study: What the Model Builders Admit They Don't Know

Anthropic and OpenAI both publish documentation alongside their major model releases — Anthropic's **model cards** and OpenAI's **system cards** — that are unusually candid about limitations and unknowns. Reading these documents is a calibration exercise: if the people who built the models are uncertain about certain behaviors, you should be too.

OpenAI's GPT-4 system card (2023) acknowledged several categories of concern: the model can generate convincing misinformation, it can be manipulated through adversarial prompts to bypass safety training, and its performance on tasks requiring real-world knowledge degrades for events after its training cutoff. More striking is what the system card says about evaluation gaps — the company acknowledges that their safety testing is incomplete and that the model may have failure modes they haven't discovered. The GPT-4o and o1 system cards continued this pattern, with increasingly detailed taxonomies of risk but also increasingly frank admissions that comprehensive evaluation of a model's full behavior space is currently intractable.

Anthropic's Claude model cards take a similar approach, with detailed descriptions of known failure modes: sycophancy (agreeing with users even when they're wrong), hallucination patterns, inconsistent application of safety guidelines, and difficulty with certain types of mathematical reasoning. Anthropic has also published research on interpretability — the effort to understand what's happening inside the model's weights — that underscores how much remains opaque even to the model's creators. Their work on identifying features within Claude's neural network (2024) was a breakthrough in understanding individual circuits, but it also highlighted the vast distance between identifying individual features and understanding how those features compose into complex behaviors.

The lesson isn't that these models are dangerous or broken. It's that the people with the most information about them maintain real uncertainty about important aspects of their behavior. Your mental model should incorporate this uncertainty. When you build a production system on top of an LLM, you're building on a foundation whose properties are empirically characterized but not formally specified. Your system needs to be designed for graceful degradation, not just correct behavior.

Read these documents yourself — they're publicly available and written for a general technical audience. Pay attention to the language: where do the authors use words like "may," "sometimes," and "we believe" versus "we have demonstrated" and "consistently"? The hedging is intellectual honesty about a technology whose boundaries are still being mapped, not corporate defensiveness. Developing your own instinct for reading between the lines of technical documentation is a skill that will serve you throughout your career in this field.

## Practical Exercise: The Honest State of Technology Memo

**Objective:** Write a one-page (roughly 500-700 word) "State of the Technology" memo about LLMs, aimed at a non-technical executive who needs to make resourcing decisions. The memo should set real expectations — neither hyping capabilities nor dismissing them.

**Specification:**

1. The memo must cover: what LLMs can reliably do today, what they cannot reliably do, and what falls in the uncertain middle.
2. Use at least three specific, cited sources (model cards, benchmark results, published research). No blog posts or tweets as primary sources.
3. Include at least one concrete example of the capability/reliability gap — a task where the technology demos well but production deployment is hard.
4. Avoid both "AI will change everything" hype and "it's just autocomplete" dismissiveness. Your reader should come away with a calibrated view.
5. The memo must be readable by someone who doesn't know what a transformer is. No jargon without inline definitions.

**Acceptance Criteria:**

- A technical reviewer should find no factual errors.
- A non-technical reader should understand every sentence without external references.
- The memo should be useful — it should help the executive make at least one concrete decision (e.g., whether to invest in an AI-powered feature, how to evaluate vendor claims, what timeline expectations are reasonable).
- The tone should be authoritative without being condescending.

**Evaluation Component:** After writing the memo, write a 200-word self-critique identifying which claims in your memo are strongest (backed by clear evidence), which are weakest (based on extrapolation or judgment), and which would need updating first as the technology evolves.

**Time Estimate:** ~3 hours (1.5 hours research and reading sources, 1 hour writing, 0.5 hours revision and self-critique).

## Checkpoint

After completing this chapter and the exercise, you should be able to confirm the following:

- I can explain why LLMs are probabilistic, not deterministic, and describe what that means for system design.
- I can identify when a benchmark number is misleading — whether due to saturation, metric choice, or optimization pressure.
- I can explain the capability/reliability gap to a non-technical person, using a concrete example.
- I understand why "the model understands" is a contested framing and can articulate the strongest arguments on both sides.
- I can trace the lineage from the transformer architecture (2017) to modern reasoning models (2025-2026) and identify the key inflection points.
- I can distinguish between the effects of pretraining, instruction tuning, and RLHF on model behavior.
- I can read a model card or system card and extract actionable information about limitations and failure modes.
