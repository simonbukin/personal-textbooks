## Opening

This book has been a compressed education. Weeks or months of focused work on skills that usually develop over years. But the real development happens after the book — in the ongoing practice of looking, making, and refining.

## The Habit Stack

Development of taste requires regular practice. Here's a sustainable stack of habits that compound over time.

### Daily (10 minutes)

**Screenshot and annotate.** One designed thing. Three observations. This takes ten minutes and trains your eye continuously. After a month, you've made 30 observations. After a year, 365. The accumulation is powerful.

The discipline is consistency, not depth. A quick observation is better than a skipped day. Over time, the observations get sharper automatically.

### Weekly (1 hour)

**Browse design references.** Fonts In Use, Brand New, Typewolf, Mobbin, Dribbble (selectively), Awwwards (critically). See what's being made. Notice what's trending. Evaluate what's good and what's just popular.

**Redesign one small element.** Pick something you use: a button, a modal, an empty state. Redesign just that element. Practice the craft in miniature.

The weekly habit keeps you connected to the broader design conversation without consuming too much time.

### Monthly (half day)

**Full design teardown.** Pick one product and go deep. Analyze everything: typography, color, layout, interaction, UX, brand coherence. Write it up. This extended analysis strengthens your ability to see systematically.

**AI evaluation session.** Generate 10 interfaces with AI. Evaluate each. What's wrong with them? What's right? This calibrates your sense of where AI succeeds and fails, which evolves as AI improves.

### Quarterly

**Ship something publicly.** A side project, a case study, a teardown, a contribution. The quarterly shipping cadence ensures you maintain a public practice.

**Revisit your taste journal.** Read your daily observations from the past quarter. Notice what's changed. What do you see now that you didn't see before? What biases persist? The reflection surfaces growth and persistent blind spots.

### The Compound Effect

These habits seem small. Ten minutes a day. An hour a week. Half a day a month. But compounded over years, they're transformative.

A designer who does this for five years has made 1,800+ observations, redesigned 260+ elements, analyzed 60+ products in depth. Their eye is trained in a way that can't be shortcut. That training is their moat.

## Trend Literacy vs. Trend Following

Trends happen. Glassmorphism. Bento grids. AI-generated illustration. Neomorphism. Brutalist revivals. Some have staying power; most don't.

**Trend following** is copying the latest style without understanding why it emerged or whether it's appropriate for your context. Trend followers are always chasing, always behind, and always interchangeable. They adopt whatever is popular because it's popular.

**Trend literacy** is understanding what's happening and why. Glassmorphism emerged partly as a reaction to flat design's severity — a reintroduction of depth and texture. Bento grids became popular as a way to present complexity accessibly. Understanding *why* trends emerge helps you evaluate whether they're appropriate for your work.

The trend-literate designer can:
- Recognize a trend when they see it
- Articulate why it's trending (what problem does it solve, what aesthetic does it satisfy?)
- Evaluate whether it fits their context (is this trend appropriate for my product and audience?)
- Adopt, adapt, or ignore based on judgment, not fashion

Trend followers are commoditized. If you just adopt whatever's popular, you're interchangeable with anyone else who adopts whatever's popular. Trend-literate designers make original choices *informed by* what's happening. That's different.

## Cross-Pollination

The best interface designers steal from other fields.

### Architecture

Architecture teaches spatial relationships and how they create feeling. The way light enters a building, the progression through spaces, the relationship between scale and intimacy. These lessons transfer to interface design — the "spatial model" of an interface, the way users move through screens, the density and breathing room.

### Film

Film teaches pacing and editing. How long to hold on a shot. When to cut. How to control attention through composition and timing. These lessons inform interface motion and page-level narrative — the pacing of a landing page, the timing of transitions, the choreography of multi-element animations.

### Fashion

Fashion teaches texture and proportion. The relationship between materials, the way garments drape and move, the difference between structured and fluid. These lessons inform digital texture and visual weight — the "feel" of interfaces that goes beyond flat color.

### Editorial Design

Magazine and editorial design teaches sequence and storytelling. How to lead a reader through content, how to create pages that reward exploration, how to balance density and breathing room. These lessons inform landing pages, documentation, and any multi-screen flow.

### Industrial Design

Product design teaches material honesty and tactile quality. The way physical products communicate their purpose through form. These lessons inform icon design, component shapes, and the "physical plausibility" of digital objects.

### Why Cross-Pollination Matters

AI is trained on existing interfaces. Its output converges toward the average of its training data. The designer who only looks at interfaces produces work that looks like interfaces.

The designer who looks at architecture, film, fashion, editorial, and industrial design brings references AI hasn't synthesized. Those references produce ideas that stand out.

Build a practice of looking outside your field. Subscribe to architecture blogs. Watch analysis of film editing. Notice fashion advertising. Read print magazines. The ideas that separate your work from generic output will come from places AI doesn't look.

## The Moving Target: AI Capabilities Over Time

What AI couldn't do in 2024 it can do in 2026. What it can't do in 2026 it may do in 2028. The capabilities are a moving target.

This could be discouraging. If AI keeps improving, does skill matter?

Skill matters more, not less. Here's why.

**As AI capabilities expand, there's more generated output that needs curation.** More AI-generated images means more need for people who can evaluate which images are actually good. More AI-generated layouts means more need for people who can recognize which layouts serve users. More AI-generated code means more need for people who can judge whether the code produces good experiences.

Curation is taste. The ability to evaluate quality, make contextual judgments, and maintain creative intent. That's what this book has been developing.

**Your moat is not any specific skill AI lacks.** If your value is "I can do X that AI can't," you're betting on X staying hard for AI. Bad bet. X might become easy next year.

**Your moat is the meta-skill of taste.** Taste is the ability to evaluate *any* output — human or machine — against criteria of quality, appropriateness, and intent. As long as there's output that needs evaluation, taste is valuable. And there will always be output that needs evaluation, because taste is definitionally about context, and AI can't fully capture context.

## Your Moat, Restated

Let me be specific about the capabilities where human designers hold decisive advantage:

**Contextual appropriateness.** Knowing what's right for *this* product, *this* audience, *this* moment. AI knows what's common; humans know what's appropriate.

**Emotional calibration.** Feeling whether something is warm enough, serious enough, playful enough. AI can't feel. Humans can.

**Cultural sensitivity.** Understanding how different audiences will perceive choices. What works in one culture may fail in another. AI averages across cultures; humans navigate them.

**Typographic refinement.** The optical adjustments that make type come alive. AI sets defaults; humans see what defaults miss.

**Strategic restraint.** Knowing when to leave space, when to remove features, when less is more. AI generates on request; humans know when not to request.

**Narrative coherence.** Creating experiences that unfold with purpose. AI produces moments; humans compose journeys.

**Brand consistency over time.** Maintaining a coherent identity across years of decisions. AI sees individual prompts; humans see trajectories.

**Productive disagreement.** Pushing back on stakeholders when the proposed direction is wrong. AI complies; humans advocate.

These capabilities compound. A designer with strong contextual judgment and emotional calibration and typographic refinement and strategic restraint is not 4x more valuable than one with one capability — they're exponentially more valuable. The capabilities interact.

Invest in these disproportionately. They're career insurance.

## Taste Interlude: The Feeling of Knowing

At a certain point in development, something shifts. You look at an interface and you know — before analysis, before vocabulary, before frameworks — whether it's right.

This isn't mystical intuition. It's pattern recognition built from thousands of hours of looking, naming, and making. Your brain has internalized so many examples of good and bad design that it processes new examples instantly, below conscious awareness.

You *feel* that the typography is wrong before you can name why. You *feel* that the layout is off before you identify the specific problem. You *feel* that the color is right before you articulate the relationship.

This feeling is your taste, fully internalized.

The goal isn't to replace this feeling with analysis. The feeling is often faster and more accurate than conscious analysis. The goal is to have analysis available when you need to explain the feeling to someone else — when a stakeholder asks why you want to change the headline size, when a teammate questions the color choice, when a client pushes back on the layout.

The feeling says "this is wrong." The analysis says "here's why, specifically, and here's the principle it violates."

Both matter. The feeling guides your work. The analysis communicates it.
