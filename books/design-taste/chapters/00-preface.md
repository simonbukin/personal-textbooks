# Preface: Why Taste Is the Only Defensible Skill

*The floor rose. The ceiling didn't move. And that changed everything.*

---

Something happened to design between 2023 and 2026. You felt it even if you couldn't name it. The barrier to producing something that looks like good design collapsed almost overnight. AI tools that could generate passable layouts, suggest reasonable color palettes, and write functional component code became ubiquitous. A founder with no design training could prompt their way to a landing page that wouldn't embarrass them. An engineer could ship a dashboard that looked, at first glance, professional.

This should have been cause for celebration. Democratized design. Everyone a designer. Instead, something stranger happened: the world flooded with mediocrity that looks correct. Interfaces that follow all the rules and break none of them. Color palettes that are harmonious and say nothing. Typography that is readable and has no voice. Motion that exists and communicates nothing beyond "this element moved." We got design slop — output that has the *form* of design without the *intent* behind it.

The floor rose. Anyone can now produce work that would have required years of training a decade ago. But the ceiling — the level at which design becomes genuinely exceptional, where it creates meaning and not just function, where it makes you feel something specific and deliberate — that ceiling didn't move at all. The tools got better at producing average. They got no better at producing great.

This created an opportunity that this book is designed to help you seize.

## What Taste Actually Is

Let me define the word that appears in this book's subtitle, because most people use it wrong.

**Taste** is not preference. Preference is personal and untransferable. You prefer blue to green, or you don't. You like dense interfaces or spacious ones. These preferences are real but they're not design skills — they're autobiographical facts about your nervous system. You can't teach someone your preferences, and your preferences don't make you a better designer.

Taste is contextual judgment. Taste says *why* something works *for its intended purpose*, not whether you personally like it. Taste recognizes that a medical dashboard should feel different from a consumer social app, and knows what "different" means in practice — the density, the color temperature, the typographic choices, the interaction patterns. Taste is what allows you to look at a design for a brutalist architecture firm and understand why the raw, uncomfortable typography is exactly right, even if you would never want to live with it. Taste is *informed judgment applied to specific contexts*.

And here's the crucial part: taste can be developed. It is not an innate gift distributed at birth. It is a skill built through thousands of hours of looking, naming, and making. This book is a systematic program for building that skill.

The reason taste matters more now than it did five years ago is precisely because the floor rose. When producing competent work required significant skill, competent work had value. Now that AI can produce competent work in seconds, competence is table stakes. What separates the work that matters from the work that doesn't is whether someone with taste — someone with informed contextual judgment — shaped the output into something specific, deliberate, and right for its context.

## The Gap

Throughout this book, I'll refer to something I call **the gap**. The gap is the distance between what AI can generate and what a designer with developed taste can produce. This gap is not about correctness or functionality. AI output is often correct and functional. The gap is about *intent*.

Look at any AI-generated interface and you'll see decisions that were made because they're statistically common, not because they're right for this specific product, this specific audience, this specific emotional register. The typeface is one of five that appear in 60% of the training data. The spacing is standard. The color palette is harmonious in a way that means it won't clash, but it's harmonious in the same way as ten thousand other palettes — it communicates "designed" without communicating anything specific about what it's designed *for*.

Your job as a designer is to widen this gap. Every decision you make that couldn't have been made by averaging a million interfaces is a decision that proves your value. Every time you choose a typeface because it's *right*, not because it's *common*; every time you set spacing that creates the exact emotional feeling this particular product needs; every time you break a convention because breaking it serves the user better than following it — you widen the gap.

AI will keep improving. The floor will keep rising. But the gap doesn't close, because the gap isn't about capability. It's about intent. AI generates defaults. You make choices. That distinction is permanent.

## Who This Book Is For

This book is for builders who make design decisions — and want to make better ones.

You might be an engineer who ships interfaces but hasn't systematically trained your eye. A product designer who learned on the job and wants to fill the gaps. A founder who designs your own product. A freelancer who needs to be good at everything. What you have in common: you make real design decisions that ship to real users, and you want those decisions to be defensible, not just defensible-looking.

You have taste instincts — you know when something feels wrong — but you can't always articulate or defend those instincts. You want to cross the line from "person who makes design decisions" to "person whose design decisions are genuinely good."

This book assumes familiarity with digital products. You've used design tools. You've looked at interfaces critically. You understand that a button has states and a form needs validation feedback. If you write code, the examples will feel natural; if you don't, they'll still make sense. This is not a book that explains what a dropdown is.

This book is practice-focused. Every principle is illustrated with real products, real typefaces, real decisions. If a concept isn't useful for making actual design choices, it isn't in this book. Theory serves practice, not the other way around.

## What This Book Replaces

You should not need to read the classic design texts after finishing this book. This is not because those texts aren't valuable — they are. It's because this book synthesizes their core ideas into original prose, applies them to modern interface design, and integrates AI into the practice throughout.

The ideas from Frank Chimero's *The Shape of Design* — design as intent and craft, not just problem-solving — are woven through every chapter. Ellen Lupton's *Thinking with Type* and Robert Bringhurst's *The Elements of Typographic Style* inform the typography chapter, their technical precision translated into practical screen typography. Steve Krug's *Don't Make Me Think* shapes the UX chapter's emphasis on usability as common sense. Don Norman's mental models, affordances, and feedback loops appear wherever interaction is discussed. Josef Albers' relational color theory transforms how the color chapter approaches palette building. Müller-Brockmann's grid systems become compositional infrastructure in the layout chapter. The design systems chapter builds on Yesenia Perez-Cruz's argument that systems should encode opinion, not just consistency. Clayton Christensen's Jobs-to-Be-Done framework becomes a practical lens for interface decisions.

I've read these books so you don't have to — though if you want to go deeper on any topic, an appendix provides annotated further reading.

## How This Book Works

The book has four parts.

**Part One: Foundations of Seeing** trains your eye. You'll learn to perceive design decisions automatically, distinguish preference from taste, understand typography deeply, and work with color as a relational phenomenon.

**Part Two: The Grammar of Interfaces** covers the structural skills. Layout, composition, space. Interaction and motion. Design systems as creative infrastructure. These chapters give you the grammar — the combinatorial rules that let you construct anything.

**Part Three: Designing with Intent** is strategy. UX thinking beyond screens. Brand as a system of associations. Designing under real-world constraints. This is where craft meets purpose.

**Part Four: Thriving in the Machine Age** is about the meta-skills. Working with AI without surrendering judgment. Building a public practice. Staying irreplaceable as capabilities evolve.

Every chapter follows the same structure: an opening that frames why the topic matters for taste, core content that teaches the material through concrete examples, a "Taste Interlude" that goes deeper on a connoisseur-level distinction, a section on AI integration, and projects that produce real, shippable work.

The projects are the most important part. Taste is not developed by reading — it's developed by making. Every project has constraints, because constraints drive creativity. Every project has a "taste check" that asks you to evaluate your own work against the chapter's principles. Every project has an AI integration angle, because learning to work with AI is part of developing taste in this era.

## The Work Ahead

You're going to develop the ability to look at any interface and immediately see what's working and what isn't — and explain why. You're going to build a vocabulary that lets you articulate design decisions instead of just feeling them. You're going to produce work that demonstrates your taste to anyone who sees it.

Most importantly, you're going to widen the gap. Every chapter pushes you toward decisions that AI cannot make, judgments that require context and intent, refinements that separate your work from the slop.

The floor rose. But you're not trying to be above the floor. You're reaching for the ceiling.

Let's begin.
