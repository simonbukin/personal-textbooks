# Building a Public Design Practice

*Taste that isn't visible doesn't exist professionally. Ship, write, and be known for your point of view.*

---

## Opening

Everything in this book develops your taste in private. The screenshot annotations, the project work, the careful refinements — they build your skills, but they don't build your reputation.

In the design world, reputation is earned through visible work. The portfolios people review, the case studies they read, the teardowns they share, the side projects that demonstrate thinking. If your taste isn't visible, it might as well not exist — at least professionally.

This chapter is about building a public practice: showing your work, demonstrating your thinking, and positioning yourself as someone with a point of view worth hiring.

---

## The Portfolio Paradox

Here's the problem: your best design work is probably invisible.

The spacing you tweaked until it felt right. The typographic refinement that took an hour to get perfect. The animation timing you adjusted by 50ms increments. The error state you designed with as much care as the happy path.

None of this shows up in a screenshot.

Portfolios reward *visible* work — dramatic hero sections, bold color choices, impressive scope. But much of design craft is subtle. The best work often looks "obvious" — as if there was no other way it could be. That's a sign of good design, but it's a terrible portfolio story.

### Creating Portfolio-Worthy Work

If your day job doesn't produce portfolio pieces, create them. This is why every chapter in this book ends with projects. They're not just learning exercises — they're portfolio pieces.

A complete design system you built from scratch demonstrates systems thinking. A typography-only redesign demonstrates typographic craft. A 48-hour landing page demonstrates speed and judgment. These are portfolio entries as valid as client work.

The portfolio-worthy bar: could this go on your website as evidence of your capabilities? If yes, it's portfolio-worthy.

## Case Studies That Demonstrate Thinking

A case study is not "here are my final screens."

Final screens are outcomes. Case studies are stories. The story is: here's a problem, here are the constraints, here's how I thought through it, here are the decisions I made, and here's why.

### Case Study Structure

**The problem:** What was broken, missing, or possible? Why did this work need doing? Ground the reader in the situation.

**The constraints:** What couldn't change? What was given? Time, budget, technology, existing systems, organizational limits. Constraints shape decisions; show them.

**The exploration:** What did you try? What did you reject, and why? This is where you show thinking, not just output. The rejected directions are as important as the final one — they prove you considered alternatives.

**The decision:** What did you choose? Why this direction over others? Be specific about the reasoning. "I chose X because users need Y, and alternative Z would have compromised that."

**The result:** What happened? Metrics if available. Qualitative feedback if not. Did the design achieve its goals? What would you do differently?

### Show Process, Not Just Polish

The temptation is to show only beautiful final work. But process shows thinking.

Include:
- Early sketches or wireframes that show where you started
- Screenshots of exploration that didn't make it
- Documentation of the decision points
- Before/after comparisons
- Evidence of iteration (version 1 vs. version 2 vs. final)

A case study that shows messy exploration followed by considered refinement demonstrates real design practice. A case study that shows only perfect finals demonstrates Photoshop skills.

### Length and Depth

A case study should be as long as it needs to be and no longer. Small projects might need 500 words. Complex projects might need 2,000.

The test: after reading, does the reader understand your thinking? If they could explain why you made the decisions you made, the case study succeeded.

## Design Criticism as Practice

Writing about other people's design sharpens your own eye and builds your public profile.

### The Design Teardown

Pick a product. Analyze its design decisions in public. Propose improvements with rationale.

This is not about dunking. It's about demonstrating that you can see, name, and suggest improvements. The tone should be constructive, even when critical.

Structure:
- What is this product? Who is it for?
- What is the product doing well? (Start with positives)
- What could be improved? (Be specific: this element, this problem, this suggested change)
- Why would these changes help users?

Published design criticism is a professional signal. It says: "I think critically about this craft. I can articulate what works and what doesn't."

### The Model: Bethany Heck's Font Review Journal

Bethany Heck built a reputation partly through Font Review Journal — deep, informed analysis of typefaces. The reviews are rigorous, generous, and demonstrate expertise. They're not "this font is good" or "this font is bad" — they're thorough examinations of what makes a typeface distinctive, useful, or problematic.

This is the model for design criticism. Go deep. Be specific. Show you know what you're talking about.

## Positioning: Your Superpower

"Design engineer" is not a compromise. It's a superpower.

The traditional model separates designers (who make mockups) from engineers (who build them). The handoff between them creates friction, misunderstanding, and lost fidelity.

You skip the handoff. You design and build. This means:

**You prototype at full fidelity.** No mockups that can't be built. No "and then engineering will figure out the animation." You create the real thing.

**You ship without translation.** The design in your head is the design that ships. No handoff documentation, no misinterpretation, no "engineering made it different."

**You iterate in hours, not days.** Try something → see it live → adjust → ship. The feedback loop is immediate.

In any fast-moving environment — startups, agencies, small teams, solo projects — this is extraordinarily valuable. The ability to go from idea to shipped product without intermediate steps saves time, reduces misunderstanding, and produces better results.

### Positioning Yourself

Don't position yourself as someone who does two things at 50% each. Position yourself as someone who does one integrated thing at 100%.

**Not:** "I'm a designer who can also code." (This suggests coding is secondary, apologetic.)

**Not:** "I'm an engineer who can also design." (This suggests design is an afterthought.)

**Instead:** "I design and build digital products. I think visually and ship code. The thinking and making are one practice."

The framing matters. "Design engineer" (one role) is stronger than "designer and developer" (two roles).

## The Compound Effect of Publishing

Every case study, design teardown, side project, and open-source contribution compounds.

You don't need to go viral. You need to be consistently visible at a level that demonstrates taste. One case study won't make your reputation. Twelve case studies, four design teardowns, and a few side projects over 18 months will.

This is the compound effect. Each piece adds to your body of work. Each piece is findable. Each piece demonstrates a consistent point of view.

Over time, you become "the person who writes thoughtfully about design." That's a professional identity that creates opportunities. Recruiters find you. Collaborators reach out. Speaking invitations arrive.

### Publishing Rhythm

A sustainable rhythm might be:
- Monthly: One case study or teardown
- Quarterly: One significant side project
- Annually: Portfolio refresh

This is achievable alongside a full-time job. One piece per month is one weekend per month dedicated to public work. The compound effect happens over 12-24 months.

## Taste Interlude: Designing Your Own Site

Your personal site is the one design you have total control over. No client constraints, no stakeholder compromises, no inherited systems. Just your taste, fully expressed.

This means it should demonstrate *everything*: typography, color, layout, motion, brand thinking. It's both your portfolio container and a portfolio piece itself.

Most engineer portfolios are competent and forgettable. A template, some project links, contact info. They look like every other portfolio.

Yours should make someone stop scrolling.

### What Makes a Portfolio Memorable

**Distinctive typography.** Not Inter and system fonts. A typeface choice that expresses your personality. Set with care.

**Purposeful color.** Not gray and blue because they're safe. A palette that creates atmosphere and reflects who you are.

**Intentional layout.** Not a grid of project cards. A composition that creates visual interest and guides attention.

**Considered motion.** Not gratuitous animation. Motion that serves the experience and adds personality.

**Point of view.** Not just a list of projects. A sense of who you are, what you believe, what you value.

Your portfolio is a design decision. Every element should be *chosen*. If any element is default, you're demonstrating defaultness.

## Projects

### Project 1: Three Case Studies

Write full case studies for 3 projects from this book (or other work).

**Each case study must include:**
- Problem: What situation called for design?
- Constraints: What was given, what couldn't change?
- Exploration: What did you try, what did you reject?
- Decision: What did you choose, why this over alternatives?
- Result: What happened, what did you learn?

**Constraints:**
- At least one project should be from this book's exercises
- Each case study should be 600-1,500 words
- Include visuals: process work, before/after, final screens
- Host them on your portfolio or a public platform

**Taste check:**
- Would someone reading these understand your thinking process?
- Do the case studies show range (different types of problems, different scales)?
- Would you be proud to share these in a job application?

### Project 2: Published Teardown

Write and publish a design teardown of a product you use.

**Requirements:**
- Pick a product with enough complexity to analyze (not a single-page app)
- Analyze multiple aspects: typography, color, layout, interaction, UX
- Propose specific improvements (not just "this could be better" but "change X to Y because Z")
- Maintain a constructive tone (not a rant)

**Deliverable:**
- Published piece (your blog, Medium, wherever)
- 1,500-2,500 words
- Include screenshots with annotations
- Propose at least 3 specific improvements with mockups if possible

**Taste check:**
- Is the analysis fair? Did you acknowledge what works before critiquing?
- Would the product team find this useful if they read it?
- Does this demonstrate expertise, or does it just express opinion?

### Project 3: Portfolio Site Rebuild

Rebuild your personal portfolio site from scratch.

**Requirements:**
- Typography that makes a statement (not system fonts)
- Memorable color or atmosphere (not gray and blue)
- At least one intentional convention-break in layout (something distinctive)
- Purposeful motion (page transitions, hover states, something that adds personality)
- The three case studies from Project 1 integrated
- A documented design system underneath (tokens, components)

**Constraints:**
- Must be live on the internet
- Must work on mobile
- Must be accessible (keyboard navigable, good contrast, alt text)
- Must load fast (performance is UX)

**Deliverable:**
- Live portfolio URL
- Brief documentation of your design system
- This is the capstone. Everything you've learned throughout this book should be visible here.

**Taste check:**
- Would someone remember this site a week after visiting?
- Does it demonstrate your taste, or does it look like everyone else's?
- Would you be proud to send this link to anyone?
