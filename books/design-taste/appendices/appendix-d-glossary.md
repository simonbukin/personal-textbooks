# Appendix D: Glossary

*Every bolded term from the book, defined.*

---

**8px grid.** A spatial system where all spacing values are multiples of 8. Creates consistent rhythm across an interface. (Chapter 4)

**Affordance.** What an object allows you to do — what actions are possible. A button affords pressing. A slider affords dragging. (Chapter 7)

**Alignment.** The visual connection between elements through shared edges or axes. One of the four C.R.A.P. principles. (Chapter 1)

**Ascenders.** The parts of lowercase letters that rise above the x-height: the tops of b, d, h, l. (Chapter 2)

**Baseline.** The invisible line that letters sit on. Most letters rest on the baseline; descenders drop below it. (Chapter 2)

**Brand.** The total set of associations a person holds about a product, company, or entity. Built from every touchpoint, not just visual identity. (Chapter 8)

**Bracketed serifs.** Serifs that curve smoothly into the main stroke, as in Garamond. (Chapter 2)

**Cap height.** The height of capital letters in a typeface. (Chapter 2)

**Closure.** The Gestalt principle that the mind completes incomplete shapes. A three-quarter circle reads as a complete circle with part hidden. (Chapter 1)

**Component tokens.** Design tokens specific to a single component, used when semantic tokens don't fit. (Chapter 6)

**Continuity.** The Gestalt principle that the eye follows continuous lines and curves. Aligned elements feel connected. (Chapter 1)

**Contrast.** The visual difference between elements. Effective contrast is obvious — timid contrast (small differences) creates confusion. One of the four C.R.A.P. principles. (Chapter 1)

**C.R.A.P.** Contrast, Repetition, Alignment, Proximity — the four basic operations of graphic design. (Chapter 1)

**Density.** How tightly packed elements are on a page. Dense layouts serve data-heavy contexts; spacious layouts serve persuasion contexts. (Chapter 4)

**Descenders.** The parts of lowercase letters that fall below the baseline: the tails of g, p, y, q. (Chapter 2)

**Design slop.** AI-generated or AI-influenced design that looks professional but has no intent — output that works but means nothing. (Preface, Chapter 10)

**Display typefaces.** Typefaces designed for large sizes — headlines, logos, titles. Often have high stroke contrast and refined details that fail at body sizes. (Chapter 2)

**Easing.** How an animation's value changes over time. Ease-out (fast start, slow finish), ease-in (slow start, fast finish), ease-in-out (slow-fast-slow). (Chapter 5)

**Feedback.** Information that tells users what happened after they took an action. Crucial for the gulf of evaluation. (Chapter 7)

**Feedforward.** Information that tells users what will happen before they take an action. "Delete (cannot be undone)" is feedforward. (Chapter 7)

**Figure/ground.** The Gestalt principle that the visual system separates elements into foreground (figures) and background (ground). (Chapter 1)

**Geometric sans-serif.** Sans-serif typefaces constructed from geometric shapes. The "o" is often a near-perfect circle. Examples: Futura, Circular. (Chapter 2)

**Gestalt principles.** Psychological principles describing how humans perceive visual relationships: proximity, similarity, continuity, closure, figure/ground. (Chapter 1)

**Grid.** A compositional infrastructure of vertical lines that organize a page. Provides consistency while enabling variety. (Chapter 4)

**Grotesque/neo-grotesque.** Sans-serif typefaces aiming for neutrality, with minimal stroke contrast and uniform shapes. Examples: Helvetica, Inter. (Chapter 2)

**Gulf of evaluation.** The distance between what a system did and whether the user can tell. Minimized by clear feedback. (Chapter 7)

**Gulf of execution.** The distance between what a user wants to do and how the interface lets them do it. Minimized by clear, direct interaction paths. (Chapter 7)

**Humanist sans-serif.** Sans-serif typefaces retaining calligraphic influence — strokes vary in width, forms feel organic. Examples: Gill Sans, Source Sans. (Chapter 2)

**HSL.** Hue, Saturation, Lightness — a color model more intuitive than hex/RGB for design decisions. (Chapter 3)

**Jobs-to-Be-Done.** A framework for understanding what users actually want. Users "hire" products to do a job; understanding the job reveals design requirements. (Chapter 7)

**LCH.** Lightness, Chroma, Hue — a perceptually uniform color model where equal lightness values look equally bright. (Chapter 3)

**Line-height.** The vertical space between lines of text. Also called leading. Typically 1.4-1.6 for body text, tighter for headlines. (Chapter 2)

**Measure.** The length of a line of text, usually expressed in characters. Optimal: 45-75 characters per line. (Chapter 2)

**Mental model.** A user's assumptions about how an interface works, built from previous experience. Good design aligns with mental models. (Chapter 7)

**Modern/Didone serifs.** Serif typefaces with extreme stroke contrast — thick verticals, hairline horizontals. Examples: Didot, Bodoni. (Chapter 2)

**Modular scale.** A type scale where sizes are generated by multiplying a base by a consistent ratio. Creates coherent visual hierarchy. (Chapter 2)

**Old-style serifs.** Serif typefaces descended from Renaissance models. Moderate stroke contrast, warm character. Examples: Garamond, Bembo. (Chapter 2)

**Optical centering.** Positioning elements slightly above mathematical center because the eye perceives a point above true center as the visual middle. (Chapter 4)

**Orchestration.** Coordinating multiple animated elements — staggered entrances, overlapping transitions, motion hierarchy. (Chapter 5)

**Patterns.** Recurring compositions of primitives that solve common UI problems: forms, navigation, tables. (Chapter 6)

**Preference.** Personal, untransferable liking ("I prefer blue"). Distinguished from taste, which is contextual judgment. (Chapter 1)

**Primitives.** Base components: buttons, inputs, badges. Built from tokens, combined into patterns. (Chapter 6)

**Proximity.** The Gestalt principle that elements close together are perceived as belonging together. Also one of the four C.R.A.P. principles. (Chapter 1)

**Raw tokens.** The actual values in a design token system: hex colors, pixel values. (Chapter 6)

**Repetition.** Using the same visual treatment consistently throughout a design. Creates coherence. One of the four C.R.A.P. principles. (Chapter 1)

**Saccades.** Rapid eye movements that sample a scene. The eye doesn't see continuously; it jumps and samples. (Chapter 1)

**Semantic tokens.** Design tokens that describe intent rather than value: `color-surface-raised` rather than `gray-100`. (Chapter 6)

**Signifier.** What tells you an affordance exists. A shadow suggests a button is clickable. A blue underline suggests a link. (Chapter 7)

**Similarity.** The Gestalt principle that elements sharing visual properties are perceived as related. (Chapter 1)

**Simultaneous contrast.** The phenomenon where the same color looks different depending on surrounding colors. (Chapter 3)

**Slab serif.** Serif typefaces with thick, blocky serifs. Examples: Rockwell, Clarendon. (Chapter 2)

**Stagger.** Offsetting animation start times for multiple elements, creating a cascade effect. (Chapter 5)

**Stroke contrast.** The variation between thick and thin parts of a letter. High contrast is dramatic; low contrast is neutral. (Chapter 2)

**Taste.** Contextual judgment — the ability to evaluate what's right for a specific situation, not just what you prefer. Can be developed. (Preface, Chapter 1)

**Taste filter workflow.** Generate broadly with AI, curate ruthlessly with human judgment, refine by hand. (Chapter 10)

**Templates.** Page-level compositions that combine patterns: dashboard layouts, settings pages. (Chapter 6)

**Terminal.** The ending of a stroke in a letter. Ball terminal (circular), flat terminal, angled terminal. (Chapter 2)

**Text typefaces.** Typefaces designed for body copy — lower stroke contrast, generous x-height, optimized for sustained reading. (Chapter 2)

**The gap.** The distance between AI-generated output and human-refined output. Your professional value as a designer. (Preface)

**Theming.** The ability for a design system to express different moods by changing the token layer while keeping components constant. (Chapter 6)

**Tokens.** Atomic design decisions: colors, type sizes, spacing values. The foundation of a design system. (Chapter 6)

**Tracking.** Uniform adjustment of spacing between all letters in text. Appropriate for all-caps (positive tracking) or large display text (slight negative tracking). (Chapter 2)

**Transitional serifs.** Serif typefaces between old-style and modern. More contrast than old-style, more vertical stress. Examples: Baskerville, Georgia. (Chapter 2)

**Typographic color.** The overall grayness of a text block when squinted at. Well-set type has even color. (Chapter 2)

**Unbracketed serifs.** Serifs that meet the stroke at a sharp angle, as in Bodoni. (Chapter 2)

**Unhappy paths.** Error states, empty states, loading states, edge cases — the moments when the normal flow doesn't happen. (Chapter 7)

**Value structure.** The pattern of lightness/darkness across a design. If you squint, can you still read the hierarchy? (Chapter 3)

**Visual hierarchy.** The order in which elements are perceived. Controlled by size, contrast, position, and motion. (Chapter 1)

**Whitespace.** Empty space in a layout. Not waste — emphasis through absence. (Chapter 4)

**X-height.** The height of lowercase letters like "x" that have no ascenders or descenders. The most important property for screen readability. (Chapter 2)
