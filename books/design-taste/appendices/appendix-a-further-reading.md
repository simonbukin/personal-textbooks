# Appendix A: Further Reading

*For those who want to go deeper.*

---

This book synthesizes ideas from the design canon. You don't need to read these books — the core ideas are already here. But if you want to go deeper on any topic, these are where to go.

---

## Read First

These four books are worth reading in full. They're accessible, practical, and shaped how working designers think.

### The Design of Everyday Things — Don Norman

The foundational text on how humans interact with designed objects. Norman introduces mental models, affordances, signifiers, and feedback — vocabulary this book uses throughout. The examples are physical objects (doors, stoves, faucets), but the principles transfer directly to interfaces.

**What it offers beyond this book:** Deeper treatment of human cognition and error. Norman's analysis of why mistakes happen and how design can prevent them goes further than this book's summary. Read it if you want to understand the psychology behind usability.

### Thinking with Type — Ellen Lupton

A comprehensive visual guide to typography: anatomy, classification, hierarchy, and setting. Heavily illustrated with examples from both print and screen. This book taught a generation of designers to see type.

**What it offers beyond this book:** Extensive visual examples and historical context. Where this book covers typography in one chapter, Lupton's book is an entire education. Read it if you want to understand typography deeply rather than practically.

### Don't Make Me Think — Steve Krug

The clearest statement of usability as common sense. Krug argues that websites should be self-evident — users shouldn't have to think about how to use them. Includes practical guidance on lightweight usability testing.

**What it offers beyond this book:** The full usability testing methodology. This book touches on testing; Krug's book provides a complete, step-by-step approach. Read it if you're running usability research.

### Refactoring UI — Adam Wathan & Steve Schoger

Practical, engineer-friendly visual design guidance. Covers hierarchy, spacing, typography, color, and depth with specific, actionable rules. Written by developers for developers.

**What it offers beyond this book:** More concrete rules with visual examples. Where this book develops taste, Refactoring UI provides heuristics you can follow mechanically. Read it if you want a checklist approach alongside the taste-development approach.

## Read Next

These books go deeper into specific topics. Read them when you're ready to specialize.

### The Elements of Typographic Style — Robert Bringhurst

The bible of typography. Bringhurst treats type as a craft tradition stretching back centuries. Detailed coverage of optical spacing, proportions, and the relationship between type and page.

**What it offers beyond this book:** The deep craft of typography that this book only gestures at. Read it when you're serious about type and ready for density.

### Interaction of Color — Josef Albers

The definitive demonstration that color is relational. Albers' experiments show how the same color looks different depending on context. No color theory rules — just perception.

**What it offers beyond this book:** The experiments themselves. Reading about color relativity is one thing; seeing Albers' demonstrations is another. The book includes interactive color plates for hands-on learning.

### Grid Systems in Graphic Design — Josef Müller-Brockmann

The Swiss systematic approach to layout. Müller-Brockmann shows how grids create order while enabling variety. Heavily illustrated with his own work and others'.

**What it offers beyond this book:** Historical depth and visual examples. The grid principles in this book come from Müller-Brockmann; his book shows them in action across dozens of projects.

### About Face — Alan Cooper

The comprehensive reference on interaction design. Covers research, personas, patterns, and detailed guidance for common interface types. Dense but thorough.

**What it offers beyond this book:** Exhaustive pattern coverage. This book teaches principles; About Face catalogues implementations. Read it when you need specific guidance on a complex interaction pattern.

## Read When Ready

These books are excellent but require more context to appreciate fully.

### The Shape of Design — Frank Chimero

A meditation on why designers design. Chimero argues that design is fundamentally about intent — shaping how things are rather than accepting how things are. Philosophical rather than practical.

**What it offers beyond this book:** The "why" behind craft. This book focuses on how; Chimero's book asks why it matters. Read it when you want to reflect on purpose.

### Designing Interface Animation — Val Head

Comprehensive coverage of motion in interfaces. Easing, timing, principles, and patterns, all focused on digital products.

**What it offers beyond this book:** Depth on every motion topic. The animation chapter here is a summary; Head's book is the full treatment. Read it when motion is central to your work.

### Competing Against Luck — Clayton Christensen

Jobs-to-Be-Done theory from its originator. Christensen explains how to understand what customers actually want, with case studies from various industries.

**What it offers beyond this book:** The full JTBD framework. This book applies JTBD briefly; Christensen develops it thoroughly. Read it if you're working on product strategy, not just interface design.

### Expressive Design Systems — Yesenia Perez-Cruz

How design systems can encode creative direction, not just consistency. Perez-Cruz argues against the "component library is enough" mentality.

**What it offers beyond this book:** Deeper treatment of design system philosophy. The design systems chapter here takes her perspective; her book develops it fully. Read it when building or evolving a design system.

## Ongoing Resources

These websites and publications are worth following regularly.

### Fonts In Use
fontsinuse.com

Archive of typography in practice. Real-world examples of typefaces in use, organized by typeface, industry, and style. Excellent for pairing research and seeing typefaces in context.

### Brand New
underconsideration.com/brandnew

Daily coverage of corporate identity projects. Critique and discussion of rebrands, new identities, and brand evolutions. Good for staying current with brand design trends.

### Typewolf
typewolf.com

Curated typography inspiration. Features sites with notable type, plus guides to typeface recommendations. Useful for discovering typefaces and seeing them in context.

### Mobbin
mobbin.com

Organized screenshots of mobile apps and websites. Searchable by component, flow, and pattern. Useful for research when designing specific interactions.

### Linear Blog
linear.app/blog

Linear's product blog occasionally publishes detailed design explorations. Their approach to product design — opinionated, systematic, craft-focused — is worth studying.

### Figma Blog
figma.com/blog

Figma's blog covers design practice, tooling, and process. Relevant given Figma's centrality to modern design workflows.

---

## How to Use This List

Don't read all of these. Read what's relevant when it's relevant.

Starting out? Read Refactoring UI and Krug.

Going deep on typography? Read Lupton, then Bringhurst.

Building design systems? Read Perez-Cruz.

Working on motion? Read Head.

Thinking about purpose? Read Chimero.

The bibliography exists to serve you, not to assign homework. Follow your interests. The best book is the one you'll actually read.
