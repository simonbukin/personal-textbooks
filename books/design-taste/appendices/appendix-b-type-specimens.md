# Appendix B: Type Specimen Reference

*A curated reference of typefaces for screen design.*

---

This is not an exhaustive list. It's a curated selection of typefaces that work well on screens and are commonly available through Google Fonts, Adobe Fonts, or commercial licensing. For each: what it is, when to use it, and what to watch for.

---

## Sans-Serif Typefaces

### Grotesque/Neo-Grotesque

**Inter**
Designer: Rasmus Andersson
Classification: Neo-grotesque sans-serif

The workhorse of modern web design. Designed specifically for computer screens with attention to legibility at small sizes. Extensive weight range. Open-source and free.

*Best for:* UI text, body copy, anywhere you need reliable legibility without strong personality.
*Watch for:* Ubiquity. Inter is everywhere. Using it means you won't stand out typographically.

**Helvetica / Helvetica Neue**
Designer: Max Miedinger, Eduard Hoffmann
Classification: Neo-grotesque sans-serif

The canonical neutral sans-serif. Helvetica is everywhere because it tries to be nothing. Helvetica Neue is the refined version with more weights and better screen rendering.

*Best for:* Brands that want to disappear into neutrality. Situations where the type should not add personality.
*Watch for:* Default-ness. Helvetica often signals "didn't make a choice" rather than "chose neutrality."

**Aktiv Grotesk**
Designer: Dalton Maag
Classification: Neo-grotesque sans-serif

A more refined grotesque than Helvetica with better screen optimization. Available in a wide range of weights and widths. Less baggage than Helvetica.

*Best for:* When you want neutral sans-serif but want to avoid Helvetica's associations.
*Watch for:* Cost — it's commercially licensed, not free.

### Humanist Sans-Serif

**Source Sans Pro / Source Sans 3**
Designer: Paul Hunt
Classification: Humanist sans-serif

Adobe's open-source text face. Excellent legibility at all sizes, with a warmer character than grotesques. Tall x-height.

*Best for:* Body text, documentation, anywhere readability matters most.
*Watch for:* Slightly generic. Works everywhere but stands out nowhere.

**Fira Sans**
Designer: Erik Spiekermann, Ralph du Carrois
Classification: Humanist sans-serif

Originally designed for Firefox OS. Friendly without being casual. Works well for both UI and body text.

*Best for:* Products that want warmth without playfulness.
*Watch for:* Some weights are better than others; test carefully.

**Lato**
Designer: Łukasz Dziedzic
Classification: Humanist sans-serif

Warm and approachable. Semicircular details give it a friendly character while maintaining professionalism.

*Best for:* Marketing sites, products aiming for approachability.
*Watch for:* Can feel slightly dated as it was heavily used in the 2010s.

### Geometric Sans-Serif

**Futura**
Designer: Paul Renner
Classification: Geometric sans-serif

The original geometric sans. Pure geometric forms: the "o" is a perfect circle. Bold and confident. A classic.

*Best for:* Display use, headings, logos. Makes a strong statement.
*Watch for:* Difficult at body text sizes. Some letter combinations require manual kerning.

**Circular**
Designer: Laurenz Brunner
Classification: Geometric sans-serif

The geometric sans of the 2010s-2020s startup era. Soft, friendly geometric forms. Used by Spotify, Airbnb, and countless others.

*Best for:* When you want contemporary-feeling but friendly geometric.
*Watch for:* Extreme ubiquity. Using Circular says "startup" loud and clear.

**DM Sans**
Designer: Colophon Foundry
Classification: Geometric sans-serif

Free alternative to geometric sans-serifs like Circular. Clean, contemporary, with slightly softened geometric forms.

*Best for:* Startup contexts where you want geometric aesthetic without licensing cost.
*Watch for:* Less refined than commercial alternatives at some sizes.

**Poppins**
Designer: Indian Type Foundry
Classification: Geometric sans-serif

Cheerful geometric sans with slightly more personality than pure geometrics. Generous letter-spacing. Open-source.

*Best for:* Casual, friendly products. Consumer apps.
*Watch for:* Very widely used; ubiquity may be an issue.

## Serif Typefaces

### Old-Style Serifs

**EB Garamond**
Designer: Georg Mayr (digitization)
Classification: Old-style serif

Digital revival of Claude Garamond's 16th-century types. Classic, warm, literary. Open-source.

*Best for:* Long-form content, editorial, products aiming for warmth and trust.
*Watch for:* Relatively small x-height makes it less legible at small sizes on screens.

**Crimson Pro**
Designer: Jacques Le Bailly
Classification: Old-style serif

Contemporary old-style with better screen optimization than traditional Garamonds. Warmer than transitional serifs while maintaining readability.

*Best for:* Body text in editorial contexts where you want old-style warmth but need screen readability.
*Watch for:* Less character than historic revivals.

### Transitional Serifs

**Georgia**
Designer: Matthew Carter
Classification: Transitional serif

Designed specifically for screens in 1993 and still excellent. Tall x-height, sturdy serifs, reliable at all sizes. Ships with operating systems.

*Best for:* Body text anywhere. The safest serif choice for screens.
*Watch for:* Associations with older web design (though it's making a comeback).

**Libre Baskerville**
Designer: Impallari Type
Classification: Transitional serif

Web-optimized Baskerville. More contrast than Georgia, more personality than typical web serifs. Open-source.

*Best for:* When Georgia is too neutral and you want more classical elegance.
*Watch for:* Requires good contrast management; thin strokes can fade.

**IBM Plex Serif**
Designer: Mike Abbink
Classification: Transitional serif

Part of IBM's comprehensive type system. Designed for both digital and print. Pairs with IBM Plex Sans.

*Best for:* Products wanting a systematic serif that works across contexts.
*Watch for:* IBM brand associations (may or may not be desirable).

### Modern/Didone Serifs

**Playfair Display**
Designer: Claus Eggers Sørensen
Classification: Didone serif

High-contrast Didone with dramatic hairline strokes. Beautiful at large sizes; struggles at small sizes.

*Best for:* Headlines, display use, editorial emphasis.
*Watch for:* Display only. Do not use for body text.

### Slab Serifs

**Roboto Slab**
Designer: Christian Robertson
Classification: Slab serif

The slab companion to Roboto. Friendly, sturdy slab serif with good screen optimization. Open-source.

*Best for:* Adding warmth and solidity to interfaces already using Roboto.
*Watch for:* Best in the context of a Roboto-based design system.

**Rockwell**
Designer: Frank Hinman Pierpont
Classification: Slab serif (geometric)

The classic geometric slab serif. Bold, industrial, no-nonsense. Distinctive rectangular serifs.

*Best for:* When you want authority and solidity. Headlines with weight.
*Watch for:* Very strong personality. Dominates when present.

**Zilla Slab**
Designer: Typotheque
Classification: Slab serif (contemporary)

Mozilla's custom slab serif, now open-source. Softer than Rockwell, friendlier than traditional slabs.

*Best for:* Tech contexts wanting warmth without the industrial feel of classic slabs.
*Watch for:* Mozilla associations.

## Monospace Typefaces

**JetBrains Mono**
Designer: JetBrains
Classification: Monospace

Designed for code readability. Ligatures for programming symbols. Excellent for code and technical content.

*Best for:* Code samples, terminal interfaces, technical content.
*Watch for:* Ligatures can be polarizing; some developers prefer them off.

**Fira Code**
Designer: Nikita Prokopov
Classification: Monospace

Fira Mono with programming ligatures added. Popular in developer communities.

*Best for:* Code display where ligatures are appreciated.
*Watch for:* Very developer-y. May feel out of place in non-technical contexts.

**IBM Plex Mono**
Designer: Mike Abbink
Classification: Monospace

Part of IBM's type system. Cleaner than traditional monospace faces. Good for code and data.

*Best for:* Technical content in a refined context.
*Watch for:* IBM associations.

## Pairing Suggestions

**Inter + Merriweather:** Neutral sans heading, warm serif body. Good for editorial.

**Source Sans + Source Serif:** Same designer, designed to pair. Safe, cohesive.

**DM Sans + Crimson Pro:** Contemporary geometric with warm old-style. Friendly contrast.

**Poppins + Lora:** Geometric casual with transitional elegance. Consumer-friendly.

**IBM Plex Sans + IBM Plex Serif:** Systematic pair, designed together.

**Aktiv Grotesk + Georgia:** Refined neutral with classic serif. Professional.

---

## How to Use This Reference

This list is a starting point, not a prescription. When choosing typefaces:

1. Define the emotional register you need (warm, professional, playful, neutral)
2. Consult this list for candidates in that register
3. Test the candidates at actual sizes with actual content
4. Evaluate for both aesthetics and technical performance (loading, rendering)
5. Make a choice and commit

The best typeface for your project isn't necessarily the "best" typeface in abstract. It's the one that serves your context.
