# Appendix B: Recommended Reading and Resources

This appendix collects the most useful books, websites, and communities for continuing your education after finishing this book. The list is deliberately short. A curated selection of excellent resources is more valuable than a comprehensive list of everything available.

---

## Books (In Suggested Reading Order)

### 1. "Making Music: 74 Creative Strategies for Electronic Music Producers" -- Dennis DeSantis

This is the book to read first after finishing this one. DeSantis (who works at Ableton) addresses the creative and psychological challenges of music production with specific, actionable strategies. Stuck on an arrangement? There is a chapter for that. Cannot finish a track? There is a chapter for that. It is organized as a collection of independent strategies rather than a linear narrative, which makes it excellent for picking up whenever you hit a specific problem. Pair it with active production -- read a strategy, then immediately apply it in a session.

### 2. "Music Theory for Electronic Music Producers" -- J. Allen

This book covers music theory specifically through the lens of electronic music production, with examples in Ableton. It goes deeper than the theory covered in this book's Chapters 4 and 13, covering modes, advanced chord voicings, and harmonic analysis in a way that stays relevant to what you are actually doing in the DAW. If theory felt abstract in earlier chapters, Allen's approach may click because it is relentlessly practical.

### 3. "Dance Music Manual" (5th Edition) -- Rick Snoman

The most comprehensive single-volume reference on electronic music production. Snoman covers synthesis, sampling, mixing, mastering, arrangement, and genre-specific production techniques in exhaustive detail. The fifth edition is updated for current tools and practices. This is not a book to read cover to cover -- it is a reference you keep on your shelf and consult when you need deep information on a specific topic. Its strength is breadth and depth. Its weakness is that it can be overwhelming for beginners, which is why it is third on this list rather than first.

### 4. "Mixing Secrets for the Small Studio" -- Mike Senior

The best practical book on mixing for home studio environments. Senior addresses the reality that most of us are mixing in untreated rooms on imperfect monitors, and provides specific techniques for getting professional results despite those limitations. Covers EQ, compression, reverb, delay, automation, and monitoring in a way that is immediately applicable. Read this when you want to level up your mixing beyond what Chapters 9 and 15 of this book covered.

### 5. "Music Habits: The Mental Game of Electronic Music Production" -- Jason Timothy

Addresses the psychological dimension of music production head-on: motivation, creative blocks, perfectionism, comparison, and building sustainable habits. Timothy is an electronic music producer who writes from direct experience. The advice is specific and practical rather than generic motivational content. If the mindset sections of this book resonated with you, Timothy's book goes much deeper.

### 6. "The War of Art" -- Steven Pressfield

Not about music. Not about production. About Resistance, the internal force that prevents you from doing creative work. Pressfield names and dissects the ways we sabotage ourselves: procrastination, self-doubt, distraction, rationalization. It is a short, intense book that many creative professionals consider essential. Read it when you find yourself consistently avoiding the work you say you want to do.

## Free Online Resources

### Ableton Learning Music (learningmusic.ableton.com)

An interactive, browser-based introduction to music fundamentals: beats, notes, chords, basslines, melody, and song structure. No software required. Ableton made this and it is excellent. If you know someone who is curious about music production but not ready to commit to a DAW, point them here. If you worked through the early chapters of this book and want to reinforce the fundamentals in a different format, this is valuable.

### Ableton Learning Synths (learningsynths.ableton.com)

The synthesis companion to Learning Music. Interactive, browser-based exploration of oscillators, filters, envelopes, LFOs, and modulation. Hear each concept as you learn it. This covers similar ground to Chapter 5 of this book but in an interactive format where you manipulate parameters in real time. Excellent for reinforcement and for filling in any gaps.

### The Ableton Live Manual

The official manual for Ableton Live is one of the best DAW manuals in existence. It is clearly written, thoroughly illustrated, and covers every feature and device in detail. It is available online and within Ableton itself (select any device and press Cmd-? or the ? button). When you want to know exactly what a parameter does, the manual is the authoritative source. Get in the habit of consulting it before searching YouTube.

### Syntorial

Syntorial is a standalone application (not browser-based) that teaches synthesis through ear training. It plays you a sound and you try to recreate it by adjusting synth parameters. It starts simple and builds to complex patches. The ear-training approach is uniquely effective -- you learn to hear synthesis rather than just understand it intellectually. There is a free demo with the first several lessons. The full version is paid. It is worth the cost if synthesis is an area you want to develop seriously.

## Online Communities

### Reddit

- **r/edmproduction** -- General electronic music production discussion. Feedback threads, technique discussions, and gear recommendations. Quality varies, but the community is large and active.
- **r/ableton** -- Ableton-specific discussion. Good for troubleshooting, workflow tips, and staying current with updates.
- **r/synthesizers** -- Hardware and software synthesis. Leans toward hardware, but the sound design discussions are valuable regardless of your tools.

### Discord

Multiple production-focused Discord servers exist with active communities. Search for servers related to your specific genre or interest. The real-time format makes Discord better for quick questions and informal feedback than forums.

### Genre-Specific Forums

Depending on your genre, dedicated forums may exist with deep, focused communities. These tend to have higher signal-to-noise ratios than general production forums because the members share a specific interest and vocabulary.

## YouTube Channels Worth Following

This book deliberately avoided recommending YouTube during the learning process because the unstructured nature of video content can fragment your development. Now that you have a solid foundation, you can engage with YouTube content critically, evaluating advice against your own experience rather than accepting it uncritically.

Look for channels that explain the "why" behind techniques, not just the "how." Look for producers who demonstrate their process honestly, including mistakes and dead ends, rather than presenting polished tutorials that make everything look easy. Be wary of channels that focus primarily on gear and plugin reviews. These are entertainment, not education.

Specific channel recommendations become outdated quickly as creators come and go. Instead of naming channels, here is a filter: if a video teaches you something you immediately want to try in your next session, it was useful. If a video teaches you something that sounds impressive but you cannot imagine applying, it was entertainment. Both are fine. Know which is which.

## A Note on Paid Courses

The online education market for music production is enormous. Masterclasses, multi-week courses, mentorship programs, and subscription-based learning platforms all compete for your attention and money.

Some of these are excellent. Many are not. Before investing in a paid course, ask:

- Does this teach something I cannot learn from the free resources listed above?
- Is the instructor someone whose music I respect?
- Does the course structure match how I learn (project-based vs. lecture-based)?
- Are there reviews from people who completed the course and can speak to outcomes, not just the experience of watching the videos?

The most expensive course is not necessarily the best one. And no course is a substitute for the hours of actual production work that build real skill. A $500 course followed by no production time is worth less than a free YouTube video followed by three hours in the DAW.

Invest in courses strategically and sparingly. Your time in Ableton is the most valuable educational resource you have.
