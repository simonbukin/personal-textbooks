# Chapter 21: Making an Album

An album is not a playlist. A playlist is a collection of songs that happen to be grouped together. An album is a statement, a coherent artistic work where the whole is greater than the sum of its parts. The difference is intention. Every track on an album exists in relationship to every other track. The order matters. The transitions matter. The arc from first track to last track matters.

This chapter is about making that kind of album. Not as a distant goal, but as a project you start now, with a process that turns the abstract idea of "making an album" into a series of concrete decisions and actions.

---

## Pre-Production: Decide Before You Open the DAW

The biggest mistake you can make with an album is opening Ableton and hoping that an album emerges from enough individual sessions. It will not. What emerges from undirected production is a collection of unrelated tracks in different styles at different quality levels. That is not an album. That is a hard drive.

Before you open the DAW, make these decisions:

**Track count.** How many tracks will your album have? For your first album, aim for seven to ten. Fewer than seven feels insubstantial. More than ten requires a level of consistency and stamina that is extremely difficult on a first attempt. Pick a number and commit.

**Mood and theme.** Your album does not need a concept or a narrative. But it needs a mood. "Late-night melancholy." "Optimistic but restrained." "Industrial anxiety." "Summer mornings." Write it down in one phrase. This phrase is your creative compass. Every decision you make during production (sound selection, chord choices, tempo, effects, arrangement) should be tested against this phrase. Does this sound serve the mood? If not, it belongs on a different project.

**Tempo range.** An album with ten tracks all at 128 BPM is monotonous. An album that jumps from 70 to 174 to 90 to 160 is disorienting. Set a range. For example, 100-130 BPM. This gives you enough variety to keep things interesting while maintaining a sense of coherence. You might have one track that breaks the range intentionally, but the range should cover the majority.

**Key relationships.** You do not need to plan the key of every track in advance. But be aware of key relationships as you produce. An album where half the tracks are in A minor and the other half are in C major will feel harmonically flat. Vary your keys. Use the circle of fifths as a guide -- tracks in related keys (a fifth apart, a fourth apart, relative major/minor) will transition smoothly when sequenced next to each other.

**Defining sounds.** Choose two or three sonic elements that will recur across the album. Maybe a specific Drift patch that appears in different contexts. Maybe a particular reverb setting that creates a consistent spatial signature. Maybe a vinyl crackle layer or a field recording that appears in several tracks. These recurring elements create the sonic threads that make a collection of tracks feel like an album.

Write all of this down. Put it somewhere visible. This is your album brief. Refer to it every time you sit down to work.

## The Production Phase

With your brief defined, start producing tracks. Here are strategies for maintaining focus and momentum across an album-length project.

**Produce more than you need.** If your album is eight tracks, produce twelve to fourteen. This gives you the freedom to cut the weakest tracks without feeling like you are short on material. Some tracks will surprise you by being better than expected. Some will not work despite your best efforts. Having surplus material means you sequence from strength.

**Produce in batches.** Do not finish one track completely before starting the next. Instead, work in phases across all tracks. Spend two weeks generating ideas and sketching tracks. Then spend two weeks developing arrangements. Then mix. This approach has several advantages: it keeps the sonic palette consistent because you are making decisions with the whole album in mind, it prevents you from over-investing in a single track before knowing whether it fits, and it lets your ears stay fresh by rotating between tracks.

**Test for cohesion regularly.** Every week or two, export rough bounces of all your in-progress tracks and put them in a playlist. Listen to them as a sequence. Do they feel like they belong together? Is one track obviously from a different universe? Is the energy too uniform? This regular check prevents you from drifting away from your album brief and catching the problem only after months of work.

**Kill your darlings.** You will produce tracks that you love individually but that do not serve the album. This is one of the hardest things in music. A great track that breaks the album's cohesion makes the album worse. Save it for another release. The album is the unit, not the track.

**Maintain a session log.** Keep a simple text document where you note what you worked on each session, what decisions you made, and what you want to do next time. This takes two minutes and saves you the ten minutes of "where was I?" that happens at the start of every session otherwise. It also creates a record of the project that you will value later.

## Sequencing: Track Order as Composition

Sequencing (deciding the order of tracks on an album) is a compositional act. The order creates an arc, a flow of energy and emotion that the listener experiences across thirty to sixty minutes. Bad sequencing can make a collection of great tracks feel exhausting or aimless. Good sequencing can elevate a collection of good tracks into something that feels significant.

**The opener sets the tone.** Your first track tells the listener what kind of experience they are about to have. It does not need to be the best track or the most energetic. It needs to be the most inviting. An opener should welcome the listener into the album's world. A short, atmospheric track works well. So does something mid-tempo that establishes the mood without overwhelming.

**Avoid stacking intensity.** Do not put three high-energy tracks in a row. Intensity is relative. A track feels energetic partly because of what came before it. After two intense tracks, the third feels fatiguing rather than exciting. Follow a peak with a valley. Let the listener breathe. The contrast makes both the peaks and valleys more powerful.

**Build toward the middle.** Many strong albums place their most intense or ambitious tracks around the two-thirds mark. The first third establishes the world. The middle third explores and pushes. The final third resolves. This is not a rule, but it is a pattern that works because it mirrors narrative structure.

**The closer provides resolution.** Your last track is the listener's final impression. It should feel like an ending. Not necessarily quiet or slow, but complete. Many great albums end with something contemplative, offering a sense of arrival or reflection after the journey. Avoid ending on your most intense track. That leaves the listener amped up with nowhere to go.

**Key and tempo transitions.** Adjacent tracks should not clash harmonically. If track three ends in B minor and track four begins in B-flat major, the transition will feel jarring. Use the circle of fifths: tracks in the same key, the relative major/minor, or a fifth apart will transition smoothly.

Similarly, tempo transitions should be gradual. Going from 120 BPM to 125 BPM feels natural. Going from 90 to 160 feels like a different album started. If you have a large tempo jump, put a transitional moment between them -- an ambient interlude, a spoken word piece, a field recording. Give the listener a bridge.

**Use interludes sparingly.** Short interludes (thirty seconds to a minute) can serve as palette cleansers between sections of the album. But too many interludes make the album feel padded. One or two is usually enough. And an interlude should add something (an atmosphere, a texture, a moment) rather than just fill space.

**Test your sequence.** Once you have a proposed order, listen to the album from beginning to end without stopping. Not while cooking or driving. Sit down, put on headphones, and listen. Does the flow feel natural? Are there moments where your attention drops? Are there jarring transitions? Note what does not work and resequence. Do this at least three times over the course of a week, adjusting each time.

## Cohesion Without Monotony

This is the central challenge of album-making: how to make ten tracks sound like they belong together without making them sound like the same track ten times. The solution is to share some elements while varying others.

**Elements that create cohesion (keep consistent):**

- **Reverb character.** Use the same reverb (or the same type of reverb with similar settings) across all tracks. This creates a consistent spatial signature, a sense that all the tracks exist in the same "room" or "world." It does not mean identical settings on every track. It means the same reverb personality.

- **A recurring sound.** A specific sample, a particular synth patch, a vocal phrase, a percussive hit. Thread it through the album in different contexts: as a background element in one track, a lead element in another, a rhythmic element in a third. The listener may not consciously notice it, but their subconscious registers the connection.

- **Mix approach.** Consistent low-end treatment, similar vocal processing if you use vocals, a consistent dynamic range across tracks. These create a sonic DNA that ties the album together.

- **Sonic palette.** If your album uses warm analog-style synths, do not suddenly introduce a digital, harsh wavetable lead in one track. Stay within a general timbral family.

**Elements that create variety (change between tracks):**

- **Tempo.** Vary it within your defined range. Two tracks at the same tempo back-to-back is fine. Five is not.

- **Key.** Move around the circle of fifths. Different keys create different emotional colors even when everything else is similar.

- **Rhythmic approach.** Some tracks can be four-on-the-floor. Some can be breakbeat. Some can have minimal, spacious rhythms. Rhythmic variety is one of the most effective ways to differentiate tracks.

- **Energy level.** Alternate between high and low energy. Give the album dynamic range at the macro level.

- **Arrangement length.** Not every track needs to be five minutes. Having a mix of three-minute, five-minute, and seven-minute tracks creates variety in pacing.

- **Featured elements.** One track foregrounds a vocal sample. Another foregrounds a synth lead. Another is percussion-driven. Each track should have a clear identity, a thing that makes it itself, within the album's shared aesthetic.

The analogy is a clothing collection. Every piece belongs to the same brand, shares a color palette and material quality, but each piece is a different garment. You would not wear the same outfit every day, but everything in your wardrobe should look like it came from the same closet.

## Case Study: Four Tet -- "New Energy"

Four Tet's "New Energy" (2017) is a masterclass in album cohesion that spans a wide range of tempos, moods, and approaches. Understanding how Kieran Hebden pulls this off will inform your own album-making process.

**Sonic palette as unifier.** "New Energy" includes delicate ambient pieces, driving four-on-the-floor tracks, and hip-hop-influenced beats. On paper, these should not cohere. They cohere because Hebden processes everything through a consistent sonic lens. Acoustic recordings -- bells, strings, harps, vocal samples -- are treated with similar processing: granular textures, gentle digital artifacts, warm but precise EQ. Whether a track is at 80 BPM or 130 BPM, it sounds like it was made in the same studio by the same person with the same aesthetic priorities.

This is the deepest form of cohesion. It is not about using the same synth patch on every track. It is about having a consistent approach to how sound is treated. Hebden's signature involves sampling acoustic and organic sounds and recontextualizing them in electronic arrangements. A harp becomes a rhythmic loop. A vocal phrase becomes a texture. Church bells become a melodic motif. The source material varies, but the processing philosophy stays consistent.

**Sequencing for emotional arc.** "New Energy" opens with "Alap," a meditative, ambient piece that immediately establishes the album's contemplative character. It does not open with a banger. It opens with an invitation. The album then moves through varying energy levels -- "Two Thousand and Seventeen" is a slow-building, deep track; "SW9 9SL" and "Planet" are more uptempo and driving; "Lush" is lush and midtempo. The closer, "South London Boroughs," is a gentle, almost hymn-like piece that resolves the album into stillness.

The sequencing follows the principle of contrast and flow. High-energy tracks are followed by breathing room. Intense moments earn their impact because of the quiet that preceded them.

**Acoustic sampling as sonic DNA.** The consistent use of acoustic samples (real instruments, found sounds, environmental recordings) gives the album a warmth and humanity that purely synthetic production would lack. Even on the most driving, club-oriented tracks, there is an organic quality. This is the album's DNA: electronic structures housing organic sounds.

For your own album, identify what your DNA is. It might be a specific synthesis technique, a sampling approach, a particular effects chain, or a sonic reference. Whatever it is, let it run through every track.

**Diversity within unity.** "New Energy" includes tracks you might dance to and tracks you might meditate to. But it never feels like a compilation. The lesson is that diversity and cohesion are not opposites. You create cohesion through the elements you keep consistent (sonic palette, processing approach, spatial character) and create interest through the elements you vary (tempo, rhythm, energy, featured instruments).

## The Finishing Process

At some point, you have to stop producing and start finishing.

**Lock the tracklist.** Decide which tracks are on the album and in what order. Once you lock it, do not add new tracks. If inspiration strikes, save it for the next project. Scope creep is the enemy of completion.

**Mix with the album in mind.** When mixing each track, listen to the previous track and the next track in the sequence. The transitions between tracks matter. If track three ends with a long reverb tail, track four might begin with space or silence to let that tail breathe. If track five is very bass-heavy, track six might start with something in the upper frequencies to create contrast.

**Master for consistency.** When mastering your album, the priority is consistency. All tracks should be at similar loudness levels (within 1-2 LUFS). The tonal balance should be consistent; one track should not be drastically brighter or darker than the others. This does not mean they all sound the same. It means they sound like they belong together.

Listen to the complete album after mastering. Pay attention to the transitions between tracks. Adjust spacing (the silence between tracks) until the flow feels right. Some transitions want two seconds of silence. Some want four. Some want a crossfade. The right gap is the one that feels natural.

**Set a deadline and ship it.** An album is never perfect. At some point, it is done enough. Set a release date and work backward from it. When the date arrives, release it. The next album will be better. But it will only exist if you finish this one first.

## Exercises

1. Write an album brief. Define your track count, mood phrase, tempo range, key relationships, and two to three defining sounds. Pin it next to your screen.

2. Produce three rough sketches in one week, all guided by your album brief. Export rough bounces and listen to them in sequence. Do they feel like they could live on the same album?

3. Take an existing collection of your finished tracks (at least four) and practice sequencing them. Try three different orders. Listen to each from start to finish. Which order tells the best story? Why?

4. Analyze the sequencing of an album you love. Write down the tempo, key, energy level, and mood of each track. Chart the energy arc across the album. Where are the peaks and valleys? How does the album manage transitions?

5. Set a deadline three months from today. Produce and release your first album by that date. Write the date down somewhere you will see it every day.
