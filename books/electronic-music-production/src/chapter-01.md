# Chapter 1: Make Sound Immediately

Open Ableton Live 12. If this is your first time launching it, close whatever demo set or tutorial popup appears. Go to File > New Live Set, or hit Cmd-N. You are looking at an empty session. It might look overwhelming. It is not. You need about 10% of what is on screen right now, and I am going to show you exactly which 10%.

Within the next few minutes, you will have drums playing. Within the hour, you will have an 8-bar loop with drums, bass, and a melody. By the end, you will export it as an audio file. That file will not be good. It will be yours, and it will be real, and it will be more than most aspiring producers ever actually finish.

---

## Your First Sounds: Five Minutes or Less

You should be in Session View. This is the grid of empty rectangular slots you see in the middle of the screen. If you see a horizontal timeline instead, press Tab to switch. Session View is where we start because it is designed for experimentation. You trigger loops and ideas without worrying about linear arrangement.

Look at the left side of the screen. There is a sidebar called the Browser. If you do not see it, press Cmd-Option-B to toggle it. In the Browser, you will see categories: Sounds, Drums, Instruments, Audio Effects, MIDI Effects, and more.

Click on **Drums**. You will see a list of drum rack presets. Pick any one you like the look of. It does not matter which. I would suggest something from the "Kit" subcategory -- "Kit-606 Core" or "Kit-808 Core" are solid starting points. Drag it into the empty area where tracks live. Ableton creates a new MIDI track with that Drum Rack loaded automatically.

Now look at the bottom of the screen. You should see the Drum Rack device with a grid of pads. If you do not see it, click on the track's title bar and make sure the device view is open (press Cmd-Option-L if needed, or just click the small toggle button at the bottom-left of the track).

Press the letter **M** on your keyboard. This activates Computer MIDI Keyboard, which maps your QWERTY keyboard to MIDI notes. Now press some keys in the middle row of your keyboard -- A, S, D, F, and so on. You should hear drum sounds. The bottom row maps to the lower pads of the drum rack, the middle row to the upper pads.

You are making sound. That is step one, done.

## Recording Your First Pattern

Double-click on one of the empty clip slots in your Drum Rack track. This creates an empty MIDI clip and opens the piano roll editor at the bottom of the screen. You will see a grid with pitch on the vertical axis and time on the horizontal axis.

But there is a faster way to start. Close that empty clip by pressing Cmd-Z. Instead, we are going to record in real time.

Make sure Computer MIDI Keyboard is still on (M key, or check the small keyboard icon in the upper-right area of the screen). Set your tempo -- the number in the top-left of the screen that probably reads 120. Leave it at 120 for now, or change it to whatever feels right. Click the metronome icon next to the tempo to turn it on so you can hear a click.

Click the record button in one of the empty clip slots of your drum track (the circle icon that appears when you hover over a slot), or simply arm the track by clicking the Arm button (the circle on the track itself), then click the Session Record button in the transport bar. Ableton counts in, then starts recording. Play a simple pattern on your keyboard. Kick on beats one and three, snare on two and four. Do not worry about timing. Keep it simple. Let it loop for one or two passes, then press the spacebar to stop.

You now have a recorded MIDI clip. Double-click on it to see your notes in the piano roll. They will probably not be perfectly on the grid. That is fine. Right-click in the piano roll, choose "Quantize" or press Cmd-U. Your notes snap to the grid. Play it back with spacebar. You have a drum pattern.

## Adding Drift: Your First Synth

Go back to the Browser. This time, click on **Instruments**. Find **Drift**. It is Ableton's streamlined subtractive synthesizer, and it is the best stock synth for learning because it has a clear, limited interface where every parameter does something audible and nothing is hidden behind menus.

Drag the Drift preset called "Init" or any basic preset into an empty area to create a new MIDI track. If you cannot find an "Init" preset, just drag the Drift instrument itself. It loads with a default sound.

Arm this new track (click its record arm button). Make sure the first track is disarmed so you do not trigger drums and synth at the same time. With Computer MIDI Keyboard still on, play some notes. You should hear a basic synth tone.

Now do the same thing you did with drums. Record a simple bassline or melody into a clip slot. Two bars is plenty. Quantize it. Play both clips at the same time by clicking the play button on each clip (the triangle). You have drums and a synth playing together. This is a loop. This is how all electronic music starts.

## The Interface: Only What You Need Right Now

Ableton has a deep and powerful interface. You do not need most of it yet. What matters right now:

**Session View vs Arrangement View.** Press Tab to switch between them. Session View is the grid of clips. Arrangement View is the horizontal timeline. For now, stay in Session View. We will move to Arrangement View when we start building full tracks in later chapters.

**Tracks.** Each vertical column in Session View is a track. MIDI tracks hold virtual instruments (like Drum Rack and Drift). Audio tracks hold recorded or imported audio. You create new tracks by right-clicking in the empty space to the right of existing tracks, or with Cmd-T (MIDI) and Cmd-Option-T (audio).

**Clips.** Each rectangle in the grid is a clip slot. A clip is a chunk of music, either a MIDI pattern or an audio recording. You trigger clips by clicking their play buttons. Clips in the same row (called a Scene) can be triggered simultaneously by clicking the scene's play button on the right side.

**The Browser.** The left sidebar where you find instruments, effects, sounds, and samples. Learn to search it by typing in the search bar at the top. Searching "drift" will find all Drift presets instantly.

**The Detail View.** The bottom panel shows whatever you have selected -- a device's controls, a clip's MIDI notes, or a clip's audio waveform. If it disappears, press Cmd-Option-L or click the small triangle at the bottom-left of the interface.

**Transport Controls.** Play, stop, and record buttons at the top. But honestly, use the keyboard instead: spacebar for play/stop, and the record buttons in the clip slots for recording.

That is it. That is all the interface knowledge you need for this chapter. We will unlock more as we need it.

## Five Keyboard Shortcuts That Change Everything

Learn these now. Use them constantly. They will save you hundreds of hours over your production career.

**Tab** -- Switch between Session View and Arrangement View. You will use this constantly once we start arranging.

**Cmd-Z** -- Undo. The most important shortcut in any creative software. Ableton's undo history is deep. Use it freely. Try things, undo them, try other things. There is no cost to experimentation.

**Spacebar** -- Play and stop. You already know this one, but I list it because some beginners reach for the mouse to hit play. Do not. Spacebar. Always.

**Cmd-D** -- Duplicate. Select a clip and press Cmd-D to duplicate it to the next slot. Select notes in the piano roll and Cmd-D duplicates them. Select a track and Cmd-D duplicates the whole track. This shortcut is how you build things quickly.

**Cmd-Shift-T** -- Insert MIDI track. Adds a new MIDI track to your set instantly. Faster than using the Create menu.

Five shortcuts. That is all. You will learn more over time, but these five will handle 80% of your navigation needs for the first several chapters.

## Capture MIDI: The Best Feature You Do Not Know About

This one gets its own section because it is game-changing and most beginners do not discover it for months.

The scenario: you are noodling on your keyboard, not recording, just playing around. You play something great. A melody, a chord progression, a bassline that just feels right. You were not recording. It is gone.

Except it is not. Ableton has a feature called Capture MIDI. Press **Cmd-Shift-C** (or click the Capture MIDI button in the transport bar -- it looks like a circle with a line). Ableton retroactively captures what you just played and creates a clip from it. It even figures out the correct tempo and loop length.

This is not a gimmick. It changes how you interact with the instrument. You can play freely without the pressure of the record button. If something good happens, you capture it. If not, you keep playing. It removes the performance anxiety of recording and lets you stay in a creative flow state.

Use it. Make it a habit. Some of your best ideas will come from noodling, and Capture MIDI makes sure you never lose them.

## MIDI Controllers: Helpful but Not Required

Everything in this book can be done with your computer keyboard and mouse. A MIDI controller (a keyboard with piano-style keys that connects via USB) is nice to have, but it is not necessary and you should not let the lack of one stop you from working.

If you do want to get one, here are practical recommendations for beginners:

A 25-key controller is the sweet spot for starting out. It is small enough to fit on a desk next to your laptop and has enough range to play basslines and melodies comfortably. The Arturia MiniLab 3 and the Novation Launchkey Mini are both solid choices in the $100 range. Either one integrates well with Ableton out of the box.

If you have more desk space and budget, a 49-key controller gives you a more comfortable playing range and is better for learning piano-style playing. The Novation Launchkey 49 or Arturia KeyLab Essential 49 are good mid-range options.

Do not buy anything bigger than 49 keys unless you are an experienced keyboard player. Do not buy a controller with lots of knobs and faders you do not understand yet. Start simple. Upgrade when you know what you need.

The most important thing: do not research MIDI controllers for three weeks instead of making music. If you do not have one, use your computer keyboard. The M key activates it. It works. Make music now, shop later.

## Permission to Be Bad

This section is short and important.

Your first productions will not sound good. Not compared to the music you listen to and love, and not compared to what you imagine in your head. This is normal. This is universal. Every producer you admire went through this phase.

Ira Glass described this as the "taste gap." You got into music because you have good taste. You can hear what makes a great track great. But your skills have not caught up to your taste yet, so everything you make falls short of your own standards. The gap between what you can hear and what you can make is real, and it closes only with practice.

The dangerous response to the taste gap is to stop making things. To retreat into tutorials and theory and gear research, because learning feels safe and making feels vulnerable. Resist this. The gap closes by making things, not by learning about making things.

Your job right now is not to make good music. Your job is to make music. Volume, not quality. Finish things. Export them. Move on to the next one. Quality emerges from quantity. Not immediately, not linearly, but inevitably if you keep going.

I am not asking you to lower your standards permanently. I am asking you to lower them temporarily and strategically so that you can build the skills that will eventually meet those standards.

## Building the 8-Bar Loop: Your First Milestone

Time to put it together. You are making an 8-bar loop with three elements. Kick, snare, and hi-hats from Drum Rack. A bass part from Drift. And one more melodic or textural element, also from Drift.

This is your first milestone because it represents the seed of a real track. Most electronic music is built from loops that get arranged, layered, and developed. The loop is the fundamental unit of electronic music production, and by the end of this exercise, you will have made one.

**Step 1: The drum pattern.** If you have not already, set up a Drum Rack track with a kit you like. Record or draw in a pattern. Keep it simple: kick on beats 1 and 3, snare or clap on 2 and 4, hi-hats on every eighth note. That is a perfectly functional dance music foundation. Set the clip length to 2 bars, then duplicate it to fill 8 bars, or just let it loop.

To draw notes instead of recording them, double-click an empty clip slot to create a clip, then use the pencil tool (press B to toggle Draw Mode) to click notes into the piano roll. The rows correspond to drum pads. Drawing is often more precise than recording for drums.

**Step 2: The bass.** Create a new MIDI track with Drift. Find a preset that sounds like a bass, or start from the default and lower the oscillator's octave (we will get into sound design in the next chapter). Record or draw a simple bassline, even just root notes on beat 1 of each bar is fine. Keep it in the lower register. Four to eight notes total across your 2 bars.

A practical tip: if you are using Computer MIDI Keyboard, press Z and X to shift the octave range down and up. You want to be in a low octave for bass -- around C1 to C2.

**Step 3: The melodic element.** Create another MIDI track with a second instance of Drift. Choose a different preset (a pad, a pluck, a lead, anything that contrasts with the bass). Record or draw a simple melody or chord pattern. It does not need to be complex. Two or three notes that sound good together over your bass line.

If you do not know what notes to use, here is a cheat: stick to the white keys only, and start from A. The notes A, C, D, E, G make up the A minor pentatonic scale, and almost anything you play using only those notes will sound reasonable together. We will cover theory properly in Chapter 4. For now, this gets you moving.

**Step 4: Listen and adjust.** Play all three clips together. Listen. Does anything feel off? Is the bass too loud or too quiet relative to the drums? Adjust the track volume faders on the right side of each track. Is the melody clashing with the bass? Try different notes. Is the whole thing too busy? Remove some notes. Less is almost always more when you are starting out.

**Step 5: Export.** This step is important because it means declaring the loop done. Not perfect. Done.

Switch to Arrangement View (Tab). Select all your clips in Session View and drag them to the arrangement, or record your session into the arrangement by pressing the Arrangement Record button in the transport bar and then triggering your clips. In Arrangement View, you should see your clips laid out on a timeline.

Select the region you want to export by clicking and dragging in the timeline ruler at the top to highlight your 8 bars. Go to File > Export Audio/Video, or press Cmd-Shift-R. In the export dialog, the defaults are fine for now: WAV format, 16-bit, 44100 Hz. Choose a location, name your file, and hit Export.

You have a WAV file. You have produced a piece of music. It is short, it is rough, and it is a real thing that exists in the world that did not exist before you made it.

## Case Study: Examining a Simple Loop

Listen to "Stranger Things" by Kygo (the main synth riff that opens the track), or the intro of "Midnight City" by M83, or the opening bars of any electronic track you love. What you will notice is that many iconic moments in electronic music are, at their core, simple loops. A drum pattern, a bass part, and one melodic idea. The magic is not in complexity. It is in the right sounds, playing the right notes, at the right time.

When you listen to a track you admire, try to identify the loop at its core. Strip away the production polish, the layering, the effects. What are the three or four elements that define the track? A kick pattern. A bass sound. A lead melody or chord progression. A rhythmic texture. That is what you just made. The difference between your loop and a professional track is not the number of elements; it is refinement, arrangement, and mixing, all of which we will cover in the coming chapters.

## What You Accomplished and What Comes Next

In this chapter, you went from an empty Ableton session to a finished audio file. You learned the minimum viable interface knowledge to navigate Session View, record clips, and export audio. You met Drum Rack and Drift, the two instruments that will carry you through Phase 1. You learned five keyboard shortcuts that will serve you for your entire production career. You learned about Capture MIDI, a feature that will save your best accidental ideas. And you confronted the taste gap head-on.

In the next chapter, we go deeper into Drum Rack and Drift. Not as abstract instruments to study, but as tools to make specific sounds. You will learn what every relevant knob and slider does by turning it and listening. You will build a kick drum pattern, design a bass sound from scratch, create a pad, and craft a lead. All from initialized presets, all by ear.

But first: make another loop. Right now, before you move on. Different sounds, different tempo, different vibe. The exercise above was structured. This one is not. Just make something. Export it. The habit of finishing and moving on is the most important skill this book will teach you, and you should start building it immediately.
