# SPEC.md — *Minds in the World: A Practical Introduction to Cognitive Science*

---

## 1. Project Overview

**Working Title:** *Minds in the World: A Practical Introduction to Cognitive Science*

**Tagline:** What we know about how we think — and how honest we should be about what we don't.

**Core Purpose:** Bring a complete beginner from zero background to genuine, working competence in cognitive science — understanding its core findings, its methods, its live debates, and its limits. This is not a survey course that name-drops fields. It is a curriculum designed to make the reader *think like a cognitive scientist*.

**Target Audience:** Intelligent adults with no prior background in cognitive science, psychology, or neuroscience. This includes curious generalists, professionals who interact with human cognition daily (designers, educators, clinicians, engineers, managers), and early undergraduates deciding whether to pursue the field.

**Format:** Book-length curriculum (~80,000–100,000 words), structured as a self-contained textbook with integrated exercises, case studies, and annotated primary literature.

## 2. The Problem with Existing Resources

A thorough review of current options reveals a consistent set of failure modes. Understanding them is essential to avoiding them.

### 2a. The Bestseller Problem
The most widely read books on cognition — *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Kahneman), *How the Mind Works* (Pinker), *Incognito* (Eagleman) — are brilliant but are *arguments*, not curricula. They advocate specific frameworks and tend to skip rival accounts, methodological caveats, and failed replications. A reader finishes them feeling informed but lacking the conceptual toolkit to evaluate any new claim they encounter. They also predate or underplay the severity of the replication crisis in cognitive and social psychology (~50% of cognitive psychology studies have failed to replicate in large-scale projects).

### 2b. The Standard Textbook Problem
Academic introductory textbooks — Eysenck & Keane's *Cognitive Psychology*, Sternberg's *Cognitive Psychology*, Bermúdez's *Cognitive Science* (Cambridge, 4th ed., 2022) — are admirably comprehensive but built for classroom adoption, not self-directed learning. They suffer from:

- **Box-checking coverage** over genuine insight: the reader learns the names of models without understanding why they were proposed or what evidence genuinely supports them.
- **The myth of settled science**: findings are typically presented as established fact without flagging replication status, effect sizes, or ongoing debate.
- **Jargon before concept**: terms like "phonological loop," "forward model," and "spreading activation" appear before the reader has any felt sense of the *problem* each term is meant to solve.
- **Absence of the replication crisis**: most major textbooks still present social priming, ego depletion, and stereotype threat as robust findings despite repeated failures to replicate. This constitutes intellectual dishonesty by omission.
- **No primary literature integration**: students are told *about* papers but are rarely walked through the actual reasoning of a landmark study.

### 2c. The Interdisciplinary Scatter Problem
Cognitive science is explicitly interdisciplinary — drawing on psychology, neuroscience, linguistics, philosophy of mind, computer science, and anthropology. Most resources either collapse this into a single story (usually cognitive psychology with a neuroscience gloss) or overwhelm the beginner with the full panorama of six disciplines simultaneously. Neither approach produces understanding. The reader needs a stable home discipline (experimental cognitive psychology) with well-mapped excursions into adjacent fields.

### 2d. The No-Stakes-No-Application Problem
Many introductory resources treat cognitive science as purely theoretical. They don't answer the reader's implicit question: *Why does this matter?* How does understanding working memory change how I study, teach, or design? How should knowing about inattentional blindness change how I drive or write error-checking procedures? This book must answer those questions throughout, not in a separate "applications" chapter at the end.

### 2e. The Epistemic Cowardice Problem
Too many textbooks hedge with "some researchers argue" or "studies suggest" without ever telling the reader *how confident* they should be in a finding, *why* that confidence is warranted, or *what it would take* to change our minds. This trains passive consumers of science rather than critical thinkers. This curriculum takes a firm stance: the reader deserves to know what we know, what we think we know, what we're genuinely uncertain about, and what popular accounts have gotten wrong.

## 3. Design Principles

### Principle 1: Honesty About Epistemic Status
Every major claim is tagged with an epistemic flag — not pedantically, but naturally woven into prose. The reader will learn to distinguish:
- **Robust findings** (replicated across labs, large effect sizes, convergent methods)
- **Well-supported but contested findings** (replication record mixed; active methodological debate)
- **Intriguing but fragile findings** (compelling single study; failed or partial replications)
- **Discredited findings still in popular circulation** (e.g., most "brain training" claims, ego depletion in its strong form, many priming effects)

### Principle 2: Problem Before Concept
Every technical concept is introduced by first establishing the *problem* it solves. Working memory is not defined and then explained — the reader is first confronted with the question: *Why can you hold a phone number in your head for ten seconds but not ten minutes, and why does doing mental arithmetic make you forget it?* The concept emerges as the answer.

### Principle 3: Primary Literature as Evidence
Each unit includes at least one landmark study read *in detail* — not summarized, but examined: What was the question? What did they manipulate? What did they measure? What did they find? What are the caveats? This trains the reader in the norms of scientific reasoning and gives them the experience of following a real argument.

**Landmark papers used throughout the curriculum include:**
- Miller, G.A. (1956). "The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two." *Psychological Review.* (Working memory capacity)
- Broadbent, D. (1958). *Perception and Communication.* (Early attention research)
- Cherry, E.C. (1953). "Some experiments on the recognition of speech with one and two ears." *JASA.* (Cocktail party effect)
- Tversky, A. & Kahneman, D. (1974). "Judgment under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases." *Science.* (Decision-making)
- Sperling, G. (1960). "The information available in brief visual presentations." *Psychological Monographs.* (Iconic memory)
- Loftus, E. & Palmer, J. (1974). "Reconstruction of automobile destruction." *JEPG.* (Memory as reconstruction)
- Shepard, R. & Metzler, J. (1971). "Mental rotation of three-dimensional objects." *Science.* (Mental imagery)
- Open Science Collaboration (2015). "Estimating the reproducibility of psychological science." *Science.* (Replication crisis)
- Simons, D. & Chabris, C. (1999). "Gorillas in our midst." *Perception.* (Inattentional blindness)
- Tulving, E. (1972). "Episodic and semantic memory." *Organization of Memory.* (Memory systems)
- Fodor, J. (1983). *The Modularity of Mind.* (Architecture of mind)
- Sacks, O. Clinical cases throughout (phenomenology of cognitive breakdown)
- O'Keefe, J. & Dostrovsky, J. (1971). "The hippocampus as a spatial map." *Brain Research.* (Place cells and spatial cognition)
- Rosch, E. (1975). "Cognitive representations of semantic categories." *JEPG.* (Prototype theory and categorization)
- Stickgold, R. (2005). "Sleep-dependent memory consolidation." *Nature.* (Sleep and memory)
- Firestone, C. & Scholl, B.J. (2016). "Cognition does not affect perception." *BBS.* (Perception/cognition boundary)
- Mahowald, K., Ivanova, A.A., et al. (2024). "Dissociating language and thought in large language models." *Trends in Cognitive Sciences.* (AI and cognitive science)
- Yarkoni, T. (2020). "The generalizability crisis." *BBS.* (Methodology and ecological validity)

### Principle 4: Cases and Examples Earn Their Place
Every case study and real-world example must directly illuminate a concept. No "interesting anecdotes" that merely decorate the page. Clinical neuropsychology cases (Phineas Gage, HM, patient DF) are used not as curiosities but as natural experiments that reveal cognitive architecture. Everyday examples (GPS navigation and spatial memory degradation, multitasking myths, eyewitness testimony failures) are used to show that understanding cognition has real stakes.

### Principle 5: The Reader Builds a Model
The curriculum is structured so that each unit builds on previous ones. The reader is building a model of the mind as they go. By the end, they have a working architecture: perception → attention → working memory → long-term memory → language → reasoning & decision-making → executive function → consciousness (contested) — plus an honest map of what this architecture doesn't explain.

### Principle 6: Intellectual Courage
The book will not shy away from disagreements. Where experts disagree — on the architecture of long-term memory, on whether consciousness has a neural correlate that we can meaningfully measure, on the validity of dual-process theory — the curriculum presents the disagreement clearly and teaches the reader how to reason about it, rather than choosing a side for the sake of narrative tidiness.

## 4. Structural Overview

The curriculum is organized into **six Parts**, each containing **3–7 Chapters**, for a total of **26 Chapters**.

**Part I: Orientation** — What cognitive science is, how we study minds, why it's hard, what it has and hasn't delivered.

**Part II: Perception, Action, and Attention** — How sensory information is selected and processed, how motor plans are formed and executed, and how action and perception form a continuous loop. Includes spatial cognition and navigation.

**Part III: Memory** — From iconic memory to autobiographical memory; how remembering works, why it fails, and how sleep shapes what sticks.

**Part IV: Language and Meaning** — How language is processed and produced (including reading), how it develops, and how it shapes (or doesn't shape) thought.

**Part V: Reasoning, Decision-Making, and Executive Control** — Concepts and categories, the machinery of deliberate thought, how it fails, and what the dual-process framework does and doesn't explain. Includes expertise and skill acquisition.

**Part VI: Bigger Questions** — Consciousness, emotion, social cognition, development across the full lifespan, embodied cognition, and the honest limits of what we know — including what AI reveals about the mind.

**Cross-cutting theme: Individual Differences and Neurodiversity.** Rather than treating cognition as a single normative profile, the book weaves individual variation — in attention (ADHD), perception (synesthesia, aphantasia), memory, executive function, and beyond — into each relevant chapter. Neurodiversity is not pathology to be explained away; it is data about the architecture of cognition.

## 5. Pedagogy

### 5a. Chapter Structure
Each chapter follows a consistent format:
1. **Opening Problem** — A puzzle, case, or phenomenon that motivates the chapter
2. **Core Concepts** — Carefully sequenced conceptual development
3. **Primary Text Spotlight** — A landmark paper examined in detail
4. **What This Gets Right / What's Still Open** — Epistemic summary of the field's current confidence
5. **Real-World Application** — A direct, non-superficial application of the chapter's concepts
6. **Chapter Review** — Key terms defined in context, not in isolation
7. **Further Reading** — 3–5 curated sources with brief rationale for each

### 5b. Exercises
Each chapter includes:
- **Introspection exercises** (controlled first-person investigation of the chapter's topic)
- **Design challenges** (how would you test this? what would falsify it?)
- **Reading a paper** prompts (guided questions to apply to a provided abstract or excerpt)

### 5c. Tone
- Conversational but precise. Written for an intelligent adult, not a student seeking a grade.
- Uses "we" to mean "cognitive scientists" and "you" to mean "the reader engaging with this question."
- Actively models uncertainty: "We don't know" and "This is contested" are not failures — they are the honest state of a young science.
- Never condescending. Does not over-explain everyday concepts; does explain technical ones.

## 6. What This Book is Not

- **Not a self-help book.** It will not tell you how to hack your brain or become a better thinker in 10 steps. It will give you an accurate model of how cognition works, and leave applications to your judgment.
- **Not a neuroscience textbook.** Neuroscience is used where it genuinely illuminates cognitive architecture (e.g., HM's hippocampal damage and episodic memory); not as a default explanation or decorative backdrop.
- **Not a philosophy-of-mind textbook.** Philosophy is engaged where it bears on live empirical questions (e.g., consciousness, representation, the boundaries of cognition). It is not surveyed for its own sake.
- **Not an AI/cognitive modeling textbook.** Computational models appear where they help clarify cognitive architecture (e.g., connectionism, Bayesian brain). They are not the central framework.

---

## 7. Honest Limitations of This Curriculum

This curriculum reflects cognitive science as practiced primarily in the Western, WEIRD (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic) research tradition. Cross-cultural cognitive science is included but cannot be treated exhaustively at introductory level. The reader should be aware that:

- The majority of foundational studies used subjects from WEIRD populations, often undergraduate psychology students, and generalizability is genuinely contested.
- Cognitive science has historically underweighted embodied, situated, and enactive approaches. This curriculum takes these seriously as live research programs, not fringe alternatives.
- The field's relationship to neuroscience is complex: brain imaging findings are included but treated critically (the "voodoo correlations" debate, the limits of reverse inference from fMRI, etc.).

---

## 8. Authorial Voice

The authorial voice is that of an honest guide: deeply familiar with the field, unafraid to flag uncertainty, genuinely excited by good science, and committed to making the reader a more capable thinker — not just a more informed one.
