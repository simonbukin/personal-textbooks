# Chapter 23: Cognitive Development: How Minds Are Built

A six-month-old infant watches a ball roll behind a screen. A moment later, the screen is lifted. In one condition, the ball is there, right where you would expect it. In another condition, the ball has vanished. The infant stares longer at the empty stage — measurably, reliably longer.

What do we make of this? The extra looking time suggests that the infant expected the ball to still be there, and that the ball's disappearance violated that expectation. This implies that a six-month-old has some understanding that objects continue to exist when they are out of sight, what psychologists call **object permanence**.

But what kind of understanding? Does the infant "know" that objects persist, the way you or I know it? Is this a belief, a concept, a perceptual expectation, or something else entirely? The infant cannot tell us. Six-month-olds do not produce language. They have limited motor ability. The only window we have into their minds is what they look at and for how long. And the interpretation of looking time is itself a matter of active scientific debate.

This is the fundamental challenge of cognitive development: understanding how minds are built when the minds in question cannot yet describe their own contents. It is also one of the places where cognitive science has made its most surprising discoveries, because infants, it turns out, know far more than anyone expected.

---

## Piaget: Revolutionary, Revised, Partially Discarded

Any discussion of cognitive development must begin with Jean Piaget, the Swiss psychologist who, more than anyone else, transformed the study of children's minds from anecdote into science.

Piaget proposed that children move through four stages of cognitive development, each characterized by qualitatively different modes of thinking:

1. **Sensorimotor stage** (birth to ~2 years): Knowledge is tied to perception and action. Object permanence develops gradually. Thought is not yet symbolic.
2. **Preoperational stage** (~2-7 years): Children acquire language and symbolic thought but are egocentric (unable to take others' perspectives) and fail conservation tasks (they believe that pouring water from a short, wide glass into a tall, narrow glass changes the amount).
3. **Concrete operational stage** (~7-11 years): Children master conservation, classification, and logical operations on concrete objects but struggle with abstract reasoning.
4. **Formal operational stage** (~12+): Abstract reasoning, hypothetical thinking, and systematic problem-solving become possible.

Piaget's contribution was enormous. He demonstrated that children are not miniature adults with less knowledge but qualitatively different thinkers. He invented clever experimental methods for probing children's reasoning. His stage theory, whatever its flaws, provided a framework that organized decades of research.

But the stage theory has not held up well. The problems are multiple.

**Underestimation of infants and young children.** Piaget's methods relied heavily on children's ability to physically manipulate objects or verbally explain their reasoning. When researchers developed methods that did not require motor coordination or language (particularly looking-time and violation-of-expectation paradigms), they found that infants and young children have capacities that Piaget attributed to much later stages. Object permanence, as we saw in the opening example, appears far earlier than Piaget's sensorimotor timeline predicted. Young children's failure on conservation tasks may reflect difficulty with the linguistic demands of the task rather than an inability to understand quantity.

**Stage boundaries are blurry.** Children do not transition cleanly from one stage to another. The same child may show concrete operational reasoning on one task and preoperational reasoning on another, depending on the domain, the specific task demands, and the child's familiarity with the materials. Development appears to be more gradual and domain-specific than stage theory implies.

**Cultural variation.** Piaget treated his stages as universal, but cross-cultural research has shown that the rate of development varies across cultures, and the formal operational stage may not be universal in the way Piaget described. Whether this reflects genuine cognitive differences or differences in familiarity with the types of tasks Piaget used is debated.

Piaget was right that cognitive development involves qualitative change, not just the accumulation of knowledge. He was right that children actively construct their understanding of the world through interaction with it. But the specific stage theory, with its rigid sequence and age ranges, has been replaced by a more nuanced picture.

## Core Knowledge: What Infants Bring to the Table

If Piaget underestimated infants, what do infants actually know? The most influential answer comes from Elizabeth Spelke and her colleagues, who argue that humans are born with a set of **core knowledge systems**, innate, domain-specific, and shared across cultures, that provide a foundation for later learning.

Spelke proposes at least four core knowledge systems:

### Objects

Infants expect objects to be cohesive (they move as connected wholes), to be solid (two objects cannot occupy the same space), and to persist through occlusion (they continue to exist when hidden). These expectations are evident in looking-time studies by 3-4 months — well before infants can search for hidden objects manually. The knowledge is not learned through motor experience, because it appears before infants have the motor ability to reach and grasp.

### Agents

Infants distinguish between objects that move on their own (agents) and objects that are moved by external forces. By 6 months, they attribute goals to agents: they expect an agent that has been reaching for a particular object to continue reaching for that object, even when the object moves to a new location. This is not mere motion tracking. It is an inference about intention.

### Number

Infants can discriminate between small numbers of objects. Show a 6-month-old a display of 8 dots, then a display of 16 dots, and the infant will look longer at the new number. This approximate number sense is ratio-dependent (infants can discriminate 8 from 16, but not 8 from 10) and is shared with many animal species. It is not counting; it is an approximate magnitude system. But it appears to provide a foundation on which exact counting and arithmetic are later built.

### Geometry and Space

Infants and young children use geometric relationships (distances, angles, the shape of an enclosure) to navigate and reorient. When disoriented in a rectangular room with a hidden toy, even 18-month-olds use the room's geometry (the long wall is to the left of the short wall) to find the toy, while often ignoring non-geometric cues like the color of the walls. This geometric sense is shared with rats, chickens, and other species, suggesting an evolutionarily ancient navigational system.

The core knowledge proposal is important because it suggests that learning does not start from a blank slate. Infants come equipped with representational systems that structure their experience from the beginning. But the proposal raises as many questions as it answers. How do these core systems interact with each other and with later learning? How does an approximate number sense eventually support calculus? Why are the core systems sometimes inaccurate? Infants' object representations, for instance, fail to encode features like color and shape when objects are behind occluders. And how much of what looks like innate knowledge might be rapidly learned in the first weeks of life, before researchers can test for it?

## Primary Text Spotlight: Spelke et al. (1992) — "Origins of Knowledge"

Elizabeth Spelke's 1992 paper, published in Psychological Review, is the most influential statement of the core knowledge position. It synthesized a decade of experimental findings and presented a theoretical framework that reshaped developmental cognitive science.

The paper's argument proceeds in several steps. First, Spelke reviews the evidence that infants have expectations about physical objects that go well beyond what simple associative learning could explain. Infants expect objects to continue to exist behind occluders, to travel on connected paths through space, and not to pass through solid barriers. These expectations are present by 3-4 months — before infants have had significant experience manipulating objects.

Second, Spelke argues that these expectations reflect cognitive representations proper, not mere perceptual habits. The looking-time responses are not driven by low-level visual features (such as novelty or visual complexity) but by violations of physical principles. An infant who sees a ball appear to pass through a solid wall is not just seeing something unusual; she is seeing something that violates her representation of how solid objects behave.

Third, and most controversially, Spelke argues that these representations are **innate**: part of the initial endowment of the human mind, not learned through experience. The evidence for this claim comes from the early age at which the representations appear, the consistency of findings across very different testing methods and populations, and the fact that the representations emerge before the relevant motor experience (reaching, grasping, dropping) that might teach infants about object behavior.

The paper has been enormously productive. It launched decades of research on infant cognition and helped establish looking-time methods as a primary tool for studying minds that cannot speak. It also provoked vigorous debate. Empiricist critics argue that Spelke underestimates the amount of learning possible in the first months of life. Prenatal experience, early visual learning, and statistical learning could all contribute to the "innate" knowledge Spelke describes. Others argue that the looking-time method itself is ambiguous: longer looking might reflect surprise, confusion, interest, or mere preference, and the interpretation depends on theoretical commitments.

These debates are healthy and ongoing. What is not in dispute is that infants are far more cognitively sophisticated than Piaget, or most parents, expected. Spelke's paper was the most important catalyst for that recognition.

## Executive Function and Its Development

Core knowledge systems provide the initial foundation, but much of what we consider mature cognition depends on **executive function**, the set of cognitive processes that enable flexible, goal-directed behavior. Executive function includes working memory (holding and manipulating information), inhibitory control (suppressing prepotent responses), and cognitive flexibility (shifting between tasks or perspectives).

Executive function develops slowly and unevenly. Consider a classic demonstration: the **Dimensional Change Card Sort** (DCCS) task. A 3-year-old is asked to sort cards by color (blue cards here, red cards there). She does this easily. Then the rule changes: sort by shape (stars here, trucks there). The same child who just sorted by color will perseveratively continue sorting by color, even though she can state the new rule correctly. She knows she should sort by shape but cannot override the previously established response. By age 5, most children pass the switch.

This failure of inhibitory control is not limited to preschoolers. Teenagers show immature executive function relative to adults, particularly in emotionally charged situations. The prefrontal cortex, which supports executive function, does not reach structural maturity until the mid-20s. This has been widely cited in discussions of adolescent risk-taking, though the popular claim that teenagers have "mature emotion systems but immature control systems" is an oversimplification.

The development of executive function has important implications for education. Tasks that seem simple from an adult perspective may fail for young children not because they lack the relevant knowledge but because they lack the executive resources to apply that knowledge flexibly. The preoperational failures Piaget documented — egocentrism, conservation errors — may be failures of executive function as much as failures of conceptual understanding. The child may know, at some level, that the amount of water does not change when poured into a different glass, but she cannot inhibit the salient perceptual cue (the water level is higher!) to access that knowledge.

## Critical Periods

Not all aspects of development are equally flexible across the lifespan. Some capacities must be acquired during restricted time windows — **critical periods**, sometimes called "sensitive periods," a term that better captures the gradual nature of most such windows.

**Vision.** If a child is born with cataracts that are not corrected in the first months of life, the deprived eye never develops normal acuity, even after the cataracts are removed. The visual cortex, denied input during the critical period, reorganizes in ways that cannot be fully reversed. Hubel and Wiesel's work on monocular deprivation in kittens, which we encountered in the perception chapter, provided the neural basis for understanding critical periods in vision.

**Language.** First language acquisition proceeds effortlessly if it occurs in the first few years of life. Children deprived of language input, whether through profound neglect or through being deaf in a hearing family without sign language access, show permanent deficits if exposure is delayed past early childhood. The case of Genie, a girl isolated from language until age 13, is often cited; despite years of subsequent training, she never acquired full grammatical competence. Second language acquisition also shows critical period effects, with a gradual decline in the ability to achieve native-like proficiency beginning around age 5-7.

**Emotional attachment.** The quality of early caregiving relationships affects social and emotional development in ways that can persist into adulthood. Children raised in severely deprived institutional environments (as studied in the Romanian orphan cohorts) show elevated rates of attachment disorders, cognitive delays, and psychiatric problems, even after adoption into high-quality homes. The extent of recovery depends partly on the age at which deprivation ends. Earlier is better, but the window is not absolute.

Critical periods reveal something important about the nature-nurture relationship: biology provides a window during which certain experiences are expected, and if those experiences do not arrive on time, development proceeds differently, sometimes irreversibly. The brain is not a passive receiver of experience but an active system that requires specific inputs at specific times.

## Metacognition: Knowing What You Know

One of the last cognitive capacities to mature is **metacognition**, the ability to monitor and evaluate your own cognitive processes. Do you understand this paragraph? Are you confident in your answer to that question? Do you know that you do not know the capital of Burkina Faso?

Metacognitive ability develops gradually across childhood and adolescence. Young children are notoriously poor at assessing their own knowledge and abilities. A 4-year-old will confidently predict she can memorize 20 items (she cannot) and will express certainty about answers that are wrong. By age 6-8, children become somewhat more calibrated, but even older children and teenagers overestimate their own understanding. This pattern, honestly, persists in many adults.

The development of metacognition is important for education because effective learning requires knowing what you do not know. Students who cannot accurately assess their own understanding cannot allocate study time efficiently, cannot recognize when they need to re-read a passage, and cannot generate the productive sense of confusion that drives deeper learning.

## Poverty of the Stimulus and the Nature-Nurture Continuum

The observation that infants know more than experience alone seems to explain brings us to one of the oldest debates in cognitive science: how much of the mind is innate and how much is learned?

Noam Chomsky's **poverty of the stimulus argument**, originally applied to language, makes the case for innateness in its strongest form. Children acquire grammatical knowledge that goes beyond anything present in the input they receive. They hear finite, often fragmentary utterances and induce an infinite, structured grammar. Since the input underdetermines the output, something must be built in: a language acquisition device, or universal grammar.

The core knowledge findings extend this logic beyond language. If infants have expectations about object persistence before they have manipulated objects, something about those expectations must be endogenous. But how much?

Modern developmental science has largely moved beyond the nature-versus-nurture dichotomy. The current consensus is that genes and environment are not separate inputs that add together but interacting factors that co-construct development. Genes require specific environments to be expressed. Environments have different effects depending on genetic variation. Epigenetic mechanisms allow experiences to alter gene expression without changing DNA sequence. The question is not "how much is nature and how much is nurture" but "how do biological and experiential factors interact to produce this particular developmental outcome?"

This does not mean that the nativist-empiricist debate is resolved. The core knowledge camp and the statistical learning camp disagree, sometimes intensely, about how much structure is present at birth versus how much is rapidly constructed from environmental regularities. This disagreement is productive; it generates experiments and theoretical refinements. But anyone who tells you the debate is settled is not reporting the state of the field accurately.

## What This Gets Right / What's Still Open

**What this gets right:**

Piaget was both revolutionary and wrong about specifics, a common pattern for foundational figures. The core knowledge findings represent genuine discoveries: infants are far more cognitively sophisticated than previously believed. The development of executive function is well documented and has real implications for education and child-rearing. Critical period research has changed clinical practice for vision disorders and language deprivation.

**What's still open:**

The interpretation of infant looking-time data remains controversial. The nativist-empiricist debate about core knowledge is unresolved. We do not have a satisfying account of how core systems are integrated with later-acquired knowledge, or how an approximate number sense eventually supports algebra. The role of sleep, nutrition, stress, and social interaction in cognitive development is established in broad strokes but poorly understood in detail. And the vast majority of developmental research has been conducted in Western, industrialized populations, raising serious questions about generalizability.

## Real-World Application: What Actually Matters for Cognitive Development

### Why "Educational" Infant Toys Mostly Don't Work

Walk through a toy store and you will find an aisle of products marketed as cognitively stimulating for infants: Baby Einstein videos, educational apps for toddlers, black-and-white mobiles for "visual development." The implicit promise is that providing extra stimulation during critical periods will accelerate cognitive development and give children a head start.

The evidence does not support this promise. The American Academy of Pediatrics has repeatedly found no evidence that screen media provides educational benefits for children under 2 years. The "Baby Einstein" brand was pressured into offering refunds after researchers pointed out that the products had no demonstrated educational value. Studies of enriched environments in rats (more toys, more social interaction) do produce neural changes, but these studies compare enrichment to deprivation, not enrichment to normal experience. For human infants in adequately stimulating homes, adding more stimulation does not produce measurable cognitive benefits.

This makes sense in light of core knowledge research. If infants come equipped with representational systems that expect certain inputs (faces, objects, language, social interaction), then a normal, loving home environment provides those inputs in abundance. The bottleneck on cognitive development is not a shortage of stimulation but the maturation of neural systems that operate on their own timetable.

### What Actually Matters

The developmental science literature converges on a few factors that matter for cognitive development, and they are not the ones that the toy industry sells:

**Language exposure.** The quantity and quality of language that children hear in the first three years is one of the strongest predictors of later language and cognitive outcomes. This is not about flashcards or drilling. It is about conversational interaction, responsive communication, and the rich, varied language that comes from engaged caregivers talking to and with children.

**Responsive caregiving.** Secure attachment, formed when a caregiver is consistently responsive to the child's signals, provides the emotional foundation for exploration and learning. Children who feel safe explore more, and exploration is how children learn.

**Play.** Unstructured, child-directed play, especially pretend play and play with other children, supports the development of executive function, perspective-taking, and language. Organized, adult-directed "enrichment" activities are not substitutes and may be less effective.

**Protection from adversity.** Chronic stress, neglect, and exposure to violence are among the most powerful negative influences on cognitive development. The physiological stress response, when chronically activated, disrupts the development of prefrontal circuits that support executive function and self-regulation. The most impactful interventions for cognitive development may not be educational at all. They may be economic and social programs that reduce family stress and increase stability.

The unsexy truth of cognitive development is that evolution has designed children to develop in the context of attentive caregiving, rich language, and safe environments. When those conditions are met, cognitive development proceeds robustly. When they are not met, no amount of educational toys can compensate.
