# Chapter 26: The Limits and Future of Cognitive Science

We have spent twenty-five chapters mapping a science. We have traced the arc from behaviorism through the cognitive revolution, the rise of connectionism, and the embodied turn. We have examined how minds perceive, remember, reason, speak, develop, and break down. Now it is time to do something harder: map the edges of the map. Where does cognitive science's confidence end? Where has it overreached? And what new tools, from large-scale neural recording to large language models, are redrawing the boundaries?

This is not a victory lap. Cognitive science has real accomplishments, findings that have survived decades of replication and informed real-world practice. But it also has blind spots, overstatements, and open questions so fundamental that honesty requires naming them. The mark of a mature science is not that it has all the answers but that it knows what it does not know. This chapter is an attempt at that kind of inventory.

---

## What Cognitive Science Has Definitively Delivered

Before we criticize, let us take stock. Some findings are robust, replicated, and applicable. They have survived the replication crisis, held up across multiple methods, and made a real difference outside the laboratory.

**Memory is reconstructive, not reproductive.** This is one of the most thoroughly documented findings in the field. From Bartlett in the 1930s to Loftus and the modern misinformation literature, the evidence is overwhelming: memory does not work like a recording. It is a constructive process subject to systematic distortions, with direct consequences for legal testimony, clinical practice, and education.

**Attention is limited and selective.** From the early filter theories through the attentional blink and inattentional blindness, the evidence is clear: you cannot process everything, and what you miss can be dramatic. The practical implications for driving, aviation, and medical monitoring are significant and well-documented.

**Cognitive development follows broadly predictable sequences.** While Piaget's specific stage model has been substantially revised, the core insight that children's thinking changes qualitatively over development, and not only quantitatively, has held up. Infants know more than Piaget thought, but they still develop in patterned ways.

**Dual-process frameworks capture something real about human reasoning.** The specific details of System 1 and System 2 are debated, but the general observation that humans have both fast, automatic processes and slow, deliberate ones, and that these can conflict, is one of the most productive ideas in the field. Kahneman and Tversky's work on heuristics and biases has been translated into policy, medicine, and law.

**Language is structured at multiple levels, and these levels are at least partially dissociable.** Phonology, syntax, semantics, and pragmatics can be independently disrupted by brain damage, independently studied in the lab, and independently modeled computationally. This is not a trivial result.

**The brain is functionally organized, but not rigidly modular.** Lesion studies, neuroimaging, and now large-scale recording have converged on a picture of functional specialization (particular brain regions contribute disproportionately to particular cognitive functions) combined with massive interconnection and plasticity. Neither strict modularity nor equipotentiality is correct.

## What Cognitive Science Thought It Delivered

Not every confident claim has survived scrutiny. The replication crisis, which swept through psychology beginning around 2011, forced a painful reckoning. Some celebrated findings turned out to be fragile, inflated by small samples, flexible analyses, and publication bias.

**Ego depletion**, the idea that self-control draws on a limited resource that is depleted by use, was one of the most cited concepts in social psychology. Large-scale replications have failed to find the effect, or found it to be far smaller than originally claimed. The metaphor of willpower as a fuel tank is probably wrong.

**Social priming**, the claim that subtle environmental cues (holding a warm cup of coffee, walking slowly) can dramatically shift social judgments and behavior, has been among the hardest hit by replication failures. Some specific effects may be real but small; many of the most dramatic demonstrations have not replicated.

**Embodied metaphor priming**, as we discussed in the previous chapter, has also faced serious replication challenges. The general thesis that bodies influence cognition is plausible, but many of the specific demonstrations that made it into textbooks are now questionable.

**The Mozart effect**, the claim that listening to Mozart improves spatial reasoning, was always overstated. The original finding was a small, temporary effect that subsequent research has largely attributed to arousal rather than anything specific to music.

The lesson is not that cognitive science is unreliable. It is that the field, like all sciences, is self-correcting, but the correction can take decades. The well-informed reader should be alert to the difference between findings supported by many large, pre-registered studies and findings supported by a handful of small, flexible ones.

## The Measurement Problem

Cognitive science faces a foundational challenge that physics overcame centuries ago but that the sciences of the mind have not. We cannot directly observe the thing we are studying.

No brain scanner reveals a thought. No behavioral measure directly reads a mental representation. Every method in cognitive science involves an **inferential gap** between what we observe (behavior, brain activity, response time) and what we want to understand (mental processes, representations, computations). Reaction times can tell us that one process is slower than another, but they cannot tell us the nature of the process. Brain imaging can tell us which areas are active during a task, but it cannot tell us what computation those areas are performing.

This does not make cognitive science impossible. But it means that our theories are always underdetermined by the data to a greater degree than in sciences where the target phenomena can be directly measured. Two very different cognitive architectures can produce the same pattern of behavior. Two very different computational processes can produce the same pattern of brain activity. Resolving this underdetermination requires converging evidence from multiple methods, which is why cognitive science is interdisciplinary by necessity, not just by preference.

## The Ecological Validity Problem

Much of what cognitive science knows comes from carefully controlled laboratory experiments. Control is essential for causal inference; you need to isolate variables to know what is causing what. But control comes at a cost: laboratory settings strip away the context that characterizes real-world cognition.

Consider memory research. In a typical lab study, participants memorize lists of unrelated words and are tested after a short delay. This tells us important things about encoding and retrieval. But how well does it predict how a doctor remembers patient histories, how a cook remembers recipes, or how you remember the conversation you had with a friend last week? The gap between list-learning and real-world remembering is not trivial.

Or consider decision-making research. Much of what we know about human judgment comes from studies where people evaluate hypothetical scenarios with clearly defined options and outcomes. Real decisions are made under time pressure, with incomplete information, in social contexts, with emotional stakes. Whether the biases identified in the lab persist, and at the same magnitudes, in ecologically rich settings is an empirical question, not a settled one.

The field is increasingly aware of this problem. Ecological momentary assessment, experience sampling, field studies, and computational modeling of naturalistic behavior are all attempts to bridge the gap. But bridging is difficult, and much of the textbook canon rests on laboratory findings whose ecological validity has not been rigorously tested.

## The WEIRD Problem

In 2010, Joseph Henrich, Steven Heine, and Ara Norenzayan published a paper with a title that became a label: "The weirdest people in the world?" Their argument was that the vast majority of behavioral science research, including cognitive science, draws its participants from Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic (WEIRD) societies, and frequently from the narrowest slice of those societies: undergraduate psychology students at research universities.

The problem is not just that these samples are unrepresentative. It is that WEIRD populations are often outliers on the very dimensions being studied. Visual illusion susceptibility, spatial reasoning strategies, moral intuitions, concepts of self, categorization strategies: all of these vary cross-culturally in ways that matter for cognitive theory.

If a theory of categorization is tested only on American undergraduates, and those undergraduates turn out to categorize objects differently from people in small-scale non-Western societies, then the theory is not a theory of human categorization. It is a theory of a particular culturally shaped version of categorization. The generalizability problem is not a minor footnote. It strikes at the heart of what cognitive science claims to be doing: studying the human mind.

Progress has been slow but real. Cross-cultural cognitive science is growing. Large-scale multi-site collaborations are becoming more common. But the field is still far from the point where its major theories have been tested on anything close to a representative sample of humanity.

## What Consciousness Research Cannot Yet Address

Consciousness is the elephant in the room of cognitive science. We have theories (Global Workspace Theory, Integrated Information Theory, Higher-Order Theories, predictive processing accounts) but we do not have anything approaching consensus on what consciousness is, how it arises, or even how to measure it reliably.

The "hard problem" identified by David Chalmers in 1995, why there is subjective experience at all and why physical processes give rise to felt qualities, remains unsolved. Some researchers think it is a genuine mystery that requires new theoretical tools. Others think it is a confused question that will dissolve as our understanding of neural processing advances. Still others think it is simply beyond the reach of current scientific methods.

What we can say with confidence is that no existing theory of consciousness makes precise, testable predictions that would allow us to definitively confirm or disconfirm it. This does not mean that consciousness research is useless. It has produced important findings about the neural correlates of conscious experience, the conditions under which information reaches awareness, and the dissociations between conscious and unconscious processing. But the core question, what makes a physical process conscious, remains open.

## AI and Cognitive Science

Perhaps no development in the last decade has shaken cognitive science more profoundly than the rise of large language models (LLMs). These systems, trained on vast corpora of text to predict the next word, have achieved performance on language tasks that many researchers believed would require human-like understanding. This forces a question: what does language ability actually tell us about cognition?

### What LLMs Reveal About Language

The landmark paper here is Mahowald et al. (2024), "Dissociating language and thought in large language models," which we will examine in detail below. The central argument is that LLMs have revealed a dissociation that was always present in human cognition but was hard to see: **formal linguistic competence** (knowledge of syntax, morphology, and phonological patterns, the structural machinery of language) is separable from **functional linguistic competence** (the ability to use language for reasoning, planning, social interaction, and real-world understanding).

LLMs are spectacularly good at formal competence. They produce grammatically fluent text, handle complex syntax, maintain long-range agreement, and exhibit sensitivity to subtle linguistic patterns. But they are notably poor at functional competence: they struggle with logical reasoning, causal understanding, consistent world modeling, and the kind of grounded common sense that even young children possess.

This dissociation matters for cognitive science because it suggests that much of what makes language impressive, its structural complexity, may be learnable from distributional statistics alone, without the kind of embodied, social, goal-directed experience that characterizes human language acquisition. But the things we do with language (reason, plan, persuade, deceive, empathize) may require cognitive machinery that goes well beyond linguistic pattern learning.

### Marr's Levels Revisited

The relationship between LLMs and human cognition can be usefully analyzed through David Marr's three levels. At the **computational level**, the question of what input-output mapping is being computed, LLMs and humans may be solving overlapping but not identical problems. Both process language, but humans process language in the service of goals, social relationships, and survival in a physical world. LLMs process language in the service of next-token prediction.

At the **algorithmic level**, where we ask what representations and processes are used, the systems almost certainly differ profoundly. Transformer architectures process entire sequences in parallel through attention mechanisms. Human language processing is incremental, memory-constrained, and deeply integrated with perception, action, and emotion. Two systems can compute similar input-output functions using completely different algorithms, and there is no reason to assume that LLMs have discovered the algorithms the brain uses.

At the **implementational level**, the differences are obvious: silicon versus neurons, massive parallelism versus biological circuitry. But the more interesting question is whether the algorithmic differences matter. If an LLM and a human produce the same grammatical judgments for different algorithmic reasons, what does that teach us about language? The answer: it teaches us about the computational-level structure of the problem (what the task requires) but not about the algorithmic-level structure of the human solution (how the brain accomplishes it).

### What AI Gets Right That Cognitive Science Missed

LLMs have demonstrated that an enormous amount of linguistic knowledge can be extracted from distributional statistics, specifically patterns of word co-occurrence in large text corpora. This was theoretically anticipated by some researchers (the "distributional hypothesis" dates to the 1950s), but the scale of the success was not predicted. Many linguists and cognitive scientists assumed that learning the syntax of a natural language would require innate, language-specific constraints. LLMs suggest that at least the formal aspects of syntax can be learned from data, given enough data and the right architecture.

Conversely, LLMs have highlighted what pure pattern learning cannot do. Despite training on more text than any human could read in a thousand lifetimes, LLMs still make errors that no cognitively normal adult would make: failures of causal reasoning, physical intuition, and social understanding that reveal the limits of learning from text alone. This is a valuable finding: it tells us what aspects of human cognition are not captured by linguistic experience, even unlimited linguistic experience.

### The Alignment Problem

The practical significance of understanding human cognition has never been greater. As AI systems become more capable, the problem of ensuring that they behave in ways consistent with human values, the **alignment problem**, becomes urgent. Alignment is fundamentally a cognitive science problem. It requires understanding what human values are, how they are represented, how they are communicated (often implicitly), and how they conflict and resolve. It requires understanding human intention, human communication, and human social cognition. Building safe AI systems may depend on cognitive science more than it depends on any other field.

## Primary Text Spotlight: Mahowald et al. (2024)

Kyle Mahowald, Anna Ivanova, Idan Blank, Nancy Kanwisher, Joshua Tenenbaum, and Evelina Fedorenko's 2024 paper "Dissociating language and thought in large language models" uses LLMs as a natural experiment to test a hypothesis about the architecture of human cognition.

The paper draws on a large body of evidence from neuroscience showing that the brain's language network — a set of regions in the left hemisphere that respond selectively during language processing, is dissociable from the brain's reasoning and social cognition networks. Patients with severe aphasia (language network damage) can retain intact logical reasoning, planning, and social understanding. Conversely, patients with intact language can have severely impaired reasoning. Language processing and thinking, the neuroscience suggests, are supported by different neural systems.

Mahowald and colleagues argue that LLMs provide converging evidence for this dissociation from a completely different direction. LLMs are essentially language networks without reasoning networks. They have been optimized to process language, and they do so with notable facility, but they lack the separate cognitive systems that in humans support reasoning, world modeling, and social cognition. If the neuroscience is right that language and thought are dissociable, then a system that has only the language part should exhibit exactly the pattern that LLMs exhibit: excellent formal competence, poor functional competence.

The paper is carefully argued and avoids the trap of treating LLMs as either complete models of human cognition or irrelevant curiosities. Instead, it treats them as **tools for cognitive science**, systems that, by virtue of what they can and cannot do, illuminate the structure of the human mind. The failures of LLMs are as informative as their successes, because they tell us which cognitive abilities require more than language processing.

The paper also introduces a useful taxonomy. **Formal linguistic competence** includes phonology, morphology, syntax, and the distributional semantics that can be learned from text. **Functional linguistic competence** includes reasoning, world knowledge, social cognition, and pragmatic inference, the abilities required to use language in the real world. This distinction, the authors argue, should replace the vague notion that LLMs either "understand" or "don't understand" language. The truth is more specific. They understand its form but not its function.

## Large-Scale Neural Recording and the Future of Measurement

One of cognitive science's persistent limitations has been the tradeoff between temporal resolution (when does brain activity happen) and spatial resolution (where does it happen). EEG gives millisecond timing but poor spatial localization. fMRI gives millimeter spatial resolution but poor timing. Single-cell recording gives both but is invasive and limited to small regions.

Emerging technologies are beginning to break through this tradeoff. Neuropixels probes can record from thousands of neurons simultaneously across multiple brain regions in animal models, providing unprecedented views of how neural populations coordinate during cognitive tasks. Advanced calcium imaging can record from tens of thousands of neurons in behaving animals. High-density EEG and MEG arrays are improving non-invasive human recordings. Computational methods for analyzing these massive datasets, from dimensionality reduction to machine learning decoders, are advancing rapidly.

What these tools promise is a shift from studying individual brain regions to studying **neural dynamics**: how patterns of activity unfold across distributed networks over time. This may help resolve some of the field's open questions: How does the brain represent abstract concepts? How does information flow between regions during complex tasks? How do neural populations code for different types of memory?

But technology alone does not solve the interpretation problem. More data requires better theories. A high-resolution recording of a million neurons during a decision task is useless without a computational framework for understanding what those neurons are doing. The hope is that the combination of better data and better models will be more than the sum of its parts.

## Open Questions

Some of the deepest questions in cognitive science remain open:

**The neural code.** How do neurons encode and transmit information? Rate coding (information in firing rate) is established but insufficient. Temporal coding, population coding, predictive coding: all are active research areas, and none provides a complete account.

**How abstract thought develops.** How does a child who begins with sensorimotor interactions with the world come to think about justice, infinity, and hypothetical futures? The transition from concrete to abstract thought is one of the most impressive feats of cognitive development, and we have only partial accounts of how it works.

**The architecture of long-term memory.** How is knowledge organized in the brain? Is there a unified system or multiple systems? How are memories linked, updated, and selectively retrieved? Despite decades of work, the computational principles governing long-term memory storage and retrieval remain poorly understood.

**Conscious access.** Why are some mental processes accessible to conscious awareness and others not? What determines the boundary? This is related to the hard problem of consciousness but is in some ways more tractable; it is about the function of consciousness rather than its ultimate nature.

## What "Understanding a Mind" Means

As we close, it is worth returning to a question we raised in Chapter 1: what would it mean to understand a mind?

David Marr's three levels (computational, algorithmic, implementational) remain the most useful framework. To fully understand a cognitive capacity, we need to know what problem it solves (computational level), how it solves it (algorithmic level), and how the solution is physically realized (implementational level). A complete cognitive science would have answers at all three levels for every major cognitive capacity, and those answers would be mutually consistent.

We are nowhere near that goal. For most cognitive capacities, we have partial answers at one or two levels and no answers at the third. We understand the computational problem of stereoscopic depth perception quite well, and we have good algorithmic models. We understand the implementational details of early visual processing reasonably well. But for higher cognition (reasoning, planning, creative thought) we often struggle even to specify the computational problem precisely, let alone the algorithm or the implementation.

This is not cause for despair. Cognitive science is a young field, roughly seventy years old depending on when you start counting. Physics is several centuries further along and still has foundational questions. The mind is arguably the most complex system in the known universe. That we understand as much as we do is impressive.

## The Honest Inventory

What cognitive science has **established**: That the mind is an information-processing system that can be studied scientifically. That mental processes are real, lawful, and at least partially decomposable. That cognition involves multiple specialized but interacting systems. That behavior is shaped by both automatic and deliberate processes. That memory, perception, and reasoning are constructive processes, not passive recording. That cognitive development follows broadly predictable patterns. That the brain is functionally organized but massively interconnected and plastic.

What cognitive science has **overreached on**: The precision and reliability of many specific experimental findings, particularly in social cognition. The generalizability of findings from WEIRD populations. The completeness of any single-framework account (pure computationalism, pure connectionism, pure embodiment). The promise of neuroimaging as a mind-reading tool.

What cognitive science has **corrected**: The behaviorist denial of mental processes. Naively modular views of brain function. The assumption that cognitive capacities are uniform across cultures. The assumption that intelligence is a unitary trait. Overconfidence in specific empirical findings, through the painful but necessary process of replication.

What cognitive science is **still working on**: Consciousness. The neural code. The transition from concrete to abstract thought. The architecture of long-term memory. The relationship between language and thought. The degree to which cognition extends beyond the brain. The integration of computational, algorithmic, and implementational explanations. The development of theories that account for the full range of human cognitive diversity.

## Real-World Application: What the Well-Informed Non-Specialist Should Be Able to Do

After twenty-six chapters, what should you, the intelligent non-specialist who has worked through this book, be equipped to do?

**Evaluate claims about the mind critically.** When you encounter a news article claiming that scientists have discovered the "brain region for jealousy" or that a new brain training app will prevent dementia, you should be able to identify the inferential leaps, ask about sample sizes and replication, and distinguish what the study actually showed from what the headline implies. You should recognize the difference between a correlation and a mechanism, between a laboratory demonstration and a real-world effect.

**Understand your own cognition better.** Not in a pop-psychology, self-help sense, but in a practically useful way. You should understand why you are bad at multitasking (attention is limited and switching is costly), why your memories are unreliable (reconstruction, not recording), why you are susceptible to specific reasoning errors (heuristics are useful but imperfect), and why your intuitions about your own mental processes are often wrong (introspection is a poor guide to cognitive mechanism).

**Engage with interdisciplinary questions.** Cognitive science sits at the intersection of psychology, neuroscience, linguistics, computer science, philosophy, and anthropology. If you have internalized the approach of this book, you should be comfortable with the idea that understanding the mind requires multiple levels of analysis and multiple disciplinary perspectives. No single method, no single framework, no single discipline has the whole picture.

**Think carefully about AI.** As AI systems become more capable and more present in daily life, the ability to think clearly about what these systems can and cannot do, and how they relate to human cognition, will be increasingly important. You should understand that a system producing fluent language is not necessarily understanding anything, that behavioral performance does not guarantee shared mechanism, and that the alignment of AI with human values requires understanding those values at a depth that current AI lacks.

**Hold uncertainty productively.** This may be the most important skill of all. Cognitive science is full of partial answers, provisional theories, and open questions. The temptation is either to treat current knowledge as settled truth or to dismiss the whole enterprise because nothing is certain. Neither response is appropriate. The productive stance is to hold what we know with calibrated confidence — strong where the evidence is strong, tentative where it is thin, and honest about the gaps.
