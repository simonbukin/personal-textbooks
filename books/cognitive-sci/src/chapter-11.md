# Chapter 11: Memory as Construction: Why We Misremember

Three people witness the same car accident at the same intersection. Later, each gives a different account. The first says the blue car ran the red light. The second says the light was yellow. The third says there was no traffic light at all, it was a stop sign. Each witness is confident. Each is willing to testify. At least two of them are wrong.

This is not an unusual scenario. It is the norm. Decades of research on eyewitness memory have established, beyond reasonable doubt, that human memory for events is not a video recording. It is a reconstruction, assembled from fragments, shaped by expectations, influenced by what happened after the event, and maintained with a confidence that bears disturbingly little relationship to its accuracy.

This chapter is about why we misremember, how reconstructive memory works, and what the practical consequences are for law, organizations, and daily life. The stakes are not merely academic. People have spent decades in prison because a confident eyewitness pointed at them and said, "That's the one." Understanding memory as construction is a matter of justice, not just intellectual curiosity.

---

## Memory Is Reconstructive, Not Reproductive

The idea that memory is a faithful recording is intuitive and wrong. It feels like your memories are stored somewhere, intact, waiting to be played back. The reality is that memories are constructed at the time of retrieval from whatever fragments are available (perceptual details, emotional associations, general knowledge, and contextual cues), combined according to your current expectations, beliefs, and goals.

This idea is not new. Frederic Bartlett, a British psychologist working in the 1930s, demonstrated it with a simple and brilliant experiment. He asked English university students to read a Native American folk tale called "The War of the Ghosts" and then retell it from memory at various intervals.

The story was deliberately unfamiliar; it involved ghosts, canoes, and a narrative structure that did not follow Western storytelling conventions. What Bartlett found was that with each retelling, participants systematically transformed the story. They shortened it. They made it more coherent by Western standards. They replaced unfamiliar details with familiar ones: "canoe" became "boat," supernatural elements were dropped or rationalized. The story, in other words, was assimilated to the participants' existing schemas.

Bartlett concluded that remembering is not retrieval of a stored copy but an "imaginative reconstruction," an active process of building a plausible account from schematic knowledge and whatever specific details are available. This was a radical claim in the 1930s, and it took decades for the field to fully appreciate it. But modern research has confirmed and extended Bartlett's insight in ways he could not have imagined.

## Source Monitoring: Where Did I Learn That?

One of the core mechanisms of memory error is source monitoring failure, the inability to correctly identify where a piece of information came from. You know something, but you misattribute it to the wrong source.

Marcia Johnson's source monitoring framework, developed in the 1990s, provides a useful way to think about this. When you retrieve a memory, you do not just retrieve the content; you also make a judgment about its origin. Was this something I saw, or something I imagined? Did I hear this from a reliable source or an unreliable one? Did this happen to me or did I see it in a movie?

These judgments are based on the qualitative characteristics of the memory. Real memories tend to have more perceptual detail, more spatial and temporal context, and more sensory vividness than imagined ones. But these are probabilistic cues, not definitive markers. If you vividly imagine something, the resulting memory trace can have enough perceptual detail to be confused with a real experience.

Source monitoring failures explain a wide range of memory errors. Cryptomnesia, unconsciously plagiarizing someone else's idea and believing it is your own, is a source monitoring failure. So is confusing something you saw on the news with something you personally witnessed. And for legal contexts, so is confusing a face you saw in a mugshot with the face you saw at the crime scene.

## The Misinformation Effect

If memory is reconstructive, then information encountered after an event should be able to alter the memory of the event itself. This is exactly what Elizabeth Loftus and her colleagues demonstrated in a series of landmark studies beginning in the 1970s.

The basic paradigm is simple. Show people a sequence of slides depicting an event, say a car accident. Then, in a subsequent questionnaire, slip in misleading information. For example, ask "How fast were the cars going when they passed the yield sign?" when the slides actually showed a stop sign. Later, when people are asked what sign was at the intersection, a significant proportion will now report a yield sign. The post-event misinformation has been incorporated into the memory of the event.

This is the misinformation effect, and it is robust. It works with details large and small. It works even when people are warned that some of the post-event information might be misleading. It works with children (who are more susceptible) and adults. It works in the lab and, as we will see, in real-world legal settings.

The mechanism is debated. One possibility is that the misinformation overwrites the original memory trace, that the stop sign is literally replaced by the yield sign in memory. Another is that both traces coexist, but the more recent misinformation is more accessible at retrieval. A third is that people never encoded the original detail firmly, and the misinformation fills a gap rather than overwriting existing information.

The practical implication is the same regardless of mechanism: what happens after an event can change what people remember about the event. This means that memory is not merely unreliable in a random way; it is systematically malleable, shaped by social influence, leading questions, media exposure, and repeated telling.

The misinformation effect is amplified by social factors. If you discuss an event with other witnesses before being formally interviewed, their accounts can contaminate yours — and vice versa. This is called memory conformity or social contagion of memory. In laboratory studies, when people discuss a shared experience, they frequently incorporate details mentioned by their conversational partner, even when those details are wrong. The social pressure to agree, combined with the difficulty of distinguishing "I saw it" from "I heard someone say it," makes group discussion before formal interviews a potent source of memory contamination.

## Primary Text Spotlight: Loftus & Palmer (1974)

Elizabeth Loftus and John Palmer's paper "Reconstruction of automobile destruction" is one of the most cited studies in all of psychology, and it is notable for its simplicity.

In the first experiment, participants watched films of car accidents and then answered questions about what they had seen. The critical manipulation was a single word in one question: "About how fast were the cars going when they _____ each other?" The blank was filled with one of five verbs: contacted, hit, bumped, collided, or smashed.

The results were striking. Participants who got the word "smashed" estimated significantly higher speeds (40.8 mph on average) than those who got "contacted" (31.8 mph). A single word in a question altered the quantitative judgment about a witnessed event.

In the second experiment, Loftus and Palmer went further. A week after watching the film and answering the speed question, participants were asked: "Did you see any broken glass?" There was no broken glass in the film. But participants who had been asked the "smashed" version of the speed question were more than twice as likely to falsely report seeing broken glass.

This second finding is the more important one. It suggests that the verb did not merely bias people's speed estimates; it actually altered their memory of the event. "Smashed" imported a schema of a more violent collision, and that schema generated false details (broken glass) that were incorporated into the memory.

The study has limitations. Films are not real-life events. The sample sizes were modest by modern standards. The broken glass question was asked only once, so we do not know how durable the false memory was. But the basic finding, that the phrasing of a question can alter memory for the event the question is about, has been replicated and extended in hundreds of subsequent studies.

What makes this paper a landmark is what it opened up. It launched a research program on the malleability of memory that has had profound effects on the legal system, police procedures, and our basic understanding of how memory works. It also made Elizabeth Loftus one of the most influential and controversial psychologists of the twentieth century, controversial because her work on the fallibility of memory brought her into direct conflict with advocates who believed that recovered memories of childhood abuse were necessarily accurate.

## False Memories: When It Never Happened at All

The misinformation effect involves distorting details of events that actually occurred. But the story gets more unsettling: entire autobiographical memories, memories for events that never happened, can be implanted.

The most famous demonstration is the "lost in the mall" study by Loftus and Jacqueline Pickrell, published in 1995. Participants were given four short narratives about childhood events, supposedly provided by family members. Three were real events; one, being lost in a shopping mall as a child, was fabricated. After reading and thinking about the narratives over several sessions, about 25 percent of participants came to remember the fabricated event, some in vivid detail.

Subsequent studies have replicated and extended this finding with increasingly dramatic false events. Researchers have successfully implanted memories of being attacked by an animal, being the victim of a serious accident, and even committing a crime. The methods are simple: combine social pressure (a trusted authority says it happened), plausible context (it is the kind of thing that could have happened), and repeated imaginative elaboration (keep thinking about it and the details will come).

This does not mean that all memories are false, or that you should distrust everything you remember. Most of the time, memory serves us well enough for practical purposes. But it does mean that the vividness, detail, and emotional intensity of a memory are not reliable indicators of its accuracy. You can have a rich, detailed, emotionally vivid memory of something that never occurred.

The implications extend beyond the laboratory. In the 1990s, a wave of recovered memory therapy encouraged patients to use imagination exercises to "recover" childhood memories of abuse. Many of these recovered memories were almost certainly false — implanted by the very therapeutic techniques meant to uncover them. The resulting "memory wars" between clinicians who believed in recovered memories and researchers who demonstrated the ease of false memory creation were bitter and consequential. They led to destroyed families, lawsuits, and eventually a more cautious consensus: while genuine recovered memories may exist, the techniques used to elicit them carry a serious risk of creating false ones. The lesson is not that people never forget traumatic events (they sometimes do), but that the process of trying to remember can itself generate vivid false memories that are subjectively indistinguishable from real ones.

## Confidence and Accuracy: A Troubled Relationship

Common sense says that confident memories are accurate memories. If a witness says "I am absolutely certain that was the man," we feel reassured. Courts and juries are heavily influenced by witness confidence.

The research tells a more complicated story. The relationship between confidence and accuracy in eyewitness identification is weak to moderate, especially at the initial identification. People can be highly confident and completely wrong. They can be uncertain and correct.

The relationship is further degraded by post-identification feedback. If a witness makes an identification and is then told "Good, you identified the suspect," their confidence in the identification increases, even if the identification was wrong. This confidence inflation is dangerous because by the time the witness reaches the courtroom, months or years later, the inflated confidence is all that remains. The original uncertainty has been overwritten.

Gary Wells, a psychologist who has spent his career studying eyewitness identification, has been particularly influential in documenting these effects and advocating for procedural reforms. His work shows that the conditions under which an identification is made (the instructions given to the witness, the composition of the lineup, the feedback provided afterward) can dramatically affect both the accuracy and the confidence of the identification, often in opposite directions.

## Motivated Memory

Memory is not shaped only by schemas and post-event information. It is also shaped by our motivations and goals. We tend to remember our past in ways that serve our current self-image.

Research on motivated memory shows that people remember their past attitudes as more consistent with their current attitudes than they actually were. If you changed your mind about a political issue, you are likely to misremember your previous position as closer to your current one. If you have been through therapy and feel better, you may retrospectively exaggerate how bad you felt before, creating a narrative of greater improvement.

This is not deliberate dishonesty. It is an automatic feature of reconstructive memory. When you reconstruct a past event, your current state serves as an anchor, and the reconstruction is biased toward consistency with that anchor. The result is a personal history that feels coherent and continuous, even if it has been substantially revised.

The "flashbulb memory" literature illustrates this nicely. People report vivid, detailed, seemingly photographic memories of where they were when they learned of major events: the September 11 attacks, a presidential assassination, a natural disaster. These memories feel special, preserved in amber. But when researchers check flashbulb memories against accounts recorded shortly after the event, they find substantial distortions. People confidently misremember where they were, who told them, what they were doing. The emotional intensity of the event creates a feeling of memorial accuracy without producing actual accuracy. The confidence is real; the content is partly fabricated.

Motivated memory also operates at the group level. Collective memories, the shared narratives that communities construct about their past, are shaped by current group identity and goals. Nations remember their history in ways that serve national self-image. Organizations remember their founding stories in ways that reinforce current values. These collective reconstructions are not just individual memory errors aggregated; they are actively maintained through social processes of shared narration, selective commemoration, and institutional reinforcement.

## Case Study: The Innocence Project

The Innocence Project, founded in 1992 by Barry Scheck and Peter Neufeld at the Cardozo School of Law, uses DNA evidence to exonerate people who were wrongly convicted of crimes. As of the mid-2020s, the project and affiliated organizations have helped exonerate more than 375 individuals.

The most sobering finding from this work: eyewitness misidentification was a contributing factor in approximately 69 percent of the convictions that were later overturned by DNA evidence. This is not a marginal effect. Eyewitness misidentification is the single leading cause of wrongful conviction in the United States.

The cases are instructive. In many, the eyewitness was confident, detailed, and sincere. They believed they were identifying the right person. The memory felt real. The problem was not lying; it was the nature of memory as reconstruction.

Consider the case of Ronald Cotton, convicted of rape in 1985 on the basis of confident eyewitness identification by the victim, Jennifer Thompson. Thompson was certain. She had studied her attacker's face during the assault, determined to remember it. She picked Cotton from a photo lineup and then from a live lineup. She testified at trial with absolute confidence.

Cotton spent over ten years in prison before DNA evidence proved he was innocent and identified the actual perpetrator. When Thompson later saw the real attacker, she did not recognize him. Her memory had been reconstructed around Cotton's face from the moment she picked him from the photo lineup.

Thompson and Cotton later became friends and advocates for eyewitness identification reform. Their story illustrates something important: the victim was not careless, stupid, or dishonest. She did everything a witness is supposed to do. The system failed because it was built on the assumption that confident, detailed memories are accurate, an assumption that the science of memory does not support.

## What This Gets Right / What's Still Open

**What the science gets right:** Memory is reconstructive. Post-event information can alter memories. Entire false memories can be created. Confidence is a poor marker of accuracy, especially when contaminated by post-identification feedback. Eyewitness misidentification is a major cause of wrongful conviction. These findings are well replicated, practically important, and have already driven policy changes.

**What's still open:** The mechanism of the misinformation effect is still debated: overwriting vs. coexistence vs. gap-filling. The boundary conditions of false memory implantation are not fully mapped. How extreme can the false event be before people resist it? The role of individual differences is underexplored; some people are clearly more susceptible to false memories than others, but we do not fully understand why. The relationship between memory's constructive nature and its general reliability needs more theoretical development; most of the time memory works well enough, and we need to understand when and why it fails, not just that it can fail.

## Real-World Application: The Cognitive Interview and Minimizing Memory Contamination

Given everything we know about memory's malleability, how should we collect eyewitness evidence? The answer is the Cognitive Interview, developed by Ronald Fisher and Edward Geiselman in the 1980s and refined since.

The Cognitive Interview is based on four main techniques, each grounded in memory research:

**Mental reinstatement of context.** Ask the witness to mentally recreate the environment of the original event: what they saw, heard, felt, where they were standing. This leverages the encoding specificity principle: memory is best when retrieval conditions match encoding conditions.

**Report everything.** Encourage the witness to report every detail, no matter how trivial or uncertain, without editing or self-censoring. Small details may serve as retrieval cues for larger memories.

**Recall in different orders.** Ask the witness to recall the event in reverse chronological order or starting from different points. This disrupts schema-driven reconstruction (which tends to fill in expected details) and can surface details that forward narration misses.

**Change perspectives.** Ask the witness to recall the event from a different physical viewpoint, for example from the perspective of another person who was present. This can trigger additional retrieval cues.

The Cognitive Interview also involves things the interviewer should *not* do: do not ask leading questions, do not interrupt, do not provide feedback about the "correctness" of responses, do not repeat the interview in ways that allow post-event information to accumulate.

Meta-analyses consistently show that the Cognitive Interview elicits more correct information than standard police interviews without a meaningful increase in false information. It is now used, in various adapted forms, by police forces in the UK, Australia, and parts of the US and Europe.

Beyond policing, the principles of minimizing memory contamination apply in any organizational context where accurate recall matters. Accident investigations, medical error reviews, and workplace incident reports all benefit from the same principles: interview people separately before they can contaminate each other's memories, ask open-ended questions before specific ones, avoid leading language, and collect accounts as soon as possible before post-event information accumulates.
