# Chapter 16: Concepts, Categories, and Mental Imagery

What is a bird?

You answered that almost instantly. You did not consult a dictionary, run through a list of features, or perform any conscious analysis. Something in your mind responded to the word "bird" with a rich, structured representation, a cluster of knowledge about wings and feathers and flight and nests and songs, and you felt confident you knew what a bird was.

Now: is a penguin a bird? An ostrich? A bat?

Your confidence shifted. You probably said yes to penguin and ostrich, but you may have hesitated, and you almost certainly felt that a robin is somehow more "birdy" than a penguin. As for a bat: no, not a bird, but you can feel the pull. It flies, after all. Something about it is bird-adjacent even though you know it is a mammal.

This is strange if you think about it. If "bird" were a clearly defined box, a set of boundaries that something either falls inside or outside, then every bird should feel equally bird-like. A penguin should be just as good an example as a robin. The fact that it is not tells us something fundamental about how the mind organizes knowledge. Categories are not simple containers. They have internal structure, fuzzy edges, and a richness that resists easy formalization.

This chapter is about two related questions. First: how do we form and use concepts, the mental representations that let us carve the world into categories? Second: what is the nature of mental imagery, the pictures, sounds, and spatial representations that seem to populate our inner mental life? These questions turn out to be connected in ways that were not apparent when researchers first started studying them.

---

## The Classical View: Necessary and Sufficient Conditions

The oldest theory of concepts, inherited from Aristotle and formalized in philosophy and early cognitive science, is what we now call the classical view. On this account, a concept is defined by a set of necessary and sufficient conditions. Something is a bachelor if and only if it is an unmarried adult male. Something is a triangle if and only if it is a closed, three-sided polygon. Membership is all-or-nothing. Either you meet the conditions or you do not.

This works beautifully for some concepts — mostly formal, human-invented ones. Mathematical concepts have crisp definitions. Legal categories (at least in principle) are specified by statutes. But try to write necessary and sufficient conditions for "game." Ludwig Wittgenstein famously made this challenge in his *Philosophical Investigations* (1953), arguing that the members of the category "game" are linked by family resemblance rather than shared features. Some games involve competition, but not all (think of a child throwing a ball against a wall). Some involve rules, but not all. Some involve fun, but is professional football always fun for the players?

The problem deepens with natural categories, the kinds of things the world actually contains. Try to define "bird" with necessary and sufficient conditions. Flies? Penguins don't. Has feathers? That works for birds, but it is a single feature, not the rich structured concept you actually possess. Has wings? So do bats and airplanes. Lays eggs? So do reptiles and fish.

By the 1970s, it was clear to many researchers that the classical view could not account for how people actually use natural categories. What replaced it has shaped how we think about concepts ever since.

---

## Prototype Theory: Categories Have a Center

Eleanor Rosch's work in the 1970s transformed the study of concepts. Her central insight was that natural categories are organized not around boundaries but around prototypes, the best and most typical examples of the category.

Rosch demonstrated that category membership comes in degrees of typicality. When you ask people to rate how good an example various items are of a category, you get highly consistent gradients. A robin is a very typical bird. A chicken is less typical. A penguin is even less typical. An ostrich is a marginal case. These typicality ratings are not idiosyncratic; different people, tested at different times, produce very similar rankings.

More importantly, typicality has measurable cognitive consequences. People verify typical members faster ("Is a robin a bird?" gets a quicker "yes" than "Is a penguin a bird?"). They list typical members first when asked to generate examples. Children learn typical members earlier. In memory experiments, typical members are more likely to be falsely recalled as having been present.

The prototype itself can be understood as a summary representation, a statistical abstraction of the central tendency of the category's members. It is the "average" bird, the bird that has the most features shared by the most other birds. You may never have encountered a creature that perfectly matches the prototype, but the prototype still organizes your category.

Rosch also showed that categories are organized at different levels of abstraction, and one level, the basic level, is psychologically privileged. You are faster to identify something as a "dog" than as an "animal" (too general) or a "golden retriever" (too specific). Basic-level categories correspond to the level at which members share the most features with each other and the fewest with members of contrasting categories.

### What Prototypes Do Not Explain

Prototype theory was a major advance, but it has gaps. It struggles with concepts that have disjunctive structures, where members share no central tendency but fall into distinct clusters. "Things you would take out of a burning house" is a perfectly usable category, but there is no prototype for it. It also has difficulty with compositional concepts. A "pet fish" is typically a goldfish, but a goldfish is neither a typical pet nor a typical fish. How does the prototype emerge from the combination?

## Exemplar Theory: Remembering Rather Than Abstracting

Exemplar theory offers a different answer. Instead of abstracting a prototype from your experience, you store individual examples (exemplars) and categorize new items by comparing them to your library of stored instances.

On this view, when you decide whether something is a bird, you do not compare it to an abstract prototype. You compare it to specific birds you remember: that robin in your backyard, the pigeons downtown, the parrot at the pet store. The new item's similarity to these stored exemplars determines whether and how confidently you classify it.

This approach neatly explains some things prototypes cannot. It naturally accounts for sensitivity to the variability within a category: you know that birds come in very different shapes and sizes, and you use that knowledge. It handles atypical members better, because those members are stored as exemplars too. And it explains how the same person might categorize differently depending on context: in a bird-watching context, you retrieve different exemplars than in a cooking context.

The debate between prototype and exemplar models has been technically sophisticated, with formal mathematical models tested against behavioral data. The current consensus, if there is one, is that both mechanisms likely contribute. For well-learned, large categories, prototypes may dominate. For smaller categories or those learned from few examples, exemplar-based processing may be more important.

## Theory-Theory: Concepts Embedded in Explanations

A third approach argues that concepts are not similarity-based at all. They are embedded in naive theories about how the world works. On this theory-theory view, what makes something a bird is not that it is similar to a prototype or to stored exemplars, but that it has a certain kind of biological essence (bird DNA, bird ancestry) that your intuitive biological theory says is what matters.

This explains a puzzle for similarity-based accounts. A person in a realistic bird costume is very similar to a bird (feathered, winged, appropriately shaped), but you would never classify them as a bird. Your theory about biological kinds tells you that surface similarity is irrelevant; what matters is deep causal structure.

The theory-theory also explains developmental changes in categorization. Young children categorize largely by appearance, but as they develop naive theories about biology, psychology, and physics, their categories shift to respect deeper features. A child who initially groups whales with fish (because they look alike and live in water) eventually reclassifies whales as mammals once they acquire enough biological theory.

The downside is vagueness. Calling concepts "theories" raises all the same questions about what theories are and how they are represented. But the insight that concepts are not isolated similarity detectors, that they connect to broader explanatory frameworks, has been widely influential.

## Mental Imagery: Pictures in the Mind?

Close your eyes and picture a beach. If you are like most people, something happened. You experienced a visual scene, perhaps with waves, sand, blue sky. This mental image seems to have spatial properties. The water is to one side, the sand to another. If asked to "zoom in" on a seashell, you feel as though you are inspecting a picture.

But is it really a picture? This question generated one of the most intense debates in cognitive science.

### Mental Rotation: Images Behave Like Objects

In 1971, Roger Shepard and Jacqueline Metzler published a clever experiment. They showed participants pairs of three-dimensional block figures and asked whether the two figures were the same object rotated or different objects. The key finding: response time was a linear function of the angle of rotation between the two figures. The more one figure was rotated relative to the other, the longer it took to decide.

This linearity was striking. It suggested that participants were mentally rotating an internal representation, and that the rotation passed through intermediate orientations — just as physically rotating an object would. If mental representations were purely abstract and propositional (like sentences in a language of thought), there is no reason rotation time should scale linearly with angle. The finding supported the idea that mental images are analog representations — they preserve spatial structure rather than merely describing it.

Subsequent research extended this to other spatial operations. Stephen Kosslyn showed that it takes longer to mentally scan across a larger image, that small mental images are harder to "inspect" for details (just as small physical pictures are), and that the time to scan between two points on a mental map correlates with the actual distance between them.

### The Imagery Debate: Kosslyn vs. Pylyshyn

These findings set the stage for what became known as the imagery debate. Stephen Kosslyn argued for a depictive account: mental images are representations in a spatial medium, functioning like pictures on a screen. They preserve spatial relationships, have a limited extent (like a visual display), and are processed by some of the same neural mechanisms as actual visual perception.

Zenon Pylyshyn countered with a propositional account. He argued that the apparently pictorial properties of mental imagery are epiphenomenal, by-products of underlying propositional representations. On his view, when you "scan" across a mental image, you are not literally traversing a spatial representation. You are accessing propositional knowledge about spatial relationships, and the scanning time reflects your tacit knowledge about how long scanning *would* take if you were looking at a real picture.

Pylyshyn raised a deep concern: if you tell participants to imagine an object and then ask them how long it takes to scan across it, of course they take longer for larger distances. They know how perception works and simulate it. The question is whether the spatial properties are intrinsic to the representation or imported by the process of using it.

The debate moved forward with neuroimaging. Kosslyn and others showed that visual mental imagery activates early visual cortex, areas with retinotopic organization where the spatial layout of the visual field is physically mapped onto brain tissue. This was harder to explain on a purely propositional account. If imagery were just propositions, why would it activate brain regions whose defining feature is spatial organization?

Most researchers today accept some version of the depictive account, at least for visual imagery. But the details remain contested, and Pylyshyn's core challenge (how do you distinguish intrinsic spatial properties from simulated ones?) has never been fully resolved.

## Aphantasia and the Spectrum of Imagery

In 2015, Adam Zeman and colleagues gave a name to a phenomenon that people had described for over a century: aphantasia, the inability to form voluntary mental images. People with aphantasia report no visual experience when asked to imagine a beach, a friend's face, or their childhood bedroom. Their mind's eye is, as many describe it, blank.

This is not a memory deficit. People with aphantasia can answer questions about what things look like; they know that a stop sign is red and octagonal. They can navigate, recognize faces, and succeed in visually demanding professions (there are aphantasic architects, designers, and artists). What they lack is the subjective visual experience of imagining.

At the other end sits hyperphantasia, imagery so vivid it approaches perceptual experience. People with hyperphantasia describe mental images with photographic clarity, rich color, and fine detail.

### What Aphantasia Reveals

Aphantasia is scientifically valuable because it is a natural experiment. If people can function well without mental imagery, then imagery cannot be essential for every cognitive task that we assumed it supported. This forces us to reconsider the role of imagery in cognition.

Research suggests that aphantasic individuals rely more heavily on spatial and propositional representations. They may solve mental rotation tasks using different strategies, such as abstract reasoning about spatial relationships rather than visualizing the rotation. Their memory tends to be less episodic and more semantic: they remember facts about events rather than reliving them visually.

Individual differences in imagery vividness are now understood to be genuine and large. For decades, some researchers dismissed self-reports of imagery differences as unreliable. But converging evidence from behavioral tasks, neuroimaging, and physiological measures (people with vivid imagery show pupil constriction when imagining bright scenes, just as they would with real brightness) confirms that these differences are real.

## Primary Text Spotlight: Rosch (1975), "Cognitive Representations of Semantic Categories"

Eleanor Rosch's 1975 paper in the *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General* is the study that dismantled the classical view and established prototype theory as a serious alternative. It is worth understanding what she actually did and found.

Rosch asked participants to rate, on a seven-point scale, how good an example various items were of superordinate categories like "furniture," "vehicle," "fruit," and "bird." The results were highly consistent across participants. For "bird," robin and sparrow were rated as the best examples; chicken, duck, and goose were intermediate; penguin, ostrich, and bat (included as a non-member) were rated poorly.

She then showed that these typicality ratings predicted performance on a verification task. When people were asked to judge whether a sentence like "A robin is a bird" or "A penguin is a bird" was true or false, they responded faster for typical members. The effect was large and robust.

Rosch also examined priming effects. Hearing the category name "bird" facilitated recognition of the word "robin" more than recognition of "penguin." The prototype, in other words, was not just a judgment. It was an organizing principle that influenced processing across multiple tasks.

**What makes this paper landmark material** is the theoretical shift it catalyzed, beyond the findings themselves. Before Rosch, the dominant assumption was that categories had sharp boundaries defined by rules. After Rosch, the field recognized that most natural categories are graded, that typicality is a fundamental property of how categories are represented, and that theories of concepts must explain this internal structure.

**Epistemic status: Robust.** Typicality effects have been replicated extensively across languages, cultures, and paradigms. The basic phenomenon is not in doubt. What remains debated is the best theoretical explanation: whether prototypes, exemplars, or theories best account for the data.

## What This Gets Right / What's Still Open

**What we can be fairly confident about:**

- Natural categories have graded internal structure. Typicality effects are real, large, and replicable.
- Category membership is not purely about similarity; deeper causal and theoretical knowledge matters.
- Mental imagery has genuine spatial/analog properties, at least in most people. The evidence from neuroimaging and behavioral tasks converges on this.
- Individual differences in imagery vividness are real and have cognitive consequences.
- The Shepard & Metzler mental rotation effect is one of the most robust findings in cognitive psychology.

**What remains open:**

- The prototype vs. exemplar debate has not been decisively resolved. Both mechanisms likely contribute, but we lack a unified account of when and how.
- The relationship between concepts and language is contested. Do you need language to have concepts? Preverbal infants and nonhuman animals categorize, suggesting no, but language may transform the kinds of concepts that are possible.
- Whether aphantasia represents a different cognitive architecture or a difference in metacognitive access to imagery processes that are actually intact. Some evidence suggests aphantasic individuals show imagery-related neural activation even when they report no subjective imagery.
- How concepts compose, specifically how "pet" and "fish" combine to produce a concept whose prototype (goldfish) differs from either component's prototype, remains an unsolved problem.

## Real-World Application: When Categories Shape Decisions

Categorization is not just an academic curiosity. It shapes consequential decisions in medicine, law, and beyond.

### Medical Diagnosis

Diagnosis is, at its core, a categorization problem. A patient presents with symptoms, and a physician must assign them to a diagnostic category. Research shows that physicians, like everyone else, are influenced by typicality. A patient whose presentation closely matches the prototypical case of a disease gets diagnosed faster and more accurately. Atypical presentations, such as women having heart attacks without chest pain, are missed more often because they do not match the prototype.

This is not a failure of competence. It is a feature of how human categorization works, operating as expected but in a context where the design has costs.

### Legal Judgment

Legal categories are supposed to be classical, defined by statutes with necessary and sufficient conditions. But jurors and judges are human beings using prototype-based cognition. Research by James Hampton and others shows that legal judgments are influenced by typicality. A robbery at gunpoint feels like a more prototypical "robbery" than a complex financial fraud, even if both meet the legal definition. This affects sentencing, jury verdicts, and how cases are prosecuted.

### AI Classification Errors

Modern AI classification systems (deep neural networks) make categorization errors, but their errors are systematically different from human errors. A network might confidently classify an image of static as a "school bus" — a mistake no human would make. Conversely, humans are fooled by adversarial examples that networks handle fine. Understanding both systems' error patterns helps us design better human-AI collaborations and reveals something about the nature of categorization itself. Human categories are organized around explanatory and functional knowledge; neural network categories are organized around statistical regularities in pixel patterns. The difference matters.
