# Chapter 3: A Brief and Honest History of Cognitive Science

## Opening: Why Are We Still Arguing About the 1960s?

Here is something that might surprise you about cognitive science: researchers in 2026 are still actively debating ideas that were first articulated in the 1950s. The nature of mental representation, the relationship between language and thought, whether cognition is computation at bottom — these are not settled questions. They are live controversies, argued with data and formal models, by researchers who disagree sharply.

This might seem like a sign that the field is stuck. It is not. It is a sign that the field is young, that its central questions are hard, and that the first wave of answers, however brilliant, were not complete.

Understanding the history of cognitive science is not an antiquarian exercise. It is the fastest way to understand why the field looks the way it does today: why certain assumptions are baked into its methods, why certain debates keep recurring, and why some ideas that were once considered radical are now mainstream while others that were once mainstream are now questioned.

This chapter moves roughly chronologically, but the goal is not to produce a timeline. It is to show how the field's central ideas emerged, were challenged, evolved, and in some cases collapsed.

## The Cognitive Revolution and Its Discontents (1950s-1970s)

### What Behaviorism Got Right (and What It Could Not Handle)

As we discussed in Chapter 1, behaviorism dominated experimental psychology for the first half of the twentieth century. It is easy, in hindsight, to dismiss behaviorism as obviously wrong. But it was not obviously wrong at the time, and it had genuine virtues.

Behaviorism imposed methodological discipline on a field that badly needed it. The introspectionists who preceded the behaviorists had no reliable way to settle disagreements. If two observers reported different experiences while looking at the same stimulus, there was no independent check on who was right. The behaviorists insisted that scientific psychology must be grounded in publicly observable data. That insistence was correct, even if their conclusion — that only stimulus-response associations were worth studying, was too restrictive.

The restrictiveness became untenable as researchers confronted phenomena that resisted behaviorist explanation. Language was the most famous case (Chomsky's 1959 review of Skinner), but there were others. Edward Tolman's experiments on latent learning in rats showed that animals could learn the layout of a maze without reinforcement. They formed "cognitive maps" that only manifested in behavior when there was a reason to use them. This was difficult to explain without positing internal representations.

### The Founding Moment

The cognitive revolution did not happen in a single moment, but if you had to pick one, September 11, 1956 is a strong candidate. On that day, at a symposium at MIT, Noam Chomsky presented his theory of transformational grammar, George Miller discussed the capacity limits of short-term memory, and Allen Newell and Herbert Simon demonstrated the Logic Theorist. Three papers, one day, three pillars of what would become cognitive science.

The timing was not coincidental. Several enabling conditions had converged. Information theory, developed by Claude Shannon in 1948, provided a mathematical framework for talking about information processing. The digital computer, still new, provided both a tool for modeling cognition and a metaphor for understanding it. And the shortcomings of behaviorism had created a vacuum that needed filling.

### The Classical Architecture

The framework that emerged from the cognitive revolution is sometimes called the "classical" or "symbolic" architecture of cognition. Its core commitments were:

1. **Mental representations exist.** The mind contains internal states (symbols, propositions, images, rules) that represent aspects of the world.
2. **Mental processes are computations over those representations.** Thinking is symbol manipulation, governed by rules that operate on the structure of the symbols.
3. **The mind can be studied at an abstract level.** You do not need to understand neuroscience to understand cognition, any more than you need to understand transistors to understand software. (This was the "functionalist" position in philosophy of mind.)

This framework was enormously productive. It generated detailed theories of memory (Atkinson-Shiffrin's multi-store model), attention (Broadbent's filter theory), language (Chomsky's generative grammar), and problem-solving (Newell and Simon's problem space theory). By the 1970s, cognitive psychology was the dominant paradigm, and the main outlines of cognitive science as an interdisciplinary field were in place.

### Early Discontents

But even in its early years, the classical framework attracted criticism. Hubert Dreyfus, a philosopher at MIT and later Berkeley, published *What Computers Can't Do* in 1972, arguing that human intelligence depends on embodied, situated know-how that cannot be captured in formal rules. His critique was widely dismissed at the time (he was, after all, a philosopher telling computer scientists their enterprise was misguided), but many of his arguments have aged well.

J.J. Gibson, an ecological psychologist, offered a different challenge. Gibson argued that perception does not require internal representations at all. The information needed to guide behavior is available directly in the environment, in what Gibson called "affordances," and the job of the perceptual system is to pick up that information, not to construct a representation from impoverished input. Gibson's ideas were marginal in the 1970s but have become increasingly influential in the 4E cognition movement.

## The Connectionist Challenge (1980s-1990s)

### The Rise of Neural Networks

In 1986, David Rumelhart, James McClelland, and the PDP Research Group published *Parallel Distributed Processing*, a two-volume work that changed the landscape of cognitive science. They proposed a radically different architecture for cognition: instead of symbolic rules operating on discrete representations, cognition could be modeled as patterns of activation in networks of simple processing units inspired by neurons.

These connectionist (or neural network) models had several attractive properties:

- **Graceful degradation.** Damage a few units and the network's performance declines gradually, much like a brain with minor damage. Damage a rule in a symbolic system and the system fails catastrophically.
- **Learning.** Connectionist networks could learn from experience using algorithms like backpropagation, rather than having rules programmed in by a designer.
- **Capturing regularities.** Networks were good at learning statistical patterns, the kind of fuzzy, probabilistic regularities that pervade natural language and perception.

### The Debate

The debate between symbolism and connectionism was one of the most heated in the history of cognitive science. Jerry Fodor and Zenon Pylyshyn published a famous critique in 1988, arguing that connectionist models could not, in principle, account for the systematicity and compositionality of thought. If you can think "John loves Mary," you can also think "Mary loves John." This compositionality, Fodor and Pylyshyn argued, requires structured, symbol-like representations — exactly what connectionism lacked.

Connectionists responded that their models could, with appropriate architecture, capture compositional structure. The debate was never cleanly resolved. In practice, the field moved toward a pragmatic middle ground: some phenomena are better modeled with symbolic approaches, others with connectionist ones, and many require hybrid architectures.

**Epistemic status: Contested.** Whether cognition is symbolic, connectionist, or something else entirely remains an open question. The recent success of deep learning (a descendant of connectionism) in AI has reignited the debate, but scaling up a model does not settle the theoretical question of what kind of computation the mind performs.

## Cognitive Neuroscience and the Brain Imaging Era (1990s-2000s)

### The Decade of the Brain

In 1990, President George H.W. Bush declared the 1990s the "Decade of the Brain." This was partly political theater, but it reflected a real shift. The development of functional neuroimaging, especially fMRI, gave cognitive scientists a new way to study the mind: by watching the brain in action.

The promise was enormous. Instead of inferring mental processes from behavioral data alone, you could now see which brain regions were active during different tasks. The hope was that neuroimaging would resolve long-standing debates: if two theories predicted different patterns of brain activity, you could look at the data and settle the question.

### What Actually Happened

The reality was more complicated. Neuroimaging did produce genuine insights. It confirmed that many cognitive functions are localized to specific brain regions (though never to a single region; all complex tasks involve networks). It revealed the default mode network, a set of brain regions that are active during rest and mind-wandering, suggesting that the "resting" brain is anything but idle. It also enabled the study of processes that are difficult to study behaviorally, like the neural correlates of consciousness.

But neuroimaging also produced a flood of low-quality studies. The field suffered from small samples, weak statistical methods, and what Ed Vul and colleagues memorably called "voodoo correlations," impossibly high correlations between brain activity and behavior that resulted from circular analysis practices.

A 2009 paper by Craig Bennett demonstrated the problem with delicious absurdity: Bennett put a dead Atlantic salmon in an fMRI scanner, showed it photographs of humans in social situations, and — using standard analysis methods without proper correction for multiple comparisons — found "significant" brain activity. The dead salmon paper was funny, but its point was serious. If your methods can find neural activity in a dead fish, they are not reliable enough to draw conclusions about living humans.

The neuroimaging field has since improved its methods substantially, with larger samples, pre-registration, and more rigorous statistics. But the early period left a legacy of unreliable findings and inflated claims that the field is still working through.

## The Replication Crisis and Credibility Revolution (2010s-Present)

We covered the replication crisis in detail in Chapter 2, so here we focus on its historical significance.

The replication crisis was not just a methodological episode. It was a paradigm-level event that forced cognitive science to confront uncomfortable questions about its own practices. How many of the "established findings" in textbooks would survive rigorous replication? How many theoretical edifices were built on empirical foundations that were weaker than anyone had admitted?

The credibility revolution that followed (pre-registration, open data, Registered Reports, multi-site replication projects) represents a real cultural shift in how cognitive science is practiced. It is arguably the most important methodological development since the cognitive revolution itself.

But the credibility revolution has also created tensions. Some researchers worry that the emphasis on replication and rigor is making the field too conservative, that the bar for publishing has been raised so high that interesting but preliminary findings never see the light of day. Others argue that the old system was producing too many false positives and that the correction was overdue. This tension is not yet resolved.

## The 4E Cognition Movement

Beginning in the 1990s but gaining momentum in the 2000s and 2010s, a cluster of approaches began challenging the classical view that cognition is computation happening inside the head. These approaches are collectively known as 4E cognition:

**Embodied**: Cognition is shaped by the body. Your conceptual system is grounded in sensory and motor experience, not in abstract symbols. When you understand the word "kick," motor areas of your brain are activated. When you reason about time, you use spatial metaphors grounded in bodily experience.

**Embedded**: Cognition is situated in an environment that provides structure and support. You do not solve problems in a vacuum; you use notebooks, diagrams, calculators, and other environmental scaffolds. Understanding cognition requires understanding the context in which it occurs.

**Enacted**: Cognition is not passive information processing but active engagement with the world. Perception is not building an internal model of the environment; it is guiding action within the environment. Thinking is not manipulating internal symbols; it is a form of skillful coping.

**Extended**: In some cases, cognition literally extends beyond the brain. Andy Clark and David Chalmers argued in their influential 1998 paper "The Extended Mind" that if a notebook plays the same functional role as biological memory (you consult it, rely on it, update it), then the notebook is part of your cognitive system.

These ideas are provocative and have generated enormous debate. The strong versions (that cognition literally extends into the environment, that internal representations do not exist) remain controversial. The weaker versions, that the body and environment matter for cognition and that classical models have been too focused on abstract computation, are increasingly accepted.

**Epistemic status: Contested.** The claim that cognition is influenced by embodiment and environment is **Robust**. The stronger claim that cognition is constituted by body-environment interactions, not merely influenced by them, is **Contested**.

## Primary Text Spotlight: Newell & Simon (1976) — "Computer Science as Empirical Inquiry: Symbols and Search"

### The Context

Allen Newell and Herbert Simon were among the founders of artificial intelligence. Their 1976 paper, delivered as the ACM Turing Award lecture, is a foundational document for the symbolic approach to cognitive science, and not only a statement about computer science.

### The Argument

Newell and Simon articulated what they called the "Physical Symbol System Hypothesis": a physical symbol system has the necessary and sufficient means for general intelligent action. In plain language: anything that manipulates symbols according to rules can, in principle, be intelligent, and intelligence requires symbol manipulation.

This was a bold claim. It said that intelligence, whether human, animal, or artificial, is about symbols and rules at bottom. A brain is a physical symbol system. A properly programmed computer is a physical symbol system. The material substrate does not matter; what matters is the computation.

Newell and Simon supported this hypothesis with evidence from their own AI programs (the Logic Theorist, the General Problem Solver) and from laboratory studies of human problem-solving. They showed that people solving problems like cryptarithmetic puzzles and logic problems appeared to use strategies that could be modeled as search through a space of symbolic states.

### What It Got Right

The symbolic approach was enormously productive. It generated testable theories of problem-solving, planning, and reasoning. It led to practical AI systems (expert systems, theorem provers, planning algorithms) that worked in well-defined domains. It also established the idea that cognitive theories should be precise enough to implement as computer programs, a standard that raised the bar for the entire field.

### What It Got Wrong (or at Least Left Out)

The Physical Symbol System Hypothesis, taken at face value, implies that all intelligence reduces to symbol manipulation. This has turned out to be, at minimum, incomplete. The things that are hardest for symbolic AI (perception, motor control, common-sense reasoning, learning from experience) are things that humans do effortlessly. The things that are easiest for symbolic AI (logic, chess, mathematics) are things that humans find difficult.

This inversion, sometimes called Moravec's Paradox, suggests that the symbolic level captures only part of what cognition involves. The connectionist and 4E critiques, whatever their own limitations, correctly identified gaps in the symbolic picture.

**Epistemic status:** The Physical Symbol System Hypothesis as originally stated is **Contested** to **Fragile**. That symbol manipulation is part of cognition is Robust. That it is the whole story is increasingly doubted.

## What This Gets Right / What's Still Open

The history of cognitive science is a story of productive ideas that turned out to be incomplete. Behaviorism's insistence on rigor was correct, but its rejection of mental states was too extreme. The symbolic approach's emphasis on formal precision was valuable, but its neglect of the body and environment was a limitation. Connectionism's emphasis on learning and neural plausibility was important, but it struggled with the systematic structure of thought. Neuroimaging's promise of bridging mind and brain was real, but the early execution was often sloppy.

What remains open:

- **The nature of mental representation.** Are mental representations symbolic, distributed, embodied, or something we do not yet have a good framework for? This may be the deepest open question in cognitive science.
- **The neural code.** How does neural activity encode information? We know a lot about individual neurons and about large-scale brain networks, but the intermediate level, how populations of neurons represent and transform information, remains poorly understood.
- **Consciousness.** We have no widely accepted theory of why some neural processes are accompanied by conscious experience and others are not. This is not only a philosophical puzzle; it has practical implications for understanding anesthesia, disorders of consciousness, and the moral status of AI systems.
- **The language of thought.** Do we think in a language-like code, as Fodor argued? Or is thought non-linguistic at its core, with language serving as a tool for communication rather than cognition? Recent work on people with severe aphasia who retain reasoning abilities has revived this question.
- **How to integrate the levels.** Marr's three levels of analysis remain useful, but we still lack a general framework for bridging between the computational level (what the mind does) and the implementational level (how the brain does it).

## Real-World Application: History as a Lesson in Paradigm Formation and Failure

The history of cognitive science is useful not just for understanding the field but as a case study in how scientific paradigms form, succeed, and fail. Several patterns recur:

**The pendulum pattern.** The field swings between extremes: from behaviorism's denial of inner states to cognitivism's neglect of behavior, from symbolic AI's neglect of the body to embodied cognition's skepticism of representation. The truth tends to be somewhere in between, but finding that middle ground requires first exploring the extremes.

**The metaphor trap.** Each era adopts a dominant metaphor (the telephone switchboard, the digital computer, the neural network, the Bayesian inference engine), and the metaphor shapes what researchers notice and what they ignore. Metaphors are useful starting points, but they become traps when they are mistaken for the thing itself.

**The technology effect.** New technologies do not only provide data; they shape the questions researchers ask. The computer made information-processing models thinkable. fMRI made brain localization the default research question. Large language models are currently reshaping questions about language and meaning. In each case, the technology opened some doors and closed others.

**The replication correction.** Periods of rapid discovery tend to be followed by periods of reassessment, as the field discovers that some of its "discoveries" were artifacts of weak methods. This is not a sign of failure; it is how science self-corrects. But the correction is always painful for the researchers whose work does not survive it.

Understanding these patterns will not make you immune to them. Even researchers who study the history of science are subject to the same cognitive biases as everyone else. But awareness helps. When you encounter a bold new framework that seems to explain everything, you can ask: what is this framework's metaphor? What is it not looking at? How strong is the evidence base, really? Those questions, informed by history, are among the most useful tools a reader of cognitive science can possess.
