# Appendix A: Glossary of Core Terms

This glossary defines the key terms used throughout the book. Each entry gives a plain-language definition and points you to the chapter(s) where the term is introduced or discussed in depth. Terms are alphabetical. Where a concept appears under multiple names in the literature, the most common variant is listed with cross-references.

---

**Affordance.** A property of the environment, relative to an organism, that offers a possibility for action. A chair affords sitting; a handle affords grasping. The concept comes from James Gibson and is central to ecological approaches to perception. (Chapters 4, 6, 25)

**Aphantasia.** The inability to form voluntary mental images. People with aphantasia report no "mind's eye" (they cannot visualize a beach or a friend's face) yet they perform surprisingly well on many tasks that seem to require imagery. (Chapter 16)

**Attentional blink.** A brief period (roughly 200-500 ms) after detecting a target in a rapid stream of stimuli during which a second target is likely to be missed. Demonstrates that attention has a temporal bottleneck, not just a spatial one. (Chapter 5)

**Bayesian brain.** The hypothesis that the brain performs something approximating Bayesian inference, combining prior expectations with incoming sensory evidence to form perceptions, beliefs, and decisions. Closely related to predictive processing. (Chapters 4, 17)

**Binding problem.** The question of how the brain combines separately processed features (color, shape, motion, location) into unified perceptual objects. You see a red ball moving left, not free-floating redness, roundness, and leftward motion. (Chapters 4, 5)

**Bottom-up processing.** Perceptual processing driven by incoming sensory data, as opposed to expectations or prior knowledge. Sometimes called "data-driven" processing. Contrast with top-down processing. (Chapter 4)

**Categorical perception.** The phenomenon in which a continuous physical dimension (such as a color spectrum or a speech sound continuum) is perceived as falling into discrete categories with sharp boundaries between them. (Chapters 4, 13, 15)

**Central executive.** In Baddeley's working memory model, the component responsible for controlling attention, coordinating information from the phonological loop and visuospatial sketchpad, and managing task-switching. Not a single brain region but a set of executive functions. (Chapter 9)

**Change blindness.** The failure to detect even large changes in a visual scene when the change coincides with a brief disruption (a flicker, a saccade, a cut in a film). Demonstrates that we represent far less of our visual environment than we intuitively believe. (Chapter 5)

**Chunking.** The process of grouping individual items into larger meaningful units ("chunks") to increase the effective capacity of working memory. The letters F, B, and I are three items; the chunk "FBI" is one. (Chapter 9)

**Cognitive map.** An internal representation of spatial relationships in an environment, originally proposed by Edward Tolman. Now known to involve hippocampal place cells and entorhinal grid cells. (Chapter 7)

**Cognitive reserve.** The idea that education, intellectual engagement, and other life experiences build a buffer against cognitive decline, allowing the brain to tolerate more damage before showing symptoms. The mechanisms remain debated. (Chapter 24)

**Confirmation bias.** The tendency to seek out, interpret, and remember information in ways that confirm pre-existing beliefs, while giving less weight to disconfirming evidence. One of the most reliably documented reasoning biases. (Chapter 17)

**Connectionism.** An approach to modeling cognition using artificial neural networks: large numbers of simple, interconnected processing units that learn by adjusting connection weights. Also called parallel distributed processing (PDP). (Chapters 3, 26)

**Constructed emotion.** Lisa Feldman Barrett's theory that emotions are not biologically basic categories (anger, fear, happiness) triggered by dedicated neural circuits, but are constructed by the brain from more general ingredients: interoception, prior experience, and conceptual knowledge. (Chapter 21)

**Critical period.** A developmental window during which certain experiences must occur for normal development. The strongest evidence is for language acquisition and visual development. After the window closes, learning is possible but significantly harder. (Chapters 14, 23)

**Crystallized intelligence.** Accumulated knowledge and skills: vocabulary, factual knowledge, expertise. Tends to remain stable or increase with age, in contrast to fluid intelligence. (Chapter 24)

**Decay.** The hypothesis that memories fade over time simply as a function of time passing, independent of interference. Whether pure decay exists (as opposed to interference) remains debated, especially for short-term memory. (Chapters 9, 10)

**Deliberate practice.** Effortful, structured practice aimed at improving specific aspects of performance, typically with feedback. Distinguished from mere repetition. The original research by Ericsson is more nuanced than the popular "10,000 hours" narrative suggests. (Chapter 19)

**Dual-process theory.** The broad claim that cognition involves two types of processing: fast, automatic, effortless (Type 1 / "System 1") and slow, deliberate, effortful (Type 2 / "System 2"). Useful as a heuristic; the details of the underlying mechanisms are contested. (Chapters 17, 18)

**Ecological rationality.** The idea, associated with Gerd Gigerenzer, that heuristics are not cognitive failures but well-adapted strategies that perform well in the environments where they evolved. A heuristic that fails a logic test may still be rational in the real world. (Chapter 18)

**Ego depletion.** The hypothesis that self-control draws on a limited resource that is depleted by use, like a muscle that tires. Once a cornerstone of willpower research, the effect has largely failed to replicate in large pre-registered studies. (Chapters 18, 19; Appendix B)

**Embodied cognition.** A family of theories arguing that cognition is shaped by, and in some cases constituted by, the body and its interactions with the environment. Ranges from modest claims (the body influences thought) to radical ones (cognition extends beyond the brain). (Chapter 25)

**Encoding.** The process of transforming perceived information into a memory representation. Encoding can be shallow (surface features) or deep (meaning), with deep encoding generally producing more durable memories. See levels of processing. (Chapter 10)

**Episodic memory.** Memory for specific personal events, situated in a particular time and place ("what happened, where, and when"). Distinguished from semantic memory by Endel Tulving. Depends heavily on the hippocampus. (Chapter 10)

**Executive function.** A set of top-down cognitive control processes, including inhibition, working memory updating, and task-switching, that allow flexible, goal-directed behavior. Linked to prefrontal cortex function. (Chapter 19)

**Extended mind.** The thesis, proposed by Andy Clark and David Chalmers, that cognitive processes can extend beyond the brain and body to include external tools, devices, and environmental structures. If your smartphone plays the same functional role as biological memory, it may count as part of your cognitive system. (Chapter 25)

**False memory.** A memory for an event that did not occur, or that occurred differently from how it is remembered. Not a failure of honesty but a byproduct of memory's constructive nature. Extensively studied by Elizabeth Loftus. (Chapter 11)

**Fitts's Law.** A robust quantitative relationship: the time to move to a target is a function of the distance to the target and the target's size. Faster for closer, larger targets. One of the few genuine laws in cognitive science. (Chapter 6)

**Fluid intelligence.** The capacity to reason, solve novel problems, and identify patterns independent of prior knowledge. Declines with age, typically beginning in the late twenties, though the rate varies widely. (Chapter 24)

**Forward model.** An internal model that predicts the sensory consequences of one's own actions before those consequences arrive. Explains why you cannot tickle yourself: your brain predicts the sensation and cancels it out. (Chapter 6)

**Framing effect.** The finding that the way a choice is described (framed) influences which option people prefer, even when the options are logically equivalent. A treatment described as having a "90% survival rate" is preferred over one with a "10% mortality rate." (Chapter 18)

**Garden-path sentence.** A grammatically correct sentence that leads the reader toward an initial parse that turns out to be wrong, forcing reanalysis. "The horse raced past the barn fell" is the classic example. Reveals the incremental, prediction-driven nature of sentence processing. (Chapter 13)

**Global workspace theory.** Bernard Baars's (later elaborated by Stanislas Dehaene) theory that consciousness arises when information is broadcast widely across the brain via a "global workspace," making it available to many cognitive processes simultaneously. (Chapter 20)

**Grid cell.** A type of neuron in the entorhinal cortex that fires in a regular hexagonal pattern as an animal moves through space, providing a metric coordinate system for navigation. Discovered by the Mosers, Nobel Prize 2014. (Chapter 7)

**Hard problem of consciousness.** David Chalmers's term for the question of why and how physical processes in the brain give rise to subjective experience — why there is "something it is like" to see red or feel pain. Distinguished from the "easy problems" of explaining cognitive functions. (Chapter 20)

**Heuristic.** A mental shortcut or rule of thumb that simplifies decision-making. Heuristics are fast and often effective, but can produce systematic errors (biases) in certain contexts. Examples include the availability heuristic and the representativeness heuristic. (Chapters 17, 18)

**Hippocampus.** A medial temporal lobe structure critical for the formation of new episodic memories, spatial navigation, and relational binding. Damage to the hippocampus (as in patient H.M.) produces severe anterograde amnesia. (Chapters 7, 10, 12)

**Iconic memory.** A very brief (roughly 250-500 ms), high-capacity visual memory store demonstrated by George Sperling's partial report experiments. Information in iconic memory fades rapidly but briefly preserves far more than can be reported. (Chapters 5, 9)

**Implicit bias.** Unconscious associations between social groups and evaluative attributes (e.g., linking a racial group with "dangerous"), typically measured with the Implicit Association Test (IAT). The reliability and predictive validity of implicit bias measures are actively debated. (Chapter 22)

**Inattentional blindness.** The failure to notice a fully visible but unexpected object or event when attention is focused elsewhere. The "invisible gorilla" experiment is the most famous demonstration. (Chapter 5)

**Integrated information theory (IIT).** Giulio Tononi's theory that consciousness is identical to integrated information (phi), the amount of information generated by a system above and beyond its parts. It makes strong, sometimes counterintuitive predictions. (Chapter 20)

**Interference.** The disruption of memory by other information. Proactive interference: old memories impair retrieval of new ones. Retroactive interference: new memories impair retrieval of old ones. A major cause of forgetting. (Chapters 9, 10)

**Internal model.** A neural representation that simulates some aspect of the body or the world, allowing the brain to predict, plan, and interpret sensory input. Forward models and inverse models are subtypes. (Chapters 6, 7)

**Levels of processing.** Craik and Lockhart's framework proposing that deeper, more meaningful processing of information at encoding leads to better memory than shallow processing of surface features. (Chapter 10)

**Lexical access.** The process of retrieving a word's meaning, pronunciation, and grammatical properties from long-term memory during language comprehension or production. Happens in roughly 200 ms and is largely automatic. (Chapter 13)

**Linguistic relativity.** The hypothesis that the language you speak influences how you think. The strong version (language determines thought) is largely rejected. The weak version (language influences certain cognitive processes) has some empirical support. Also called the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis. (Chapter 15)

**Long-term potentiation (LTP).** A persistent strengthening of synaptic connections following repeated stimulation. Widely regarded as a key cellular mechanism underlying learning and memory, though the relationship between LTP and behavioral memory is not simple. (Chapter 10)

**Loss aversion.** The finding that losses loom larger than equivalent gains; losing $100 feels worse than gaining $100 feels good. A central component of prospect theory. The effect is robust but its magnitude and universality are debated. (Chapter 18)

**Mental rotation.** The ability to mentally rotate an image of an object to determine whether it matches another object. Response time increases linearly with the angle of rotation, suggesting an analog spatial process. Demonstrated by Shepard and Metzler. (Chapter 16)

**Metacognition.** Cognition about cognition: thinking about your own thinking. Includes monitoring how well you understand something, judging how confident you are in a memory, and deciding when to seek more information. (Chapters 17, 19)

**Misinformation effect.** The finding that post-event information (leading questions, media reports, other witnesses' accounts) can distort memory for the original event. A robust phenomenon with significant implications for eyewitness testimony. (Chapter 11)

**Mirror neuron.** A neuron that fires both when an animal performs an action and when it observes the same action performed by another. Originally discovered in macaque monkeys. Their role in human cognition (imitation, empathy, language) has been extensively claimed and extensively debated. (Chapters 6, 22)

**Modularity.** Jerry Fodor's proposal that certain cognitive systems (perception, language) are informationally encapsulated modules: fast, automatic, domain-specific processors that operate independently of central cognition. The extent of modularity remains debated. (Chapters 3, 4, 13)

**Neuroplasticity.** The brain's ability to reorganize its structure and function in response to experience, learning, or injury. Real and important, but popular accounts frequently overstate its scope and speed. (Chapters 23, 24)

**Phonological loop.** In Baddeley's working memory model, the component that maintains verbal and acoustic information through subvocal rehearsal. Explains why it is hard to remember long words and why articulatory suppression disrupts verbal memory. (Chapter 9)

**Place cell.** A hippocampal neuron that fires when an animal is in a specific location in its environment. Discovered by John O'Keefe in the 1970s, Nobel Prize 2014. Place cells are a cornerstone of our understanding of spatial cognition. (Chapter 7)

**Predictive processing.** The theory that the brain is fundamentally a prediction machine, constantly generating expectations about incoming sensory data and updating those predictions based on prediction errors. Associated with Karl Friston, Andy Clark, and Jakob Hohwy. The strong version is contested. (Chapters 4, 25, 26)

**Primacy effect.** The tendency to better remember items presented at the beginning of a list, attributed to greater opportunity for rehearsal and encoding into long-term memory. Paired with the recency effect. (Chapters 9, 10)

**Priming.** A change in the processing of a stimulus due to prior exposure to a related stimulus. Semantic priming (reading "doctor" speeds recognition of "nurse") is robust. Social and behavioral priming effects have been much harder to replicate. (Chapters 10, 22)

**Procedural memory.** Memory for skills and habits: how to ride a bicycle, play a piano piece, tie a shoe. Largely implicit: you can execute the skill without being able to articulate the rules you follow. Depends on basal ganglia and cerebellum. (Chapter 10)

**Prospect theory.** Kahneman and Tversky's descriptive theory of decision-making under risk, featuring loss aversion, diminishing sensitivity, and probability weighting. Explains many deviations from expected utility theory. (Chapter 18)

**Prototype theory.** Eleanor Rosch's theory that categories are organized around prototypes — the most typical or representative members. A robin is a more prototypical bird than a penguin. Contrasts with classical (definitional) theories of categories. (Chapter 16)

**Recency effect.** The tendency to better remember items presented at the end of a list, attributed to their availability in working memory at the time of recall. (Chapter 9)

**Reconsolidation.** The hypothesis that when a memory is retrieved, it becomes temporarily labile and must be re-stabilized (reconsolidated), during which process it can be modified. Has implications for trauma therapy but the boundary conditions are still being mapped. (Chapters 10, 11)

**Replication crisis.** The broad realization, accelerating around 2011-2015, that many published findings in psychology and cognitive science fail to replicate when tested by independent labs. The Open Science Collaboration's 2015 report found that only about 36% of psychology studies replicated. (Chapters 2, 26)

**Representativeness heuristic.** Judging the probability of an event by how much it resembles a typical case, rather than by base rates. Leads to errors like the conjunction fallacy (thinking "feminist bank teller" is more probable than "bank teller"). (Chapter 18)

**Schema.** A structured mental representation of a typical situation, event, or concept — a script for what happens at a restaurant, a template for what a bird looks like. Schemas aid comprehension and memory but can introduce distortions. Originally proposed by Bartlett. (Chapters 10, 11, 16)

**Selective attention.** The ability to focus processing on relevant information while ignoring irrelevant information. Studied through dichotic listening, visual search, and filtering tasks. Broadbent, Treisman, and Deutsch and Deutsch proposed competing models. (Chapter 5)

**Semantic memory.** General world knowledge not tied to a specific personal episode: knowing that Paris is the capital of France, that dogs are mammals, that 2 + 2 = 4. Distinguished from episodic memory by Tulving. (Chapter 10)

**Sensorimotor integration.** The process of coordinating sensory input with motor output to produce adaptive behavior. Reaching for a coffee cup requires integrating visual information about the cup's location with proprioceptive information about the arm's current position. (Chapters 6, 8)

**Sleep consolidation.** The process by which memories are stabilized and integrated into long-term storage during sleep, particularly during slow-wave sleep and REM sleep. Supported by robust evidence from both behavioral and neuroimaging studies. (Chapter 12)

**Statistical learning.** The ability to extract patterns and regularities from sensory input, demonstrated even in infants. Jenny Saffran's studies showed that 8-month-olds can learn the transitional probabilities between syllables after only minutes of exposure. (Chapters 14, 23)

**Stroop effect.** The finding that naming the ink color of a color word is slower when the word and ink color conflict (the word "RED" printed in blue ink). Demonstrates the automaticity of reading and the demands of cognitive control. One of the most replicated effects in psychology. (Chapters 5, 19)

**Theory of mind (ToM).** The ability to attribute mental states (beliefs, desires, intentions, knowledge) to other people and to understand that others' mental states may differ from one's own. Also called mentalizing. Studied via false-belief tasks in children. (Chapters 22, 23)

**Top-down processing.** Perceptual processing influenced by prior knowledge, expectations, context, or goals. Explains why you can read sloppy handwriting, hear words in noise, and see faces in clouds. Contrast with bottom-up processing. (Chapter 4)

**Transfer-appropriate processing.** The principle that memory performance is best when the type of processing at retrieval matches the type of processing at encoding. Studying by generating answers leads to better test performance than studying by reading, if the test requires generation. (Chapter 10)

**Typicality.** The degree to which a member of a category resembles the category's prototype. Robins are typical birds; penguins are atypical. Typicality predicts speed and accuracy of categorization, sentence verification, and other cognitive measures. (Chapter 16)

**Visuospatial sketchpad.** In Baddeley's working memory model, the component that maintains and manipulates visual and spatial information, a mental workspace for images, maps, and spatial relationships. (Chapter 9)

**WEIRD.** An acronym for Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, and Democratic — the population from which the vast majority of psychology research participants are drawn. Coined by Henrich, Heine, and Norenzayan to highlight the sampling problem. (Chapters 1, 2, 26)

**Working memory.** The cognitive system responsible for temporarily holding and manipulating information in service of ongoing tasks. Distinguished from short-term memory by its emphasis on active processing, not just storage. Capacity is limited to roughly 3-4 items. (Chapter 9)

*This glossary is not exhaustive. For full discussions of each concept, including the evidence for and against, see the referenced chapters.*
