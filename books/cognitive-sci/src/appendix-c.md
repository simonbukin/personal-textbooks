# Appendix C: Annotated Further Reading by Part

This is not a bibliography. It is a curated guide to what to read next, organized by the six parts of this book. For each part, I have selected 8-12 resources -- books, review articles, and landmark papers -- with honest annotations about what each one delivers, what it assumes of the reader, and any caveats. Where a resource is excellent but flawed, I say so.

---

## Part I: Orientation (Chapters 1-3)

**Bermudez, J.L. (2020). *Cognitive Science: An Introduction to the Science of the Mind* (3rd edition). Cambridge University Press.**
The best traditional textbook in the field. Comprehensive, well-organized, and interdisciplinary in a meaningful way. Assumes no background. The downside is the textbook tone: everything is presented with roughly equal confidence, and the epistemic messiness of the field is smoothed over. Use it as a reference, not a narrative.

**Miller, G.A. (2003). "The cognitive revolution: A historical perspective." *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 7(3), 141-144.**
A short, readable retrospective by one of the founders of cognitive science. Miller reflects on what the "cognitive revolution" actually was and was not. Valuable for understanding the field's self-image. Takes about 20 minutes to read.

**Henrich, J., Heine, S.J., & Norenzayan, A. (2010). "The weirdest people in the world?" *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 33(2-3), 61-83.**
The paper that coined the WEIRD acronym and made the sampling problem impossible to ignore. Densely argued, with extensive commentary from other researchers. The target article is accessible; the commentaries require more background. Essential reading for understanding why cross-cultural work matters.

**Open Science Collaboration. (2015). "Estimating the reproducibility of psychological science." *Science*, 349(6251), aac4716.**
The landmark replication study that shook the field. Technical in places, but the headline findings are clear. Worth reading even if you skip the statistical details, because understanding this paper changes how you evaluate every other paper.

**Kuhn, T.S. (1962/2012). *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (4th edition). University of Chicago Press.**
Not about cognitive science specifically, but essential background for understanding how fields change. Kuhn's concepts of paradigms, normal science, and paradigm shifts are embedded in the DNA of cognitive science discourse. Short, readable, and more nuanced than the summaries suggest.

**Gentner, D. (2019). "Cognitive science is and should be pluralistic." *Topics in Cognitive Science*, 11(4), 884-891.**
A thoughtful essay on why cognitive science resists unification under a single framework, and why that resistance is productive rather than a failure. Short and accessible.

**Newell, A. (1973). "You can't play 20 questions with nature and win." In *Visual Information Processing* (pp. 283-308). Academic Press.**
A classic methodological critique, still relevant, arguing that cognitive science cannot progress by testing isolated binary hypotheses. Newell pushes for unified theories. Dense but rewarding. Understanding this paper sharpens your sense of what the field is trying (and often failing) to do.

## Part II: Perception, Action, and Attention (Chapters 4-8)

**Clark, A. (2013). "Whatever next? Predictive brains, situated agents, and the future of cognitive science." *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 36(3), 181-204.**
The single best introduction to predictive processing as a framework for understanding perception and cognition. Clark writes with unusual clarity for a philosopher. The commentaries are worth skimming for the range of critiques. Assumes basic familiarity with Bayesian ideas.

**Gibson, J.J. (1979). *The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception*. Houghton Mifflin. (Reprinted by Psychology Press, 2014.)**
Gibson's manifesto for ecological perception -- the idea that we perceive affordances directly rather than building internal models. Visionary and frustrating in equal measure. His critique of the "snapshot" model of perception is brilliant; his dismissal of internal representation goes too far for most researchers. Read it to understand the strongest version of the anti-representationalist argument.

**Simons, D.J., & Chabris, C.F. (1999). "Gorillas in our midst: Sustained inattentional blindness for dynamic events." *Perception*, 28(9), 1059-1074.**
The invisible gorilla paper. Short, clear, and one of the most influential demonstrations in attention research. Replicates consistently. The methods section is a model of simplicity. A good "first real paper" for someone new to the literature.

**Wolfe, J.M. (2021). "Guided Search 6.0: An updated model of visual search." *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review*, 28(4), 1060-1092.**
A comprehensive update of one of the most influential computational models of visual attention. Technical but well-written. Useful for understanding how cognitive scientists build and iterate quantitative models. Assumes comfort with basic experimental psychology.

**Shadmehr, R., & Krakauer, J.W. (2008). "A computational neuroanatomy for motor control." *Experimental Brain Research*, 185(3), 359-381.**
An excellent review of motor control, forward models, and the computational framework for understanding how the brain controls movement. Moderately technical. The best single-paper introduction to motor cognition if you want to understand the computational side.

**Moser, E.I., Kropff, E., & Moser, M.-B. (2008). "Place cells, grid cells, and the brain's spatial representation system." *Annual Review of Neuroscience*, 31, 69-89.**
A clear review by the Mosers (before their Nobel Prize) of the spatial navigation system -- place cells, grid cells, head direction cells. Accessible to anyone with basic neuroscience background. The illustrations are particularly helpful.

**Findlay, J.M., & Gilchrist, I.D. (2003). *Active Vision: The Psychology of Looking and Seeing*. Oxford University Press.**
An underappreciated book that takes seriously the idea that perception is an active process involving eye movements, not passive reception. Integrates perception, attention, and action. Academic but readable.

**Fitts, P.M. (1954). "The information capacity of the human motor system in controlling the amplitude of movement." *Journal of Experimental Psychology*, 47(6), 381-391.**
The original Fitts's Law paper. Unusually clear and concise by modern standards. A rare example of a finding in cognitive science that has held up for seventy years with minimal revision. Worth reading for its historical interest and as a model of clean experimental design.

## Part III: Memory (Chapters 9-12)

**Baddeley, A. (2007). *Working Memory, Thought, and Action*. Oxford University Press.**
Baddeley's own synthesis of his working memory model, including the episodic buffer. Written for an academic audience but accessibly. The best single source for understanding the multi-component model from the person who built it. Does not engage enough with competing models.

**Schacter, D.L. (2001). *The Seven Sins of Memory: How the Mind Forgets and Remembers*. Houghton Mifflin.**
A popular-science book by a leading memory researcher, organized around seven ways memory fails. Well-written and accurate. The framing as "sins" is slightly misleading -- Schacter argues these are features, not bugs -- but the content is excellent. Assumes no background.

**Loftus, E.F. (2005). "Planting misinformation in the human mind: A 30-year investigation of the malleability of memory." *Learning & Memory*, 12(4), 361-366.**
A retrospective by Loftus on her career studying false memories and the misinformation effect. Short, accessible, and gives you the arc of a research program. A good companion to the more technical literature.

**Cowan, N. (2001). "The magical number 4 in short-term memory: A reconsideration of mental storage capacity." *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 24(1), 87-114.**
Cowan's influential argument that working memory capacity is about 4 items, not Miller's 7 plus or minus 2. The target article is clear; the commentaries provide a snapshot of the debate. Essential for understanding working memory capacity limits.

**Walker, M.P. (2017). *Why We Sleep: Unlocking the Power of Sleep and Dreams*. Scribner.**
Widely read and engaging, but use with caution. Walker's enthusiasm for sleep science leads to overstatements and, in some cases, misrepresentation of the literature. Alexey Guzey's detailed critique (available online) documents specific errors. Read both together for a lesson in how popular science can go wrong even when the core science is real.

**Rasch, B., & Born, J. (2013). "About sleep's role in memory." *Physiological Reviews*, 93(2), 681-766.**
A thorough, authoritative review of sleep and memory consolidation. Long and technical, but the introduction and section summaries are accessible. The best academic source on what we actually know about sleep and memory, as opposed to what popular accounts claim.

**Roediger, H.L., & McDermott, K.B. (1995). "Creating false memories: Remembering words not presented in lists." *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Learning, Memory, and Cognition*, 21(4), 803-814.**
The DRM false memory paradigm paper. Simple, clever, and replicable. Demonstrates how easily false memories arise in a controlled setting. Readable with basic statistics knowledge.

**Squire, L.R. (2004). "Memory systems of the brain: A brief history and current perspective." *Neurobiology of Learning and Memory*, 82(3), 171-177.**
A concise review of the taxonomy of memory systems by one of the architects of the field. Useful for getting the big picture of how different memory systems relate to each other and to brain structures.

## Part IV: Language (Chapters 13-15)

**Harley, T.A. (2014). *The Psychology of Language: From Data to Theory* (4th edition). Psychology Press.**
The best single-volume textbook on psycholinguistics. Comprehensive, well-organized, and fair to competing theories. Academic but accessible to motivated undergraduates. Use it as a reference for any language topic covered in this book.

**Saffran, J.R., Aslin, R.N., & Newport, E.L. (1996). "Statistical learning by 8-month-old infants." *Science*, 274(5294), 1926-1928.**
The landmark statistical learning paper. Two pages long. Demonstrates that pre-verbal infants extract transitional probabilities from continuous speech after minimal exposure. Beautiful in its simplicity. Replicates well.

**Everett, C. (2013). *Linguistic Relativity: Evidence Across Languages and Cognitive Domains*. De Gruyter Mouton.**
A balanced, thorough survey of the linguistic relativity evidence across multiple cognitive domains (color, space, time, number). Academic but clearly written. The most even-handed treatment of a topic that inspires strong opinions.

**Chomsky, N. (1959). "Review of B.F. Skinner's *Verbal Behavior*." *Language*, 35(1), 26-58.**
The review that helped launch the cognitive revolution. Dense and combative, but the core arguments are clear: language is creative, rule-governed, and cannot be explained by stimulus-response associations. Historically essential, though some of Chomsky's specific claims have not held up.

**Tomasello, M. (2003). *Constructing a Language: A Usage-Based Theory of Language Acquisition*. Harvard University Press.**
The strongest statement of the usage-based (anti-nativist) approach to language acquisition. Argues that children learn language through general cognitive mechanisms, not a language-specific module. Well-argued and empirically grounded. Read alongside a nativist account (Pinker or Crain) for balance.

**Fedorenko, E., & Thompson-Schill, S.L. (2014). "Reworking the language network." *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 18(3), 120-126.**
A clear, modern review of the neuroscience of language, moving beyond the simplified Broca-Wernicke model. Accessible to anyone with basic neuroscience background. Useful corrective to textbook oversimplifications.

**Pinker, S. (1994). *The Language Instinct: How the Mind Creates Language*. William Morrow.**
Brilliantly written and persuasive, but it is advocacy for a specific position (strong nativism) presented as consensus. The linguistics and cognitive science have moved on in some areas. Read it for the writing and the arguments, but know that the field is more divided than Pinker suggests.

## Part V: Reasoning, Decision-Making, and Executive Control (Chapters 16-19)

**Kahneman, D. (2011). *Thinking, Fast and Slow*. Farrar, Straus and Giroux.**
The most influential popular book on judgment and decision-making. Kahneman is a careful writer and a Nobel laureate. The book is excellent on the phenomena but overly confident about the dual-process framework and does not adequately address the replication problems in social priming research that emerged shortly after publication. Read it, but read the critiques too.

**Gigerenzer, G. (2008). *Rationality for Mortals: How People Cope with Uncertainty*. Oxford University Press.**
The counterpoint to Kahneman. Gigerenzer argues that heuristics are not cognitive failures but ecologically rational strategies. More academic than Kahneman's book, but essential for understanding the other side of the rationality debate. Assumes some familiarity with the heuristics-and-biases literature.

**Murphy, G.L. (2002). *The Big Book of Concepts*. MIT Press.**
The most comprehensive treatment of concepts and categorization. Covers prototype theory, exemplar theory, theory-based concepts, and their interactions. Academic and thorough. Not light reading, but the definitive reference on how we form and use categories.

**Rosch, E. (1975). "Cognitive representations of semantic categories." *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General*, 104(3), 192-233.**
Rosch's foundational paper on prototypes and typicality. Established that categories have graded structure, not sharp boundaries. Clear experimental logic. A landmark worth reading in the original.

**Miyake, A., Friedman, N.P., Emerson, M.J., Witzki, A.H., Howerter, A., & Wager, T.D. (2000). "The unity and diversity of executive functions and their contributions to complex 'frontal lobe' tasks: A latent variable analysis." *Cognitive Psychology*, 41(1), 49-100.**
The paper that established the three-factor model of executive function (inhibition, updating, shifting). Technical (latent variable analysis), but the introduction and discussion are accessible. One of the most cited papers in cognitive psychology for good reason.

**Ericsson, K.A., Krampe, R.T., & Tesch-Romer, C. (1993). "The role of deliberate practice in the acquisition of expert performance." *Psychological Review*, 100(3), 363-406.**
The original deliberate practice paper, which is much more nuanced than the "10,000 hours" narrative that Malcolm Gladwell popularized. Ericsson is careful about boundary conditions. Reading the original is a useful corrective to the pop-science version.

**Evans, J.St.B.T., & Stanovich, K.E. (2013). "Dual-process theories of higher cognition: Advancing the debate." *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 8(3), 223-241.**
A clear summary of where dual-process theory stands, by two of its leading proponents. Acknowledges criticisms and attempts to sharpen the framework. The best place to start if you want to understand the current state of the System 1/System 2 debate.

**Kosslyn, S.M. (1994). *Image and Brain: The Resolution of the Imagery Debate*. MIT Press.**
Kosslyn's case for depictive mental imagery, drawing on both behavioral and neuroimaging evidence. Well-argued but partisan. The "imagery debate" is not as resolved as the subtitle suggests, but this is the strongest statement of one side.

## Part VI: Bigger Questions (Chapters 20-26)

**Chalmers, D.J. (1996). *The Conscious Mind: In Search of a Fundamental Theory*. Oxford University Press.**
The book that crystallized the "hard problem" of consciousness. Chalmers is a clear, rigorous philosophical writer. The arguments are careful even when the conclusions are controversial (property dualism). Dense in places, but the first few chapters are accessible to anyone willing to think slowly.

**Dehaene, S. (2014). *Consciousness and the Brain: Deciphering How the Brain Codes Our Thoughts*. Viking.**
The empirical counterpart to Chalmers. Dehaene presents the global neuronal workspace theory and the experimental evidence for it. Accessible and well-illustrated. Somewhat partisan -- other theories get less airtime -- but the science is presented clearly.

**Barrett, L.F. (2017). *How Emotions Are Made: The Secret Life of the Brain*. Houghton Mifflin Harcourt.**
Barrett's case for constructed emotion theory. Well-written and provocative. The argument against basic emotions is persuasive, but the book sometimes overstates the evidence for the constructionist alternative. Read alongside Ekman's work for balance.

**Wellman, H.M. (2014). *Making Minds: How Theory of Mind Develops*. Oxford University Press.**
A thorough, developmentally oriented treatment of theory of mind. Covers the false-belief task, its precursors, and cross-cultural evidence. Academic but clearly written. The best single source on ToM development.

**Salthouse, T.A. (2010). *Major Issues in Cognitive Aging*. Oxford University Press.**
A rigorous, sometimes contrarian assessment of what we know about cognitive aging. Salthouse is skeptical of many popular claims (cognitive reserve, brain training) and backs his skepticism with data. Academic and statistical, but the arguments are clearly laid out.

**Shapiro, L. (2019). *Embodied Cognition* (2nd edition). Routledge.**
A balanced philosophical and empirical assessment of embodied cognition. Shapiro distinguishes conceptualization, replacement, and constitution versions of the thesis and evaluates each. Clear-headed where many treatments of embodied cognition are evangelical.

**Clark, A., & Chalmers, D.J. (1998). "The extended mind." *Analysis*, 58(1), 7-19.**
The original extended mind paper. Twelve pages, no jargon, a single thought experiment (Otto and his notebook). One of the most discussed papers in philosophy of mind in the last three decades. Read it; it takes 30 minutes.

**Mahowald, K., Ivanova, A.A., Blank, I.A., Kanwisher, N., Tenenbaum, J.B., & Fedorenko, E. (2024). "Dissociating language and thought in large language models." *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 28(6), 517-540.**
A landmark review distinguishing "formal linguistic competence" (which LLMs excel at) from "functional linguistic competence" (reasoning, world knowledge, social cognition, which they handle unevenly). Essential reading for understanding the AI-cognitive science interface. Technical in places but the framework is clear.

**Gopnik, A. (2009). *The Philosophical Baby: What Children's Minds Tell Us About Truth, Love, and the Meaning of Life*. Farrar, Straus and Giroux.**
Gopnik makes developmental cognitive science exciting for a general audience. Covers Bayesian learning, theory of mind, causal reasoning, and imagination. Some simplifications, but the core science is accurate and the writing is excellent.

**Spelke, E.S. (2022). "What babies know and what adults can learn from them." In *The Routledge Handbook of Philosophy of Childhood and Children*. Routledge.**
A recent summary of core knowledge theory from one of its architects. Useful for understanding the nativist position in developmental cognitive science, presented with Spelke's characteristic precision.

---

*Reading lists date quickly. If a paper cited here is more than ten years old, check Google Scholar for more recent reviews that cite it -- they will tell you how the field has moved.*
