# Chapter 2: How We Know What We Know

## Opening: Two Studies, Two Journals, Two Failures

In 2006, a study published in *Science* reported a striking finding: people who had been asked to think about an unethical act they had committed were more likely to choose an antiseptic wipe over a pencil as a free gift. Thinking about moral transgressions, it seemed, produced a literal desire to cleanse oneself. The "Macbeth effect," the authors called it. It was clean, surprising, and intuitively appealing. It was also, as later replication attempts showed, almost certainly not real.

Around the same time, John Bargh and colleagues published a study — now one of the most discussed in all of psychology — showing that people who had been primed with words related to elderly stereotypes (wrinkle, Florida, bingo) subsequently walked more slowly down a hallway. The unconscious mind, the study suggested, was so powerful that merely reading old-age-related words could change your physical behavior. This finding became a cornerstone of the "social priming" literature and featured prominently in Daniel Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow*.

It also failed to replicate. Stéphane Doyen and colleagues attempted a direct replication in 2012 and found no effect, unless the experimenters themselves expected participants to walk slowly, in which case the effect appeared, suggesting experimenter expectancy rather than priming.

These two studies were not outliers. They were symptoms of a systemic problem in how cognitive science, and psychology more broadly, generated, tested, and communicated its claims. Understanding that problem, and the reforms it has spurred, is essential for anyone who wants to read the field intelligently.

This chapter is about methods: how cognitive scientists produce evidence, what that evidence can and cannot tell you, and why a healthy dose of skepticism is not cynicism but good scientific practice.

## The Logic of Experiments

At its core, an experiment is a controlled comparison. You change one thing (the independent variable), hold everything else constant, and measure the effect on something you care about (the dependent variable). If the measured outcome differs between conditions, and you have controlled for alternative explanations, you can infer that the independent variable caused the difference.

### Independent and Dependent Variables

The independent variable (IV) is what the experimenter manipulates. In a study of whether background music affects reading comprehension, the IV might be the presence or absence of music. The dependent variable (DV) is what the experimenter measures; in this case, scores on a comprehension test.

This sounds simple, and conceptually it is. The difficulty lies in the details.

### Controls and Confounds

A confound is anything other than the IV that differs between conditions and could explain the results. If your music condition always uses classical music and your no-music condition always takes place in a different room, any difference in comprehension could be due to the music, the room, or both. Good experimental design eliminates confounds through careful controls.

Random assignment is the most powerful tool for this purpose. If participants are randomly assigned to conditions, any pre-existing differences between them (in intelligence, motivation, mood, or a thousand other variables) are distributed roughly equally across conditions. With enough participants, random assignment makes the conditions equivalent on everything except the IV.

### Why Sample Size Matters

Small samples are noisy. In a study with twenty participants per condition, random variation can easily produce a spurious difference or hide a real one. Many of the findings that later failed to replicate were originally reported in small-sample studies where the results looked clean partly because noise happened to break in a favorable direction.

This is not fraud. It is a statistical inevitability. If you run many small studies, some will produce dramatic-looking results by chance alone. And if the field's publication system preferentially publishes dramatic-looking results (which it does; more on this shortly), the published literature will be enriched for flukes.

## The Cognitive Scientist's Toolkit

Cognitive experiments have developed a distinctive set of paradigms for getting at mental processes that are not directly observable.

### Reaction Time

If you cannot open the head and watch the mind work, you can measure how long things take. Reaction time (RT) has been the single most productive measure in cognitive psychology. The logic is simple: if a mental process takes time, then manipulations that add steps or make steps harder should increase RT.

For example, the mental rotation experiments of Shepard and Metzler (1971) showed that the time to judge whether two shapes are the same increases linearly with the angular difference between them. This suggested that people are actually rotating a mental image, and the farther they have to rotate it, the longer it takes. The reaction time data gave a window into a process that was otherwise invisible.

### Accuracy and Error Patterns

Sometimes what people get wrong is more informative than what they get right. If people systematically confuse "b" and "d" but not "b" and "x," that tells you something about how letter representations are structured. Error patterns reveal the fault lines in cognitive processes.

### Priming

In a priming experiment, you present one stimulus (the prime) and measure its effect on the processing of a subsequent stimulus (the target). If people are faster to recognize the word "nurse" after seeing "doctor" than after seeing "table," that suggests that "doctor" and "nurse" are linked in the mind's representational structure.

Priming has been one of the most widely used and most useful paradigms in cognitive science. It has also been the source of some of the field's most spectacular replication failures, mostly in the domain of "social priming," where the primes were supposed to influence complex social behavior rather than simple word recognition. The word-recognition version of priming is **Robust**. The social-behavior version ranges from **Contested** to **Fragile** depending on the specific claim.

### Dual-Task Paradigms

If two tasks can be performed simultaneously without interference, they probably rely on different cognitive resources. If they interfere with each other, they probably share resources. This logic has been used to map out the structure of attention and working memory.

## Brain Imaging: Power and Peril

### fMRI

Functional magnetic resonance imaging measures changes in blood oxygenation that are correlated with neural activity. When a brain region is more active, it consumes more oxygen, and fMRI detects the resulting change in the ratio of oxygenated to deoxygenated hemoglobin.

fMRI has excellent spatial resolution; it can identify active regions down to a few millimeters. But it has poor temporal resolution. The hemodynamic response takes several seconds to peak, while mental processes unfold in milliseconds. An fMRI scan is like a photograph with a several-second exposure: it captures where activity happened, but it blurs out the temporal sequence.

A more fundamental limitation: fMRI shows correlation, not causation. If brain region X is active during task Y, that does not mean X is necessary for Y. It might be involved in a process that accompanies Y, or it might be active for reasons unrelated to what the experimenter thinks the task measures.

### EEG

Electroencephalography measures electrical activity at the scalp. Its temporal resolution is excellent, at the millisecond level, but its spatial resolution is poor because electrical signals are smeared as they pass through the skull. EEG is useful for tracking the time course of cognitive processes. Event-related potentials (ERPs), specific patterns in the EEG signal that are time-locked to stimuli, have been used to study everything from attention to language processing.

### Lesion Studies

Historically, much of what we know about brain function comes from patients with brain damage. Broca's patient Leborgne, who could understand language but not produce it, provided early evidence that language production depends on a specific brain region (now called Broca's area).

Lesion studies have a logical advantage over imaging: if damage to a region impairs a function, that region is necessary for that function (not just correlated with it). But the disadvantage is that lesions are messy. Brain damage rarely respects functional boundaries, patients are rare, and no two patients have exactly the same damage.

### The General Lesson

No single method is sufficient. Each method has blind spots, and the strongest conclusions come from convergence: when behavioral experiments, brain imaging, computational modeling, and clinical evidence all point in the same direction.

## The Replication Crisis

The problems with social priming were not isolated incidents. Beginning around 2011, a series of events brought the reliability of published findings in psychology and cognitive science under systematic scrutiny.

### How It Unfolded

In 2011, Daryl Bem published a paper in the *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, one of the field's top journals, claiming to demonstrate precognition: that people's responses could be influenced by events that had not yet occurred. The paper used standard methods and standard statistical procedures. If those methods and procedures could produce evidence for precognition, something was deeply wrong with the methods and procedures.

The same year, the fraud of Diederik Stapel was uncovered. Stapel, a prominent Dutch social psychologist, had fabricated data in dozens of published papers. While fraud is different from methodological weakness, the Stapel case raised uncomfortable questions about how fabricated data could pass peer review so easily.

Then, in 2015, came the paper that made the problem undeniable.

---

## Primary Text Spotlight: Open Science Collaboration (2015) — "Estimating the Reproducibility of Psychological Science"

### What They Did

A team of 270 researchers, coordinated by Brian Nosek at the Center for Open Science, attempted to replicate 100 studies published in three top psychology journals in 2008. They used the original materials, consulted with original authors, and pre-registered their analyses. This was not a group of skeptics trying to debunk findings. It was a systematic, good-faith effort to estimate the reliability of the published literature.

### What They Found

The results were sobering. Of the 100 studies, 97 had originally reported statistically significant results. In the replications, only 36 did. The average effect size in the replications was roughly half what the original studies had reported.

The results varied by subfield. Cognitive psychology fared better than social psychology: about 50% of cognitive studies replicated versus about 25% of social studies. But even 50% is a troubling number for a field that considers statistical significance the threshold for publishable findings.

### What It Means

The OSC replication project did not prove that any specific finding was false. A failure to replicate could mean the original finding was a fluke, but it could also mean the replication was flawed, or that some unmeasured contextual factor differed between the original and the replication. What the project did show was that the published literature, taken as a whole, was substantially less reliable than the field had assumed.

Several factors contributed to this unreliability:

**Publication bias.** Journals preferentially published positive results, studies that found a significant effect. Studies that found nothing were filed in desk drawers. This meant the published literature was a biased sample, enriched for effects that might be real and also enriched for statistical flukes.

**Underpowered studies.** Many studies had too few participants to reliably detect the effects they were looking for. In an underpowered study, the only way to reach statistical significance is if the observed effect is inflated by noise, a phenomenon called the "winner's curse."

**Flexible analysis.** Researchers had many "degrees of freedom" in how they analyzed their data: which participants to exclude, which variables to control for, which comparisons to focus on. With enough flexibility, you can find a significant result in almost any dataset. This was not usually deliberate fraud. It was the natural result of researchers wanting their studies to "work" and having the analytical flexibility to make it happen.

**Epistemic status: Robust.** That the published literature in psychology contains a substantial proportion of false positives is no longer seriously disputed. The exact rate varies by subfield and depends on how you define "replication."

## Pre-Registration and Open Science

The replication crisis prompted a wave of methodological reform that is still ongoing.

### Pre-Registration

The single most important reform is pre-registration: researchers publicly commit to their hypotheses, methods, and analysis plan before collecting data. This eliminates flexible analysis: you cannot shop for a significant result if your analysis was locked in advance.

Pre-registration is not a cure-all. It works best for confirmatory research (testing specific hypotheses) and less well for exploratory research (discovering unexpected patterns). Both types of research are valuable, and a healthy science needs both. The key is being honest about which one you are doing.

### Open Data and Open Materials

Sharing your data and experimental materials allows other researchers to verify your analyses and attempt replications. This is now standard practice in many journals and increasingly expected by funding agencies.

### Registered Reports

Some journals now offer a format called Registered Reports, in which the study design is peer-reviewed before data collection. If the design is approved, the journal commits to publishing the results regardless of what they show. This eliminates publication bias at the source.

## The Generalizability Crisis

In 2020, Tal Yarkoni published a paper arguing that the replication crisis was only part of a larger problem. Even when studies replicate perfectly, they may not generalize in the ways researchers claim.

The issue is the gap between what researchers operationalize and what they claim to be studying. A researcher might present participants with a specific set of words in a specific font on a specific screen and measure reading times. The study is nominally about "language processing," but the actual stimuli and procedures are far narrower than that. Whether the results generalize to other words, other fonts, other tasks, or other languages is an empirical question, one that is rarely tested.

Yarkoni's point was not that all research is wrong. It was that the field routinely draws conclusions that are broader than the evidence supports. This is a problem of inference, not of replication, and it cannot be fixed by pre-registration alone. It requires researchers to be more careful about the scope of their claims.

**Epistemic status: Contested.** Yarkoni's critique is influential but not universally accepted. Some researchers argue that reasonable generalization from specific studies is necessary and unavoidable; others agree with Yarkoni that the field over-generalizes systematically.

## What This Gets Right / What's Still Open

The methodological reforms of the past decade have made cognitive science substantially more reliable. Pre-registration, open data, larger samples, and multi-site replications are now common. The field is in better shape than it was in 2010.

What remains open:

- **How much of the existing literature is reliable?** We have spot-checked with replication projects, but we have not (and cannot) replicate everything. The base rate of false positives in the pre-2015 literature is uncertain.
- **How to balance rigor with discovery.** Pre-registration is excellent for testing specific hypotheses but could, if applied too rigidly, discourage the exploratory research that generates hypotheses in the first place.
- **How to handle cross-cultural generalizability.** Most methodological reforms focus on replicability, getting the same result in the same population. The question of whether findings generalize across cultures and contexts is harder and less developed.
- **What to do with the old literature.** Thousands of published findings have never been replicated. Should we treat them as provisionally true, provisionally suspect, or something in between?

## Real-World Application: How to Read a Popular Science Article About the Brain

When you encounter a news article or blog post making claims about the mind or brain, here is a practical checklist:

**1. What is the sample size?** Studies with fewer than 50 participants per condition should be treated with caution. Studies with fewer than 20 per condition should be treated with serious skepticism.

**2. Has it been replicated?** A single study, no matter how well-designed, is a preliminary finding. Look for replications, especially from independent labs.

**3. What was actually measured?** "Scientists discover the brain region for love" probably means "an fMRI study found that a particular region was more active when participants viewed photos of romantic partners compared to friends." These are very different claims.

**4. Is the article confusing correlation with causation?** Brain imaging studies are inherently correlational. If the article says a brain region "causes" something based on fMRI alone, that is an overstatement.

**5. Was the study pre-registered?** If yes, you can have somewhat more confidence in the results. If no, consider that the reported analyses might have been selected from many possibilities.

**6. Who are the participants?** College students in an American psychology department may not be representative of humanity at large.

**7. Does the effect size matter?** Even if a result is statistically significant, the effect might be tiny, too small to have practical relevance. A study might show that background music significantly impairs reading comprehension, but if the effect amounts to half a percentage point on a test, who cares?

**8. Is the explanation adding anything?** As we discussed in Chapter 1, a brain-region label is not an explanation. If the article's key insight is just "brain area X is involved in behavior Y," ask what that actually tells you.

This checklist will not make you infallible, but it will make you harder to mislead. In a media environment saturated with "studies show" claims, that is worth a lot.
