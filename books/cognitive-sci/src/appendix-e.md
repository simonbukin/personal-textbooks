# Appendix E: A Timeline of Cognitive Science

This timeline traces the major events, discoveries, and publications that shaped cognitive science from its pre-history to the present. No timeline is neutral -- this one emphasizes the developments discussed in this book and reflects the interdisciplinary scope of the field. Dates are for publication or discovery unless otherwise noted.

---

**1879 -- Wilhelm Wundt establishes the first psychology laboratory in Leipzig.**
Often cited as the founding of experimental psychology. Wundt's methods (controlled introspection) were limited, but the ambition -- studying the mind with scientific methods -- set the stage for everything that followed.

**1890 -- William James publishes *The Principles of Psychology*.**
A sprawling, brilliant work that anticipates many modern ideas: the stream of consciousness, the distinction between habit and deliberate thought, the embodied nature of emotion. Still worth reading.

**1913 -- John Watson publishes "Psychology as the Behaviorist Views It."**
Watson's manifesto declares that psychology should study only observable behavior, not mental states. Launches the behaviorist era, which will dominate American psychology for nearly 50 years.

**1932 -- Frederic Bartlett publishes *Remembering*.**
Bartlett demonstrates that memory is reconstructive, not reproductive. His "War of the Ghosts" studies show that people systematically distort stories to fit their cultural schemas. Largely ignored during the behaviorist era; vindicated decades later.

**1948 -- Edward Tolman publishes "Cognitive maps in rats and man."**
Demonstrates that rats form internal spatial representations ("cognitive maps") of mazes, not just stimulus-response associations. A direct challenge to behaviorism from within experimental psychology.

**1948 -- Claude Shannon publishes "A Mathematical Theory of Communication."**
Establishes information theory, providing a mathematical framework for quantifying information. Becomes a foundational tool for cognitive science, giving researchers a vocabulary for describing mental processes in terms of information processing.

**1950 -- Alan Turing publishes "Computing Machinery and Intelligence."**
Proposes the Turing Test as a criterion for machine intelligence and introduces the question of whether machines can think. Sets the philosophical groundwork for artificial intelligence and its relationship to cognitive science.

**1953 -- Patient H.M. undergoes bilateral hippocampal surgery.**
Henry Molaison's surgery (and the devastating amnesia that followed) reveals that the hippocampus is essential for forming new declarative memories. Studied by Brenda Milner and others for decades, H.M.'s case is the most important single case study in the history of memory research.

**1956 -- George Miller publishes "The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two."**
Establishes that short-term memory has a limited capacity, but that capacity can be extended by chunking. One of the most cited papers in psychology. (The number has since been revised downward to about four; see Cowan, 2001.)

**1956 -- The Dartmouth Conference on Artificial Intelligence.**
John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Allen Newell, Herbert Simon, and others gather to launch AI as a field. The proposal expresses optimism that "every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can in principle be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it." That optimism would prove premature.

**1956 -- Newell and Simon demonstrate the Logic Theorist.**
The first AI program to prove mathematical theorems, demonstrating that symbolic computation can produce intelligent behavior. A landmark in the argument that cognition is computation.

**1957 -- Noam Chomsky publishes *Syntactic Structures*.**
Introduces transformational grammar and argues that language has a deep structure governed by rules that cannot be reduced to behavioral associations. Revolutionizes linguistics and, by extension, the study of mind.

**1958 -- Donald Broadbent publishes *Perception and Communication*.**
Proposes the filter model of selective attention, one of the first information-processing models of cognition. Marks the beginning of modern attention research.

**1959 -- Chomsky publishes his review of Skinner's *Verbal Behavior*.**
A devastating critique arguing that behaviorist principles cannot account for language acquisition, creativity, or grammatical structure. Widely regarded as a turning point in the decline of behaviorism and the rise of cognitive science.

**1960 -- George Sperling demonstrates iconic memory.**
Using the partial report technique, Sperling shows that visual memory briefly holds far more information than can be reported. Establishes a rigorous method for studying very short-term sensory storage.

**1960 -- Jerome Bruner, George Miller, and colleagues found the Center for Cognitive Studies at Harvard.**
One of the first institutional homes for cognitive science as a distinct enterprise. The name itself was a declaration of intent.

**1967 -- Ulric Neisser publishes *Cognitive Psychology*.**
The book that gives the field its name. Neisser synthesizes research on perception, attention, memory, and language into a coherent information-processing framework. A founding document.

**1968 -- Atkinson and Shiffrin propose the modal model of memory.**
The three-store model (sensory, short-term, long-term) provides the dominant framework for memory research for the next two decades. Influential despite its oversimplifications.

**1971 -- Shepard and Metzler demonstrate mental rotation.**
Show that the time to judge whether two objects are the same increases linearly with the angle between them, suggesting that mental imagery involves an analog spatial process. A landmark in the study of mental representation.

**1972 -- Endel Tulving distinguishes episodic and semantic memory.**
Proposes that memory for personal events (episodic) and memory for general knowledge (semantic) are different systems. The distinction becomes one of the organizing principles of memory research.

**1973 -- Tversky and Kahneman publish "Availability: A Heuristic for Judging Frequency and Probability."**
Part of their landmark heuristics-and-biases research program, which demonstrates systematic departures from rational judgment. Leads to Kahneman's Nobel Prize in 2002.

**1975 -- Eleanor Rosch publishes her work on prototypes and basic-level categories.**
Demonstrates that categories have graded internal structure organized around prototypes, overturning the classical view of categories as defined by necessary and sufficient features.

**1971-1978 -- John O'Keefe discovers place cells in the hippocampus.**
O'Keefe identifies neurons in the rat hippocampus that fire when the animal is in a specific location, providing the first neural evidence for cognitive maps. Nobel Prize in 2014.

**1979 -- James Gibson publishes *The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception*.**
Argues that perception is direct -- organisms pick up information (affordances) from the environment without constructing internal representations. Controversial but deeply influential.

**1979 -- Kahneman and Tversky publish prospect theory.**
A descriptive theory of decision-making under risk, featuring loss aversion and probability weighting. Becomes the most influential alternative to expected utility theory in economics and psychology.

**1974 -- Baddeley and Hitch propose the working memory model.**
Replaces the unitary "short-term store" with a multi-component system: the phonological loop, the visuospatial sketchpad, and the central executive. Later updated with the episodic buffer (2000). Dominates working memory research for decades.

**1983 -- Jerry Fodor publishes *The Modularity of Mind*.**
Argues that input systems (perception, language) are informationally encapsulated modules -- fast, automatic, and domain-specific. Sparks an ongoing debate about how modular the mind really is.

**1986 -- Rumelhart, McClelland, and the PDP Research Group publish *Parallel Distributed Processing*.**
Launches the connectionist revolution: the idea that cognition can be modeled by networks of simple units learning through gradient descent. Provides an alternative to symbolic AI and inspires modern deep learning.

**1974-1979 -- Elizabeth Loftus establishes the misinformation effect.**
Loftus & Palmer (1974) show that a single word in a question can alter memory for a witnessed event. Loftus's *Eyewitness Testimony* (1979) synthesizes the research program, transforming understanding of memory reliability and establishing memory distortion as a central research topic.

**1988 -- Bernard Baars proposes Global Workspace Theory.**
Argues that consciousness arises when information is broadcast across a "global workspace" of cognitive processes. Dehaene, Kerszberg, and Changeux (1998) develop the neural version, the Global Neuronal Workspace model, which becomes one of the leading neuroscientific theories of consciousness.

**1996 -- Jenny Saffran, Richard Aslin, and Elissa Newport demonstrate statistical learning in infants.**
Show that 8-month-olds extract transitional probabilities from a stream of syllables after just two minutes of exposure. Establishes statistical learning as a powerful mechanism for language acquisition and beyond.

**1998 -- Andy Clark and David Chalmers publish "The Extended Mind."**
Argue that cognitive processes can extend beyond the brain to include external tools and environmental structures. A twelve-page paper that generates decades of debate in philosophy of mind.

**1998 -- Baumeister and colleagues publish the ego depletion paradigm.**
Propose that self-control is a limited resource depleted by use. The finding will become enormously influential -- and then largely fail to replicate.

**2000 -- Akira Miyake and colleagues publish the unity/diversity model of executive function.**
Identify three separable but related executive functions: inhibition, updating, and shifting. The paper becomes a cornerstone of research on cognitive control.

**2004 -- Giulio Tononi proposes integrated information theory (IIT).**
Argues that consciousness is identical to integrated information (phi), offering a mathematical framework for measuring consciousness. Bold and controversial; generates both admiration and sharp critique.

**2005 -- The Mosers discover grid cells in the entorhinal cortex.**
Edvard and May-Britt Moser identify neurons that fire in a regular hexagonal pattern as an animal moves through space, providing a metric coordinate system for navigation. Nobel Prize in 2014, shared with O'Keefe.

**2010 -- Henrich, Heine, and Norenzayan publish "The weirdest people in the world?"**
Document the extent to which psychology's findings are based on Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, and Democratic (WEIRD) populations and argue that this sampling bias threatens the generalizability of the entire field.

**2011 -- Daryl Bem publishes "Feeling the Future" in a top psychology journal.**
Claims to demonstrate precognition using standard psychological methods. The paper's publication -- and the failure of the field's methods to prevent it -- catalyzes the replication crisis and the open science movement.

**2011-2015 -- The replication crisis accelerates.**
A series of high-profile replication failures (social priming, ego depletion, power posing) combined with statistical critiques and the Open Science Collaboration's 2015 report (estimating that only ~36% of psychology findings replicate) reshape the field's relationship to its own methods.

**2014 -- O'Keefe and the Mosers receive the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine.**
Awarded for the discovery of place cells and grid cells, the neural basis of the brain's spatial positioning system. A rare Nobel Prize for cognitive neuroscience.

**2015 -- Open Science Collaboration publishes "Estimating the reproducibility of psychological science."**
Attempted to replicate 100 published psychology studies. Roughly 36% produced statistically significant results in the same direction as the original. The single most influential publication of the replication crisis.

**2017 -- Lisa Feldman Barrett publishes *How Emotions Are Made*.**
Presents the constructed emotion theory to a popular audience, challenging Ekman's basic emotion framework. Sparks renewed debate about whether emotions are natural kinds or constructed categories.

**2017 -- The pre-registration and Registered Reports movement gains momentum.**
Major journals adopt Registered Reports format. The open science movement shifts from critique to reform, with new tools (OSF, pre-registration, open data) becoming standard practice in many labs.

**2020 -- GPT-3 is released by OpenAI.**
A large language model with 175 billion parameters that generates surprisingly fluent text. Reignites debates about whether language models understand language or merely mimic understanding. Begins the current wave of AI-cognitive science interaction.

**2022 -- ChatGPT is released, bringing LLMs to public attention.**
The public release of a conversational AI system forces cognitive science to reckon with machines that pass informal Turing Tests in many contexts. Raises urgent questions about the relationship between language competence and genuine understanding.

**2023 -- GPT-4 is released; "machine psychology" emerges as a research area.**
Researchers begin systematically testing LLMs on cognitive and psychological tasks (theory of mind tests, reasoning problems, bias probes), treating them as subjects in cognitive experiments. The field has not yet decided what to make of the results.

**2024 -- Mahowald et al. publish "Dissociating language and thought in large language models."**
A major review distinguishing formal linguistic competence (which LLMs handle well) from functional linguistic competence (which they handle unevenly). Provides a framework for understanding what AI success and failure tell us about human cognition.

---

*This timeline inevitably reflects choices about what to include and what to leave out. Every entry omitted -- Marr's levels of analysis (1982), the discovery of mirror neurons (1992), the rise of Bayesian modeling (2000s), among many others -- represents another thread in the rich, tangled history of the science of the mind. The references in Appendix C point to fuller histories.*
