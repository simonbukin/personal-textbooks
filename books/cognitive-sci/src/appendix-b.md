# Appendix B: Epistemic Status Reference

Throughout this book, major claims and theories are tagged with epistemic status markers. This appendix collects those assessments in one place, organized by chapter, so you can quickly check how confident you should be in any given finding.

A reminder of the categories:

- **Robust.** Replicated across multiple labs, typically with large or medium effect sizes, confirmed by converging methods. As close to "established fact" as cognitive science gets. You can build on these.
- **Contested.** Supported by real evidence, but actively debated. Reasonable experts disagree about interpretation, scope, or mechanism. You should know the arguments on both sides.
- **Fragile.** Based on suggestive initial studies that have not been independently replicated, have partial or mixed replication results, or rest on small samples and questionable methods. Interesting, but do not treat as settled.
- **Discredited.** Once widely accepted, now largely rejected based on failed large-scale replications, identified methodological errors, or retraction of key papers. Included because these ideas persist in popular culture and older textbooks.

These are my best-faith assessments as of the time of writing. Science moves. Some "fragile" findings may firm up; some "robust" ones may develop cracks. The point is not to hand you certainty but to make the uncertainty explicit.

## Part I: Orientation (Chapters 1-3)

### Chapter 1: The Mind as Object of Study
- **Robust:** The basic claim that mental processes are lawful and can be studied scientifically. The interdisciplinary structure of cognitive science (six contributing disciplines).
- **Contested:** The degree to which computational metaphors (mind as computer) are literally true versus useful fictions.

### Chapter 2: How We Know What We Know
- **Robust:** Core experimental methods (reaction time, accuracy, signal detection theory) as valid windows into cognitive processes. The replication crisis itself is well-documented.
- **Contested:** Exactly how much of the published literature is unreliable. Estimates range from 30% to 60% depending on subfield and criteria.
- **Fragile:** Specific estimates of false-positive rates in any given subfield, which depend on modeling assumptions.

### Chapter 3: A Brief and Honest History of Cognitive Science
- **Robust:** The historical narrative of behaviorism's dominance and the cognitive revolution is well-documented. Chomsky's critique of Skinner was pivotal.
- **Contested:** Whether the "cognitive revolution" was a sharp break or a gradual transition. Some historians argue the discontinuity is overstated.

## Part II: Perception, Action, and Attention (Chapters 4-8)

### Chapter 4: Perception -- Construction, Not Recording
- **Robust:** Perception is constructive, not a passive recording. Visual illusions demonstrate this reliably. The brain fills in the blind spot. Feature detection in early visual cortex (Hubel and Wiesel) is among the most replicated findings in neuroscience.
- **Contested:** Predictive processing as a unifying theory of perception. The basic idea that the brain generates predictions is well-supported; the strong claim that prediction error minimization is the single organizing principle of brain function remains debated.
- **Fragile:** Specific claims about the degree to which perception is "cognitively penetrable" (directly influenced by beliefs and desires). Some demonstrations have proven difficult to replicate or have alternative explanations.

### Chapter 5: Attention -- The Spotlight, the Filter, and the Gorilla
- **Robust:** Inattentional blindness (the gorilla study replicates consistently). Change blindness. The attentional blink. The Stroop effect. Basic selective attention phenomena (dichotic listening, visual search).
- **Contested:** Whether attention is best understood as a spotlight, a filter, a resource, or something else entirely. Feature integration theory's specific binding mechanism.
- **Fragile:** Precise estimates of attentional "capacity" in terms of number of objects that can be tracked simultaneously (varies considerably across paradigms).

### Chapter 6: Motor Cognition and Action
- **Robust:** Fitts's Law (one of the most reliable quantitative laws in behavioral science). The existence of forward models in motor control. The speed-accuracy tradeoff.
- **Contested:** Mirror neurons in humans: they exist in some form, but their role in action understanding, empathy, and language is far less clear than the popular narrative suggests. The "broken mirror" theory of autism is largely unsupported.
- **Fragile:** Specific claims about motor simulation during action observation (that watching someone grasp activates the same motor plans as grasping).

### Chapter 7: Spatial Cognition and Navigation
- **Robust:** Place cells in the hippocampus. Grid cells in the entorhinal cortex. The Nobel Prize-winning work by O'Keefe and the Mosers is among the strongest in cognitive neuroscience. Tolman's cognitive maps (the concept, if not all original experiments).
- **Contested:** Whether human navigation relies on a metric cognitive map or a graph-like topological representation. The degree to which grid cells in humans function identically to those in rodents.
- **Fragile:** Specific claims about sex differences in navigation strategies. Some findings replicate; many are confounded by experience and cultural factors.

### Chapter 8: Perception, Action, and Attention Together
- **Robust:** The fundamental interdependence of perception and action. Saccadic eye movements are integral to visual perception. Active sensing shapes what we perceive.
- **Contested:** The extent to which Gibson's ecological approach can replace computational approaches, or whether both are needed.

## Part III: Memory (Chapters 9-12)

### Chapter 9: Working Memory -- The Thinking Space
- **Robust:** Working memory capacity limits (approximately 3-4 items). The phonological loop and articulatory suppression effects. Chunking. Strong correlation between working memory capacity and general cognitive ability.
- **Contested:** Whether Baddeley's multi-component model or embedded-process models (Cowan) better characterize the architecture. The nature of the "central executive."
- **Fragile:** Specific claims about working memory training transferring to general intelligence. Most transfer effects have shrunk or disappeared in well-controlled studies.
- **Discredited:** The idea that working memory can be permanently expanded through short training programs (the "brain training" industry's core claim).

### Chapter 10: Long-Term Memory -- Systems, Schemas, and the Art of Forgetting
- **Robust:** The distinction between explicit (declarative) and implicit (nondeclarative) memory, supported by lesion studies and neuroimaging. The levels-of-processing effect. The testing effect. Spacing effects on retention.
- **Contested:** Whether the episodic-semantic distinction reflects truly separate systems or a continuum. Whether forgetting is primarily interference-driven or also involves true decay.
- **Fragile:** Precise claims about the number of distinct long-term memory systems.

### Chapter 11: Memory as Construction
- **Robust:** The misinformation effect. The DRM (Deese-Roediger-McDermott) false memory paradigm. The basic constructive nature of memory retrieval. Eyewitness memory is unreliable under suggestive conditions.
- **Contested:** Reconsolidation in humans: the phenomenon exists, but boundary conditions and clinical applications are still being mapped.
- **Fragile:** Specific claims about "implanting" entirely fabricated childhood memories in controlled settings. The original "lost in the mall" studies used small samples and demand characteristics are hard to rule out.
- **Discredited:** "Recovered memory therapy" as a reliable method for accessing repressed childhood trauma. The memory wars of the 1990s resolved largely against the repressed-memory framework.

### Chapter 12: Sleep, Consolidation, and the Fate of a Memory
- **Robust:** Sleep-dependent memory consolidation. Performance on memory tasks improves after sleep compared to equivalent waking intervals. Slow-wave sleep is particularly important for declarative memory consolidation. Hippocampal replay during sleep.
- **Contested:** The precise functional roles of different sleep stages (REM vs. NREM) in different types of memory. Whether sleep "actively" reorganizes memories or merely protects them from interference.
- **Fragile:** Specific claims about targeted memory reactivation during sleep (playing sounds associated with specific memories to boost consolidation). Promising but early.

## Part IV: Language (Chapters 13-15)

### Chapter 13: Language Processing -- From Sound to Meaning
- **Robust:** Incremental, word-by-word sentence processing. Garden-path effects. Lexical access is fast and automatic. Frequency effects on word recognition. Broca's and Wernicke's areas are involved in language processing (though their roles are more complex than textbook accounts suggest).
- **Contested:** Whether syntax and semantics are processed by separable neural systems or by a single integrated system. The degree to which language processing is modular.
- **Fragile:** Specific claims about prediction during sentence comprehension (do readers predict specific words, or just semantic features?).

### Chapter 14: Language Development
- **Robust:** Statistical learning in infants (Saffran et al.). Infants track transitional probabilities between syllables. Babbling follows a universal sequence. Critical/sensitive period effects on language acquisition.
- **Contested:** The nature and extent of innate linguistic knowledge (Universal Grammar). Nativist versus usage-based accounts of language acquisition remain deeply divided.
- **Fragile:** Specific claims about the "amount of language input" needed for normal development (the original "30 million word gap" study has not replicated in its original form).
- **Discredited:** The strong claim that language acquisition is purely a product of reinforcement and imitation (Skinner's account).

### Chapter 15: Language and Thought
- **Robust:** Weak linguistic relativity effects on color discrimination at category boundaries. Language influences some aspects of spatial reasoning in cross-linguistic comparisons.
- **Contested:** Linguistic relativity in the moderate form: how deep and pervasive are language effects on thought? Opinions range from "shallow and strategic" to "pervasive and automatic."
- **Discredited:** Strong linguistic determinism (Whorf's original claim that language determines thought and makes certain thoughts impossible without the right words).

## Part V: Reasoning, Decision-Making, and Executive Control (Chapters 16-19)

### Chapter 16: Concepts, Categories, and Mental Imagery
- **Robust:** Typicality effects in categorization. Prototype theory's core predictions. Mental rotation time scales linearly with angle.
- **Contested:** Whether concepts are best characterized by prototypes, exemplars, or theories (probably all three, in different contexts). The imagery debate: are mental images depictive or propositional?
- **Fragile:** Aphantasia research is in early stages; claims about how aphantasics compensate are still being tested.

### Chapter 17: Reasoning -- Logic, Heuristics, and the Clever Shortcuts
- **Robust:** Humans are poor at abstract logical reasoning tasks (Wason selection task). Confirmation bias in hypothesis testing. Content and context effects on reasoning.
- **Contested:** Dual-process theory: the broad distinction between fast/automatic and slow/deliberate processing is useful, but the specific mechanisms, the number of systems, and whether "System 1" and "System 2" are coherent natural kinds are all debated.
- **Fragile:** Specific claims about the neural basis of different reasoning modes.

### Chapter 18: Judgment and Decision-Making
- **Robust:** Framing effects. Loss aversion (core phenomenon). The availability heuristic. Prospect theory's descriptive accuracy for risky choice.
- **Contested:** Ecological rationality versus the heuristics-and-biases program: are people irrational or well-adapted? This is partly an empirical debate and partly a framing choice. The magnitude of loss aversion in everyday decisions.
- **Fragile:** Some specific bias demonstrations that rely on particular experimental setups and may not generalize to naturalistic decisions.
- **Discredited:** Ego depletion in its strong form (willpower as a depletable glucose-based resource). The large-scale pre-registered replication found no meaningful effect. Some weaker version of self-control fatigue may exist, but the original theoretical framework is not supported.

### Chapter 19: Executive Function and Expertise
- **Robust:** The three-factor structure of executive function (inhibition, updating, shifting), supported by Miyake et al. and subsequent confirmatory work. Expertise involves domain-specific chunking and pattern recognition (Chase and Simon on chess).
- **Contested:** Whether executive function is best understood as a unitary construct with separable components or as distinct abilities. The "10,000 hours" claim: deliberate practice matters, but the original claim that it is sufficient for expertise is not well-supported.
- **Fragile:** Specific claims about executive function training transferring to untrained tasks.

## Part VI: Bigger Questions (Chapters 20-26)

### Chapter 20: Consciousness
- **Robust:** The hard problem is a genuine conceptual challenge (not merely a failure of current science). Neural correlates of consciousness exist and can be studied. Global workspace effects (unconscious stimuli are processed differently from conscious ones).
- **Contested:** Every major theory of consciousness (Global Workspace Theory, Integrated Information Theory, Higher-Order Theories) is contested. There is no consensus theory.
- **Fragile:** Specific empirical predictions that distinguish between consciousness theories. The "adversarial collaboration" approach is promising but has not yet been decisive.

### Chapter 21: Emotion and Cognition
- **Robust:** Emotion and cognition interact pervasively; they are not separate systems. Emotional states influence memory, attention, and decision-making. The amygdala plays a role in processing threat-relevant stimuli.
- **Contested:** Barrett's constructed emotion theory versus basic emotion theory (Ekman). Whether there are universal facial expressions of emotion is now actively debated after decades of apparent consensus.
- **Fragile:** Specific claims about discrete emotion circuits in the brain.

### Chapter 22: Social Cognition
- **Robust:** Theory of mind as a cognitive capacity. The false-belief task in children (passes around age 4 in most populations). Humans are exquisitely sensitive to social information (faces, gaze, biological motion).
- **Contested:** Implicit bias as measured by the IAT: the measure has modest test-retest reliability, and its ability to predict discriminatory behavior is weaker than originally claimed. Whether theory of mind is an innate module or a learned skill.
- **Fragile:** Many social priming effects (e.g., elderly priming causing slower walking, professor priming improving trivia performance) have failed to replicate.
- **Discredited:** Several flagship social priming studies (Bargh's elderly walking study in its original form, Dijksterhuis's professor priming effect).

### Chapter 23: Cognitive Development
- **Robust:** Piaget's broad stage sequence (though the ages are wrong and the transitions are gradual). Core knowledge in infancy (basic object representation, numerosity). Statistical learning is present from birth.
- **Contested:** The extent of innate knowledge. Nativist accounts (Spelke, Baillargeon) versus constructivist accounts disagree on how much is built in.
- **Fragile:** Some looking-time paradigm results in infant cognition, where small methodological changes can eliminate effects.

### Chapter 24: Cognition Across the Lifespan
- **Robust:** Fluid intelligence declines with age; crystallized intelligence is preserved. Processing speed declines. The distinction between normal aging and pathological decline.
- **Contested:** Cognitive reserve: the phenomenon (education and engagement correlate with delayed onset of dementia symptoms) is well-documented, but the mechanisms are debated. Whether specific interventions (physical exercise, cognitive training) can meaningfully slow decline.
- **Fragile:** Specific brain training programs' claims to prevent or reverse cognitive aging.
- **Discredited:** The idea that cognitive decline is an inevitable, uniform process. Individual differences are enormous.

### Chapter 25: The Embodied Mind
- **Robust:** Sensorimotor experience shapes conceptual representation (e.g., reading action verbs activates motor cortex). Gesture facilitates learning and problem-solving. Distributed cognition across persons and artifacts is well-documented in field studies.
- **Contested:** Strong embodied cognition claims (that cognition is constituted by bodily processes, not merely influenced by them). The extended mind thesis (philosophically coherent but empirically hard to test).
- **Fragile:** Many embodied cognition priming studies (heavy clipboard, warm coffee, physical cleanliness affecting moral judgment) have failed or partially failed to replicate.
- **Discredited:** Several specific embodied cognition demonstrations that were prominent in the 2000s and have since failed replication (the "warm coffee" effect on interpersonal warmth judgments, in its original form).

### Chapter 26: The Limits and Future of Cognitive Science
- **Robust:** Large language models can perform tasks previously thought to require understanding, but their relationship to human cognition is unclear. The WEIRD sampling problem is real and affects generalizability. Open science practices improve reliability.
- **Contested:** Whether AI systems understand language or merely mimic understanding (the "stochastic parrots" debate). Whether cognitive science needs a paradigm shift or incremental improvement.
- **Fragile:** Specific claims about what LLMs can and cannot do, which change rapidly as models improve.

---

*These assessments reflect the state of evidence at the time of writing. Cognitive science is a self-correcting enterprise, and some of these ratings will change. That is a feature, not a bug.*
