# Chapter 15: Language and Thought: Does Language Shape What We Can Think?

The Piraha people of the Brazilian Amazon have attracted extraordinary attention from linguists, cognitive scientists, and the popular press. According to the most provocative claims, their language lacks recursion (the ability to embed one clause inside another), has no fixed color terms, no number words beyond approximate quantities, and no creation myths or distant-future planning. If these claims are accurate (and several are disputed), they raise one of the most profound questions in cognitive science: does the language you speak shape the thoughts you can think?

This question predates the Piraha controversy by a century. It strikes at the relationship between two of the most central capacities of the human mind. Here is a simpler version of the puzzle. Right now, try to think a thought that you have no words for. Can you do it? You might feel that you can, that you have experienced ineffable feelings, had ideas you struggled to express, or noticed things for which your language provided no convenient label. But how would you know? If the thought requires language, and you lack the language, the thought might simply never arise, and you would have no way of noticing its absence.

This chapter examines what we know, and what we honestly do not know, about the relationship between language and thought.

---

## The Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis

The idea that language shapes thought is most commonly associated with Edward Sapir and Benjamin Lee Whorf, though neither stated the hypothesis in the clean form found in textbooks. Sapir, writing in the 1920s and 1930s, argued that language is not merely a tool for expressing pre-existing thoughts but actively shapes the categories through which we perceive and understand the world. Whorf, his student, went further, arguing on the basis of his study of the Hopi language that the fundamental categories of Western thought — time, space, matter — are not universal features of human cognition but products of the particular languages we speak.

Modern discussions typically distinguish two versions of the hypothesis.

**The strong version (linguistic determinism):** Language determines thought. If your language lacks a concept, you cannot think it. Different languages create incommensurable worldviews. This version is dramatic, easy to state, and almost certainly wrong. It implies that translation between languages should be impossible and that speakers of different languages should be unable to understand each other's perspectives. Neither is the case.

**The weak version (linguistic relativity):** Language influences thought. It does not create impenetrable barriers but nudges cognition in particular directions, makes some distinctions more salient, and affects habitual patterns of attention and memory. This version is more modest, more plausible, and much harder to test rigorously.

The strong version has been largely abandoned. The weak version is the subject of active and productive research.

## Evidence for Linguistic Relativity

The strongest evidence for linguistic relativity comes from domains where languages carve up the world differently and where we can test whether speakers' non-linguistic cognition reflects those linguistic differences.

### Color

Color has been the most intensively studied domain. Languages differ dramatically in how they divide the color spectrum. Russian has separate basic terms for light blue ("goluboy") and dark blue ("siniy"), a distinction that English treats as a single category. The Dani of New Guinea were reported (in a famous but methodologically problematic study) to have only two color terms, corresponding roughly to "light/warm" and "dark/cool."

Does this matter for perception? A series of studies has found that Russian speakers are faster at discriminating light blue from dark blue (a cross-category distinction in Russian) than English speakers, for whom the same colors fall within a single category. Importantly, this advantage appears primarily in the right visual field (which projects to the left, language-dominant hemisphere), suggesting that the effect is mediated by linguistic processing. The effect is **robust** but small, and it can be disrupted by a concurrent verbal task (asking participants to remember a word while making color judgments), which is exactly what you would predict if language were contributing to the discrimination.

This is a far cry from linguistic determinism. Russian speakers and English speakers can both see the same colors. But the linguistic distinction appears to make the boundary between light and dark blue slightly more perceptually salient for Russian speakers. Language does not create perception, but it appears to modulate it.

### Spatial Language

Languages differ in how they describe spatial relationships. English uses relative spatial terms (left, right, front, back) anchored to the speaker's body. Some languages, like Guugu Yimithirr (an Australian Aboriginal language), use absolute spatial terms (north, south, east, west) even for small-scale spatial descriptions. A Guugu Yimithirr speaker might say "the cup is to the north of the plate" where an English speaker would say "the cup is to the left of the plate."

Stephen Levinson and colleagues have shown that speakers of absolute-frame languages maintain an awareness of cardinal directions that English speakers lack. When asked to reproduce spatial arrangements on a different table (rotated 180 degrees), speakers of relative-frame languages preserve the left-right arrangement, while speakers of absolute-frame languages preserve the cardinal-direction arrangement. These are clearly different cognitive strategies, apparently shaped by habitual language use. The evidence is **robust** for the existence of the difference, though the causal direction is harder to establish. Does language cause the spatial reasoning, or does the environment and culture shape both the language and the reasoning?

### Number Words

The Piraha case is most striking here. If the Piraha language truly lacks precise number words, do Piraha speakers have difficulty with exact numerical cognition? Studies by Peter Gordon and by Michael Frank and colleagues suggest the answer is yes — Piraha adults struggle with tasks requiring exact matching of quantities above three or four, while performing normally on approximate quantity judgments. This is consistent with the idea that precise number words are not just labels for pre-existing numerical concepts but cognitive tools that enable exact numerical reasoning.

However, the interpretation is complicated. The Piraha are a small, relatively isolated group. They may lack precise numerical cognition not because their language lacks number words, but because their way of life does not require exact counting. The language and the lifestyle co-evolved. Disentangling cause from correlation in cases like this is extraordinarily difficult, and the evidence is **contested**.

## Inner Speech: Is Language Necessary for Thought?

Step back from cross-linguistic comparisons and ask a more fundamental question: is language necessary for thought at all?

You probably experience something like inner speech, a voice in your head that narrates, deliberates, and rehearses. This inner speech feels essential to thinking. But is it?

Lev Vygotsky, the Soviet psychologist, argued that inner speech develops from social speech. Children first talk aloud to themselves while problem-solving (what Piaget dismissively called "egocentric speech"), and this self-directed speech gradually becomes internalized as inner speech, which then serves as a tool for self-regulation and complex thought.

If Vygotsky was right, then language is not just a medium for expressing thought; it is a cognitive tool that restructures thought. This has testable implications. People with damage to language areas (aphasia) should show impaired reasoning, at least on certain tasks. And indeed, some aphasic patients do show difficulties with complex reasoning, planning, and categorization, though others perform surprisingly well on non-verbal tasks. The picture is mixed.

A different kind of evidence comes from studies of deaf individuals who were not exposed to any language (signed or spoken) early in life. These "language-deprived" individuals, when studied as adults, show difficulties with certain cognitive tasks, particularly those involving false-belief reasoning and exact numerical cognition, that go beyond their language impairment. This suggests that language experience may be necessary for the normal development of certain cognitive capacities, though again, it is difficult to separate language deprivation from the broader social deprivation that typically accompanies it.

The honest summary is that language is not necessary for all thought. People clearly have non-verbal thoughts, mental imagery, spatial reasoning, and emotional experiences that do not depend on words. But language appears to be a powerful cognitive tool that augments, organizes, and sometimes transforms thought, particularly for abstract reasoning, exact quantity, and certain forms of categorization.

## Embodied Simulation: Thinking as Partial Re-Enactment

A different perspective on the relationship between language and thought comes from the embodied cognition tradition. On this view, understanding language is not a matter of manipulating abstract symbols but of mentally simulating the experiences that language describes.

When you read "She kicked the ball," embodied cognition researchers argue that you partially activate the motor representations involved in kicking. When you read "The sun was warm on her face," you partially activate the sensory representations involved in feeling warmth. Understanding language, on this view, is grounded in the body's perceptual and motor systems.

Evidence for this comes from studies showing that reading action words activates motor cortex in a somatotopic manner (leg words activate the leg region, hand words activate the hand region), and that manipulating the body can influence language comprehension (it is slightly harder to process "open the drawer" while pushing something away from you than while pulling it toward you).

This research is **contested**. The activation effects are real but small, and critics argue that they may be a byproduct of comprehension rather than a necessary component. You can understand "She kicked the ball" without literally simulating a kick. You might simply activate an abstract semantic representation. The debate is about whether embodied simulation is constitutive of understanding or merely associated with it.

What this line of research contributes to the language-thought question is a challenge to the idea that thought is purely abstract and language-like. If thinking involves partial perceptual and motor simulation, then thought is not neatly separable from the body and its experiences, and language meaning may be deeply connected to sensory-motor grounding rather than residing in a purely symbolic realm.

## Concepts, Categories, and the Language Connection

To think at all, we must categorize. The world presents us with an infinite stream of unique experiences, and cognition requires grouping them into kinds: this is a dog, that is a chair, this situation is dangerous. How do we form these categories, and what role does language play?

The classical view, associated with Aristotle, held that categories are defined by necessary and sufficient conditions. Something is a bird if and only if it has feathers, wings, a beak, and so on. But Eleanor Rosch's work in the 1970s demonstrated that natural categories do not work this way. Instead, they have a graded structure: some members are more typical than others (a robin is a more typical bird than a penguin), and category boundaries are fuzzy rather than sharp.

Language interacts with categorization in at least two ways. First, having a word for a category makes it more accessible and more likely to be used in reasoning. If your language has a single word for a concept, you are more likely to treat it as a coherent category and to notice instances of it. Second, the boundaries of linguistic categories can influence where you draw perceptual boundaries. The color perception studies described above are the clearest example: the linguistic boundary between "blue" and "green" makes the perceptual boundary slightly sharper.

But categories also exist without language. Pre-linguistic infants categorize objects, animals, and spatial relationships. Non-human animals form categories. The question is not whether language is necessary for categorization (it is not) but whether and how language modifies, sharpens, and extends our categorical capacities.

## Primary Text Spotlight: Boroditsky (2001)

Lera Boroditsky's 2001 paper "Does Language Shape Thought? Mandarin and English Speakers' Conceptions of Time" is one of the most cited studies in the linguistic relativity literature, and it illustrates both the promise and the challenges of this line of research.

### The Observation

English speakers predominantly use horizontal spatial metaphors for time: the future is "ahead," the past is "behind," deadlines are "moved forward" or "pushed back." Mandarin speakers also use horizontal metaphors but additionally use vertical metaphors: earlier events are "up" and later events are "down." This is a genuine linguistic difference. The question is whether it has cognitive consequences.

### The Design

Boroditsky tested English and Mandarin speakers on a task that required them to make temporal judgments after being primed with either horizontal or vertical spatial information. The key finding was that Mandarin speakers were faster to make temporal judgments after vertical spatial primes, while English speakers were faster after horizontal spatial primes. This suggested that the spatial metaphors habitually used in each language had shaped the non-linguistic representation of time.

### The Significance

If the result holds, it is a clean demonstration of linguistic relativity: speakers of different languages, faced with the same temporal judgments, show different patterns of facilitation from spatial primes, in a direction predicted by the spatial metaphors their languages favor. The thought pattern is not about language per se (the temporal judgments themselves are non-linguistic), but it is shaped by habitual linguistic experience.

### The Complications

This is where intellectual honesty requires us to slow down. Boroditsky's original finding has had a complicated replication history. Some studies have replicated the basic pattern; others have failed to find it or have found it only under specific conditions. January and Kako (2007) failed to replicate the key result. Chen (2007) argued that the vertical time metaphor in Mandarin is less prevalent than Boroditsky suggested. Later work by Boroditsky and colleagues refined the claims and provided additional evidence, but the debate is ongoing.

The current status is **contested**. The broader point, that spatial metaphors for time differ across languages and may have cognitive consequences, is plausible and has some support. But the specific experimental evidence is less secure than you would want for a strong conclusion. This is typical of the linguistic relativity literature: intriguing, suggestive, but often difficult to pin down with the rigor that strong claims require.

## What This Gets Right / What's Still Open

**What we understand well:** The strong form of linguistic determinism is not supported; language does not make certain thoughts impossible. Languages do differ in how they categorize the world (color, space, time, number). Some of these differences have measurable effects on non-linguistic cognition, particularly in the domains of color discrimination and spatial reasoning. Categories exist without language, but language sharpens and extends them.

**What remains open:** The magnitude and significance of linguistic relativity effects are debated. Whether language is a cognitive tool that restructures thought (the Vygotskyan view) or a medium that expresses pre-existing thought is not resolved. The role of embodied simulation in language comprehension is contested. The question of whether precise numerical cognition requires number words is still being investigated. And the fundamental question of how deeply language penetrates thought remains one of the great open questions in cognitive science.

A reasonable current position might be: language is neither a prison that determines thought nor a transparent window that merely expresses it. It is a powerful cognitive tool that shapes habitual patterns of attention, memory, and categorization without making any particular thought literally impossible.

## Real-World Application

### Why Translation Is Harder Than It Looks

If language merely encoded pre-existing thoughts, translation would be simple: decode the thought from Language A, encode it in Language B. But the linguistic relativity research explains why translation is difficult. Languages do not just use different words for the same concepts; they often package concepts differently, attend to different aspects of events, and leave different things implicit. Japanese has an elaborate system of honorifics that encode social relationships with every verb form. English tense encodes temporal information that some languages leave unspecified. Russian aspect encodes distinctions about the completeness of actions that English handles clumsily with adverbs.

A translator must not only convey the propositional content of a sentence but also navigate these structural differences, sometimes making explicit what the source language left implicit, sometimes choosing between distinctions that the target language forces but the source language did not. This is why machine translation systems, despite their impressive improvements, still struggle with nuance, register, and culturally embedded meaning. The problem is not just computational but conceptual; the mapping between languages is not one-to-one.

### Technical Terminology Design

At a more practical level, the language-thought connection has implications for how we design technical terminology. The words we use to describe problems shape how we think about them. When early computer scientists called the interruptible store "memory," they invited (and got) decades of thinking about computer memory by analogy to human memory — some of it productive, some of it misleading. When psychologists called the failure to notice an unexpected stimulus "inattentional blindness," the word "blindness" shaped intuitions about the phenomenon in ways that may be misleading (the information is processed to some degree — it is not blindness in the perceptual sense).

This is not mere pedantry. In software engineering, the choice between "technical debt" and "technical liability" subtly shifts how teams think about code quality. In medicine, the difference between calling something a "disease" and a "condition" affects patient behavior and treatment priorities. The Sapir-Whorf hypothesis, in its weak form, is a practical reminder that the terminology we choose for our professional concepts is not neutral. It shapes the intuitions of everyone who uses it.

This does not mean we should agonize over every word choice. It means we should be aware that words are not passive labels but active participants in our thinking. When you find yourself confused by a concept, it is sometimes worth asking whether the confusion lives in the thing itself or in the word someone chose for it.
