# Chapter 4: Perception: Construction, Not Recording

Try something. Read the next sentence, then look away and try to reconstruct it word for word.

*The cat sat on the mat and watched the birds through the window while the rain fell steadily outside.*

How did you do? If you are like most people, you got the gist but mangled the details. Maybe you swapped "steadily" for "softly," or forgot "through the window" entirely. And yet, while you were reading it, the sentence felt complete and clear. You did not experience any gaps. You did not notice yourself filling anything in.

This is the puzzle of perception in miniature. The world feels vivid, detailed, and continuous. But the evidence strongly suggests that what you experience is not a faithful recording of what is out there. It is a construction, an educated guess assembled from incomplete data. Understanding how and why your brain builds the world rather than simply recording it is one of the central achievements of cognitive science, and it has implications that reach from philosophy to courtroom testimony.

---

## Two Directions of Processing

Perception begins with energy hitting a receptor: photons striking the retina, pressure waves vibrating the eardrum. The signals generated by these receptors travel inward, toward higher-level brain areas that extract increasingly abstract features. This direction, from raw sensory data upward toward meaning, is called **bottom-up processing** (sometimes "data-driven" processing).

But information also flows the other way. Your expectations, your knowledge, your current goals: all of these shape what you perceive, often before you are aware of perceiving anything at all. This is **top-down processing** (sometimes "concept-driven" or "knowledge-driven" processing).

A classic demonstration: if you see the string "THE CAT," the middle letter of each word is physically identical, an ambiguous shape halfway between H and A. Yet you effortlessly read one as H and the other as A. Your knowledge of English words reaches down and resolves the ambiguity before you notice there was one. Bottom-up data provides the raw material; top-down processing shapes what you do with it.

The interaction between these two directions is not a minor detail. It is the engine of perception. Nearly every phenomenon we will discuss in this chapter reflects some interplay between what the world sends up and what the brain sends down.

## Visual Perception: From Features to Objects

### Feature Detection and the Work of Hubel and Wiesel

In the late 1950s and early 1960s, David Hubel and Torsten Wiesel performed a series of experiments that transformed our understanding of visual processing. Working with cats and later monkeys, they recorded from individual neurons in the primary visual cortex (area V1) while presenting simple visual stimuli.

What they found was striking. Certain neurons responded vigorously to a bar of light at a specific orientation, say a vertical line, but not at all to a horizontal one. Other neurons responded to edges, to specific widths, or to movement in a particular direction. Hubel and Wiesel called these **simple cells** and **complex cells**, and they proposed a hierarchy: simple cells detect oriented edges, complex cells combine those edges, and progressively higher areas combine those combinations into representations of increasingly complex features.

This was a profound insight. The brain does not process the visual scene all at once. It begins by detecting elementary features (edges, orientations, colors, motion) and builds up from there. This architecture is hierarchical, and it has been confirmed and refined by decades of subsequent work (and, notably, it inspired the convolutional neural networks that now power much of computer vision).

### Object Recognition and Two Visual Pathways

Detecting features is only the beginning. The real challenge is going from a pattern of edges and colors to the recognition that you are looking at, say, your mother's face or a coffee mug. This is the problem of **object recognition**, and it is computationally enormous. Objects can appear at different distances, angles, lighting conditions, and levels of occlusion, yet you recognize them almost instantly.

How the brain accomplishes this is still not fully understood, but we know a great deal about the architecture involved. In the early 1980s, Mortimer Mishkin and Leslie Ungerleider proposed that visual information leaving the primary visual cortex splits into two broad streams:

- The **ventral stream** (running from V1 toward the temporal lobe) processes object identity, what something is. This is sometimes called the "what" pathway.
- The **dorsal stream** (running from V1 toward the parietal lobe) processes spatial location and guides action, where something is and how to interact with it. This is the "where" (or "how") pathway.

This division is not absolute. The two streams interact extensively, and the "what vs. where" framing is a simplification. But the broad distinction has proven very useful, and we will see dramatic evidence for it when we discuss Patient DF later in this chapter.

### Perceptual Constancy

One of the most impressive feats of perception is something you never notice: **perceptual constancy**. A white piece of paper looks white whether you see it in bright sunlight or dim lamplight, even though the wavelengths reaching your eye differ dramatically. A door looks rectangular whether you see it straight on (when the retinal image is rectangular) or at an angle (when the retinal image is a trapezoid). A friend's face looks the same size whether they are three feet away or thirty, despite the retinal image shrinking by a factor of ten.

Your visual system is not reporting the raw data. It is interpreting that data in light of contextual information (the lighting, the viewing angle, the distance) and delivering a stable, useful representation. This is construction, not recording. And it usually works so well that you mistake the construction for reality.

## Predictive Processing and the Bayesian Brain

In the last two decades, a powerful theoretical framework has emerged that attempts to unify many of these observations. The core idea of **predictive processing** is that the brain is, at its core, a prediction machine. Rather than passively waiting for sensory data and then processing it, the brain constantly generates predictions about what sensory input it is about to receive, and then compares those predictions against what actually arrives.

When the prediction matches the input, little processing is needed; the brain has already "explained" the data. When there is a mismatch, the difference (called **prediction error**) propagates upward, forcing the brain to update its model. Perception, in this view, is not bottom-up construction from raw data but top-down hypothesis-testing, with bottom-up signals serving mainly as error correction.

This framework draws on **Bayesian inference**, the mathematical framework for updating beliefs in light of new evidence. Your brain, the argument goes, maintains probabilistic models of the world and updates them according to something like Bayes' rule. The technical term for how much the brain weights prediction errors versus prior expectations is **precision weighting**: in noisy or uncertain conditions, the brain relies more on its priors; in clear conditions, it lets the data speak.

Predictive processing is intellectually exciting. It offers a unified account of perception, attention, action, and even some aspects of consciousness. Karl Friston's free energy principle extends this into a grand theory of brain function. But we should be honest about its status: the framework is dominant in certain circles but contested in its strong form. Critics point out that the theory can be difficult to falsify, since almost any result can be reinterpreted as consistent with predictive processing. The empirical evidence supports the idea that prediction plays a role in perception, but whether the entire brain is best described as a prediction error minimization machine remains an open question. We model uncertainty here because the field itself is uncertain.

## Cross-Modal Perception

Perception is not neatly divided into separate channels. What you see affects what you hear, and vice versa. Two striking demonstrations make this vivid.

### The McGurk Effect

In 1976, Harry McGurk and John MacDonald discovered that if you hear the sound "ba" while watching a video of someone mouthing "ga," you will perceive a third sound, "da." This is not a subtle laboratory curiosity. It is robust, replicable, and it persists even when you know it is happening. Your brain is integrating auditory and visual information into a single percept, and when the inputs conflict, it constructs a compromise.

### The Rubber Hand Illusion

Sit at a table with one hand hidden behind a screen and a rubber hand placed in front of you, roughly where your real hand would be. Have someone stroke both your hidden real hand and the visible rubber hand simultaneously with a paintbrush. Within minutes, most people begin to feel as though the rubber hand is their own. Some people flinch when the rubber hand is threatened with a hammer.

This illusion reveals that your sense of body ownership is itself a construction, built from the integration of visual, tactile, and proprioceptive signals. When those signals are manipulated, your brain revises its model of where your body ends and the world begins.

## The Perception-Cognition Boundary

If top-down processing is so pervasive, does that mean your beliefs and desires literally change what you see? Can wanting something make it look closer? Can fear make a spider look bigger?

A number of studies in the early 2000s seemed to suggest yes. Researchers reported that hills look steeper when you are tired, distances look greater when you are carrying a heavy backpack, and desirable objects look physically closer. These findings fit neatly with a strongly constructive view of perception.

In 2016, Chaz Firestone and Brian Scholl published an influential critique titled "Cognition Does Not Affect Perception." They argued that many of these studies suffered from methodological problems — particularly **demand characteristics** (participants reporting what they thought the experimenters wanted) and **response bias** (participants shifting their verbal reports without any change in perception itself). Firestone and Scholl proposed that while top-down processing from one perceptual system to another is real (as in the McGurk effect), cognitive penetration in the strong sense, where beliefs, desires, or knowledge alter early perceptual processing, is much harder to demonstrate than it appears.

This debate is not resolved. Some researchers have pushed back with more carefully controlled studies. Others have accepted Firestone and Scholl's core argument while noting that the boundary between "perception" and "cognition" may itself be blurry. What matters for us is the principle: the claim that cognition shapes perception is empirically testable, and the bar for evidence should be high. We should not assume that because perception is constructive, it is therefore infinitely malleable.

## Primary Text Spotlight: Sperling (1960)

George Sperling's 1960 paper, "The Information Available in Brief Visual Presentations," is a masterclass in experimental design. The question was simple: how much visual information is available in a brief glance?

### The Problem

Earlier research had used **whole report**: flash an array of letters briefly, then ask the participant to report as many as they can. People typically reported about four or five items. But Sperling suspected this underestimated what was actually perceived. Maybe participants saw more than they could report, but the information faded before they could get it all out.

### The Method

Sperling introduced the **partial report** technique. He flashed a grid of letters (say, three rows of four) for just 50 milliseconds. Immediately after the flash, a tone sounded (high, medium, or low) indicating which row to report. The tone came after the display vanished, so participants could not know in advance which row they would need.

If participants had only encoded four or five letters total (as the whole report suggested), their performance on any given row should be poor. But that is not what happened. Participants could report almost all the letters in whichever row was cued, regardless of which row it was. This meant that immediately after the flash, information about all twelve letters was available, far more than the whole report had indicated.

### The Key Finding

However, when Sperling delayed the tone by even a fraction of a second, performance dropped sharply. By about one second of delay, partial report was no better than whole report.

This led Sperling to propose **iconic memory**, a brief, high-capacity visual store that holds a nearly complete image of the scene for a very short time (roughly 250-500 milliseconds). The information is there, but it decays rapidly, and only the portion that is attended to and transferred to a more durable store survives.

### Why This Matters

Sperling's experiment is clever because it reveals something about perception that introspection alone could never discover. You feel as though you see the whole scene, and in a sense you do. Iconic memory briefly holds a rich representation. But that richness is an illusion of availability. Most of it vanishes before it can be used. The feeling of completeness that opened this chapter? Iconic memory is part of the answer.

The partial report technique also illustrates a broader methodological point: the difference between what is perceived and what can be reported is not trivial. Clever experimental design can pry them apart.

## Case Study: Patient DF

In the early 1990s, Melvyn Goodale and David Milner described a patient known as DF who had suffered carbon monoxide poisoning that damaged her ventral visual stream while leaving her dorsal stream largely intact.

DF could not recognize objects by sight. She could not report whether a slot was oriented vertically or horizontally. She could not draw a simple shape from a visual model. By any standard test of visual object recognition, she was profoundly impaired, a condition called **visual form agnosia**.

But here is what was remarkable: if you asked DF to post a letter through a slot, she could do it. She would orient her hand correctly and slide the letter through, even though she could not verbally report the slot's orientation. Her dorsal stream (the "where/how" pathway) was guiding her actions accurately, even while her ventral stream (the "what" pathway) could not deliver conscious object recognition.

This dissociation is among the strongest evidence for the two-stream model. It shows that visual information can guide skilled action without ever producing conscious recognition. Perception for action and perception for identification are, to some meaningful degree, separable systems.

DF's case also raises a philosophical puzzle. Does she "see" the slot? In one sense, clearly yes: her visual system is processing its orientation and using that information to guide behavior. In another sense, clearly no: she has no conscious awareness of its orientation. What we call "seeing" may not be one thing.

## What This Gets Right / What's Still Open

The constructive view of perception is one of the most well-supported ideas in cognitive science. The evidence is overwhelming that you do not passively receive the world but actively build a representation of it. Feature detection hierarchies are real. The two visual pathways are supported by neuropsychological, neuroimaging, and behavioral evidence. Cross-modal integration is robust and replicable. Iconic memory is real and well-characterized.

What remains open:

- **Computational mechanisms.** We know perception is constructive, but the precise algorithms and neural implementations are still being worked out. Deep neural networks have provided useful models, but they differ from biological vision in important ways.
- **Predictive processing.** The framework is influential and has generated productive research, but its strong form, that all cortical processing is best described as prediction error minimization, is not yet established. The theory's flexibility is both its strength and its weakness.
- **Cognitive penetration.** Whether high-level beliefs and desires alter early perceptual processing, as opposed to merely biasing judgment and report, is actively debated. Firestone and Scholl raised the bar for evidence, and the field has not yet cleared it to everyone's satisfaction.
- **The binding problem.** How the brain combines separately processed features (color, shape, motion, location) into unified perceptual objects is still not fully understood, despite decades of work. Anne Treisman's feature integration theory was an important early account, but the complete answer remains elusive.

## Real-World Application

### Eyewitness Testimony

If perception is construction rather than recording, then memory for perceived events is also construction. This has direct consequences for the legal system.

Eyewitness testimony has historically been treated as powerful evidence. Jurors find it persuasive. But decades of research, pioneered by Elizabeth Loftus and many others, has shown that eyewitness memory is far less reliable than it feels. Witnesses confidently identify the wrong person. They incorporate post-event information (a leading question, a news report) into their memory of what they saw. They fill in gaps with plausible details and then forget that they filled them in.

The perceptual basis for this is clear from what we have discussed. If perception itself is a construction from incomplete data, then the memory of a perceived event is a construction of a construction. Each stage introduces opportunities for error, and those errors compound.

This is not a minor academic concern. The Innocence Project has documented hundreds of wrongful convictions in the United States, and eyewitness misidentification is the leading contributing factor, present in roughly 69% of cases later overturned by DNA evidence. We will examine eyewitness testimony, false memory, and the legal reforms it has motivated in much greater detail in Chapter 11.

### Warning Label Design

The constructive nature of perception also matters for design. Warning labels on products, medications, and hazardous materials must be perceived accurately to be effective. But perception is not passive reception, so a warning that is physically present on a package may not be functionally present in the user's experience.

Several factors matter: perceptual salience (contrast, size, color), location relative to where the user is already looking, and whether the warning conflicts with the user's expectations. A warning on the back of a package that a user never turns over is not a warning in any meaningful perceptual sense. A warning in small gray text on a white background fails at the feature detection level. A warning that looks like every other warning the user has seen a thousand times may fail because the brain, in good predictive-processing fashion, has learned to expect it and therefore suppresses the prediction error it would otherwise generate.

Good design works with the constructive nature of perception rather than against it. It does not assume that information presented is information perceived.
