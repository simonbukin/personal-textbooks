# Chapter 9: Working Memory: The Thinking Space

Try this. Read the following phone number once, then look away: 867-5309. Now count backwards from 100 by 3s (100, 97, 94, 91, and so on) for about thirty seconds. Really do it. Then try to recall the number.

If you are like most people, the number is gone, or at least badly degraded. You might remember the first few digits, or the last few, but the whole string has slipped away. This is strange if you think about it. Thirty seconds ago you had the number clearly in mind. Nobody erased it. You did not suffer a head injury. You simply thought about something else for half a minute, and the information evaporated.

This little demonstration reveals something fundamental about human cognition: the mental workspace where we hold and manipulate information is startlingly small and alarmingly fragile. That workspace is what cognitive scientists call working memory, and understanding its nature, its limits, its architecture, its relationship to intelligence and learning, is one of the most practically consequential topics in all of cognitive science.

---

## From Short-Term Memory to Working Memory

For most of the twentieth century, researchers talked about short-term memory, a temporary buffer that holds information for a few seconds before it either gets transferred to long-term memory or disappears. The idea was simple: information comes in, sits in a holding area, and either sticks or does not.

This picture was not wrong, exactly, but it was too passive. When you do mental arithmetic, you are not merely holding numbers in mind. You are actively manipulating them, carrying digits, keeping track of intermediate results. When you read a complex sentence, you are holding the beginning of the sentence in mind while processing the end, integrating meaning across clauses in real time. When you plan your route through a grocery store, you are juggling a mental map, a shopping list, and constraints about what is available where.

The term working memory emerged to capture this active quality. Short-term memory is a warehouse. Working memory is a workshop, a place where information is stored and actively processed, combined, and transformed. The shift in terminology reflects a real shift in how we think about what this system does.

This distinction matters because it reframes the question. The interesting problem is not "how much can you hold?" but "how much can you hold while doing something with it?" Those turn out to be rather different questions with rather different answers.

## Baddeley's Model: A Useful Map of Uncertain Territory

The most influential model of working memory was proposed by Alan Baddeley and Graham Hitch in 1974, and Baddeley continued refining it for decades. The model carves working memory into distinct components, each handling a different kind of information.

### The Phonological Loop

This is the component that handles verbal and acoustic information, the voice in your head. When you repeat a phone number to yourself, you are running it through the phonological loop. It has two parts: a phonological store that holds speech-based information for a couple of seconds, and an articulatory rehearsal process (essentially subvocal speech) that refreshes the information before it decays.

The phonological loop explains several well-established phenomena. The word-length effect: you can remember more short words than long words in a list, because longer words take longer to rehearse. The phonological similarity effect: lists of similar-sounding words (man, mat, map, cap) are harder to remember than dissimilar ones, because they interfere with each other in the phonological store. The irrelevant speech effect: background speech disrupts your ability to hold verbal information, even when you are not listening to it, because it has obligatory access to the phonological store.

### The Visuospatial Sketchpad

This component handles visual and spatial information: your mental image of a room's layout, the trajectory of a ball, the shape of a letter. When you mentally rotate an object to see if it fits through a doorway, or when you visualize a route from your house to the store, you are engaging the visuospatial sketchpad.

There is evidence that visual and spatial processing are at least partially separable; you can disrupt spatial working memory by tapping a spatial pattern while leaving visual imagery relatively intact, and vice versa. But for our purposes, the key point is that this system handles a different kind of information than the phonological loop, and it has its own capacity limits.

### The Central Executive

Here is where the model gets both most interesting and most problematic. The central executive is supposed to be the control system, the component that directs attention, coordinates information from the two slave systems (Baddeley's term), switches between tasks, and manages the overall flow of processing.

The trouble is that the central executive does most of the heavy lifting in the model while being the least well specified. It risks becoming what philosophers call a homunculus, a little person inside the head who does all the hard work, which just pushes the question back a level. What controls the central executive? Baddeley was aware of this problem and spent years trying to fractionate the central executive into more specific processes, with partial success.

### The Episodic Buffer

Added to the model in 2000, the episodic buffer was Baddeley's attempt to solve a binding problem: how does the system integrate information from different sources (verbal information, visual information, information from long-term memory) into coherent episodes? The episodic buffer is a limited-capacity store that holds multimodal representations, serving as an interface between working memory and long-term memory.

### The Model's Status Today

Baddeley's model is usefully descriptive. It gives us a vocabulary for talking about different aspects of working memory, and the phonological loop in particular is well supported by behavioral and neuroimaging evidence. But the model is increasingly questioned as a complete account. Critics argue that it is more a taxonomy of phenomena than a mechanistic explanation. The components may not be as neatly separable as the box-and-arrow diagrams suggest. And alternative models, particularly those that treat working memory as activated long-term memory rather than a separate system, have gained traction.

We will use Baddeley's terminology because it is standard and useful, but we should hold it lightly. The map is not the territory, and this particular map may be due for revision.

## How Much Can You Hold? The Capacity Question

In 1956, George Miller published one of the most famous papers in cognitive psychology: "The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two." Miller argued that the capacity of short-term memory was about seven items: seven digits, seven words, seven chunks of information.

Miller's number became one of those facts that everybody knows. Phone numbers are seven digits. Menu items are grouped in sevens. Presentation guidelines recommend no more than seven bullet points.

The problem is that Miller's estimate is almost certainly too high.

Nelson Cowan, in a series of careful studies beginning in the early 2000s, argued that the true capacity of working memory is closer to four items — or more exactly, about three to five chunks, with four as a reasonable central estimate. The difference comes from controlling for strategies that inflate apparent capacity. When people remember seven digits, they are often grouping them, rehearsing them, or drawing on long-term memory to encode them more efficiently. Strip away those strategies, and the raw capacity is very small.

Four items. That is the size of your mental workspace when you are doing something new with unfamiliar material.

This should be humbling. The system that you use for all of your conscious thinking, planning, reasoning, and problem-solving can hold roughly four things at once. If that seems impossibly small, remember that a chunk can be complex. An experienced chess player can hold a meaningful board configuration as a single chunk. A fluent reader processes word meanings in chunks that would take many individual letters to spell out. But the number of chunks is still painfully limited.

### Why Are the Limits So Tight?

This is an interesting question that does not have a settled answer. One possibility is that it is an engineering constraint; building a system with more capacity would require more neural resources than evolution could afford. But this explanation is a bit unsatisfying, because the brain is enormous and metabolically expensive; surely it could spare a few more neurons for working memory.

A more intriguing possibility is that the limits are a feature, not a bug. Working memory's tight capacity may serve as a filter that forces the system to focus on what matters. If you could hold everything in mind simultaneously, you would face a combinatorial explosion: too many possible relationships between items, too many potential inferences, too many things competing for processing. The bottleneck forces selectivity, which may actually be essential for coherent thought.

There is also an interference-based account: the more items you hold, the more they interfere with each other, degrading the quality of all representations. On this view, capacity is limited not by the number of slots but by the ability to maintain distinct representations in the face of mutual interference. Four items may be roughly where the signal-to-noise ratio drops below a useful threshold.

These accounts are not mutually exclusive, and the truth probably involves some combination. But the key insight is that working memory limitations are not an embarrassing design flaw. They are a basic feature of cognitive architecture that shapes everything we do.

## Working Memory and Intelligence

Here is a finding that has held up well: working memory capacity is one of the strongest predictors of general intelligence. People who score higher on working memory tasks tend to score higher on IQ tests, reasoning tasks, reading comprehension, and a wide range of other cognitive measures. The correlation is strong enough that some researchers have argued working memory capacity is essentially what intelligence tests measure.

This makes intuitive sense. If reasoning requires holding multiple pieces of information in mind and manipulating them (comparing options, tracking logical chains, integrating evidence), then the size and efficiency of your mental workspace should predict how well you reason.

But we should be careful about causal claims. The correlation between working memory and intelligence could mean that working memory capacity causes better reasoning. It could also mean that both are downstream effects of some other factor, like the efficiency of attentional control or the speed of neural processing. And it could mean that intelligence tests are inadvertently biased toward tasks that load heavily on working memory, which would make the correlation partly an artifact of measurement.

What is clear is that individual differences in working memory capacity are real, substantial, and consequential. They predict academic performance, job performance in complex occupations, and the ability to learn new skills. Whether we can meaningfully increase working memory capacity through training is another question, one where the evidence is, to put it gently, disappointing.

### Working Memory Training: The Hope and the Evidence

In the late 2000s, a study by Jaeggi and colleagues made headlines with the claim that training on a demanding working memory task (the "dual n-back" task) could increase fluid intelligence, the ability to reason about novel problems. If true, this would be a major breakthrough: a simple, scalable intervention that makes people smarter.

The initial excitement has not survived well. Subsequent meta-analyses, including a large review by Melby-Lervag and colleagues, found that working memory training reliably improves performance on the trained task and closely related tasks, but shows little to no transfer to broader cognitive abilities like reasoning, reading comprehension, or academic performance. You get better at the game without getting meaningfully smarter.

This pattern, near transfer without far transfer, is common in cognitive training research, and it has a simple explanation. What you are training is probably not working memory capacity itself but rather specific strategies for the specific task. Those strategies do not generalize because the tasks that would benefit from improved working memory involve different materials, different processing demands, and different strategies.

This does not mean working memory is untrainable in principle. It means we have not found a way to do it yet, and the most obvious approach, practicing working memory tasks, does not appear to work. The search continues, but expectations should be calibrated accordingly.

## Primary Text Spotlight: Baddeley & Hitch (1974)

The paper that launched the modern study of working memory was not a single dramatic experiment but a series of dual-task studies that Baddeley and Hitch reported in a book chapter, not even a journal article. The chapter, titled simply "Working memory," appeared in a volume edited by Gordon Bower called *The Psychology of Learning and Motivation*.

The logic was clean. If short-term memory is a single unitary store, then filling it up with one task should leave no capacity for a second task. Baddeley and Hitch had participants hold sequences of digits in mind (loading short-term memory) while simultaneously performing reasoning tasks, comprehension tasks, or free recall.

The critical finding: loading short-term memory did slow down the secondary tasks, but it did not destroy them. Even when participants were holding six digits in memory, near the supposed capacity limit, they could still reason, comprehend, and recall, albeit more slowly. This meant that reasoning and comprehension were not simply competing for space in the same short-term store. Something more complex was going on.

From this, Baddeley and Hitch argued that the simple box labeled "short-term memory" in the modal model needed to be replaced with a multi-component system, one where different subsystems could operate somewhat independently. The phonological loop could hold the digit string while the central executive managed the reasoning task, with some cost but not total collapse.

What makes this paper a landmark is the shift in framing it accomplished, more than any single finding. Before Baddeley and Hitch, short-term memory was a waiting room. After them, it was a workshop. That metaphor shift opened up decades of productive research on how we actively process information in real time.

The paper also illustrates an important methodological point: some of the most consequential advances in cognitive science come not from discovering new phenomena but from designing better ways to probe familiar ones. The dual-task paradigm was not new. What was new was using it systematically to test the architecture of temporary memory.

## Case Study: The Doorway Effect

You get up from your desk to fetch something from the kitchen. You walk through the doorway, arrive in the kitchen, and... nothing. You have completely forgotten why you came. You stand there for a moment, retrace your steps mentally, maybe physically walk back to your desk, and the intention floods back.

This is the doorway effect, and it has been studied experimentally. Gabriel Radvansky and colleagues at the University of Notre Dame conducted a series of studies in the early 2010s showing that walking through a doorway impairs memory for recently processed information, compared to walking the same distance within a single room. The effect has been replicated in both real environments and virtual ones.

The proposed mechanism is that doorways serve as event boundaries. Your brain uses environmental features to segment ongoing experience into discrete events, and crossing a boundary triggers an updating process that clears out some of the contents of working memory to make room for whatever is relevant in the new context. This is adaptive — when you walk into a new room, the new environment is probably more relevant than what you were thinking about in the old one. But it has the side effect of flushing information that you were actively trying to maintain.

A few caveats. The effect is real but small. It does not happen every time you walk through a doorway. And the exact mechanism is still debated; it may have more to do with context change than with doorways specifically. But it nicely illustrates how working memory is not an isolated system; it is embedded in the flow of action and environmental context. Its contents are shaped by factors you might never have considered.

## What This Gets Right / What's Still Open

**What the science gets right:** Working memory is a real and measurable system with clear capacity limits. The distinction between passive storage and active maintenance is important and well supported. Individual differences in working memory capacity predict meaningful outcomes. The basic phenomena (word-length effects, phonological similarity effects, dual-task costs) are robust.

**What's still open:** The architecture of working memory is contested. Baddeley's model is useful but may not be the right way to carve the system. The relationship between working memory and long-term memory is murky — some models treat them as continuous rather than distinct. Whether working memory capacity can be meaningfully increased through training remains unclear; the bulk of evidence suggests that training on working memory tasks improves performance on those specific tasks without much transfer to other cognitive abilities, but the debate continues. The neural basis of working memory, once thought to be sustained firing of prefrontal neurons, is being revised in light of evidence for activity-silent maintenance through synaptic weights.

## Real-World Application: Cognitive Load and Learning

If working memory can hold roughly four items, and all conscious learning passes through working memory, then the design of instruction is constrained by working memory capacity. This is the core insight of cognitive load theory, developed by John Sweller beginning in the 1980s.

Sweller distinguishes three types of cognitive load. Intrinsic load is the complexity inherent in the material itself; some things are just harder than others because they involve more interacting elements. Extraneous load is the unnecessary cognitive burden imposed by poor instructional design: busy slides, confusing layouts, irrelevant decorations, instructions that force the learner to split attention between separated sources of information. Germane load is the productive cognitive effort of actually building schemas and understanding.

The practical implications are direct. Every unnecessary element on a slide consumes working memory capacity that could be used for learning. Every time you make students split their attention between a diagram and a separate text explanation, you are imposing extraneous load. Every time you present information in a way that requires students to mentally integrate multiple sources, you are taxing working memory.

This explains several things that experienced teachers know intuitively. Novices need worked examples because they lack the schemas that would let them compress information into manageable chunks. Bullet-heavy slides are often worse than a simple image with narration, because reading and listening simultaneously overload the phonological loop. Note-taking helps partly because it forces you to compress and rephrase, which is a form of chunking, but it hurts when the lecture pace is too fast, because the transcription effort consumes the same working memory resources needed for comprehension.

The honest caveat: cognitive load theory is widely applied but not always easy to test, because "cognitive load" is difficult to measure directly. The theory sometimes risks being unfalsifiable, since any failure to learn can be attributed to excessive load after the fact. But its core prediction, that instructional design should respect working memory limits, is well supported and practically useful.
